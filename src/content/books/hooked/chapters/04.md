# Variable Reward: The Itch You Can't Scratch

## Key Ideas
- Craving runs on **anticipation**, not satisfaction: the brain's reward system fires in expectation of a reward, and **variability** is what keeps it firing.
- Olds and Milner's 1954 rats and Knutson's fMRI work both point to the same conclusion — wanting, driven by unpredictability, is distinct from liking.
- Three reward classes: **rewards of the tribe** (social), **rewards of the hunt** (resources and information), and **rewards of the self** (mastery and completion).
- **Finite variability** eventually becomes predictable and loses its grip; **infinite variability** keeps regenerating and sustains engagement.
- Rewards must preserve the user's sense of **autonomy** — heavy-handedness provokes **reactance** — and must scratch the user's real itch, not the company's.
- Variable rewards are not a cure-all; they only work when aligned with the user's internal trigger.

## The Brain Craves the Maybe, Not the Reward
The engine of the third phase is a counterintuitive fact about the brain: what drives us to act again and again is not the pleasure of the reward but the anticipation of it, and anticipation is heightened by **variability** — not knowing exactly what we'll get. Eyal builds this on two landmark pieces of research.

In 1954, James Olds and Peter Milner implanted electrodes in the brains of rats, wired to a lever the rats could press to stimulate a region we associate with pleasure. The rats pressed compulsively — reportedly up to thousands of times an hour — forgoing food and rest, some to the point of collapse. It looked like a pleasure center. But later interpretation reframed it: the region was less about the pleasure of getting and more about the *wanting*, the craving that propels the pursuit.

Stanford neuroscientist Brian Knutson sharpened the point. Using fMRI to watch blood flow in the brains of people gambling, he found that the nucleus accumbens — long thought to activate on receiving a reward — actually spiked in **anticipation** of the reward, not when the payoff arrived. Craving lives in the expectation. And expectation is most intense when the outcome is uncertain. Predictable rewards dull; the maybe is what keeps us pulling the lever. This is why variability is the active ingredient, and why a feed you can't predict is harder to close than one you can.

## Three Kinds of Reward the Product Can Offer
Eyal groups all variable rewards into three categories, each tapping a different human drive. A product can use one or several, but the reward must match what the user actually wants.

**Rewards of the tribe** are social rewards — the search for acceptance, attractiveness, importance, inclusion, and connection with other people. We are wired to seek and be rewarded by our tribe, and the variability of social feedback makes it potent. Facebook and Instagram "likes," Stack Overflow's points and badges, and the shifting social standing inside a multiplayer game all deliver rewards of the tribe: you never know exactly how others will respond, and the uncertainty keeps you checking.

**Rewards of the hunt** are the pursuit of material resources or information. This drive is ancient — chasing food, chasing money — and it maps directly onto modern products. The slot machine is the archetype: pull, and maybe win. The scrolling feed is a hunt for the next interesting morsel; you thumb onward not knowing whether the next item will be worth it, and the intermittent payoff keeps you scrolling. Any product built around an unpredictable stream of content or resources is offering rewards of the hunt.

**Rewards of the self** are intrinsic — the search for mastery, competence, completion, and control, pursued for their own sake rather than for others' approval or material gain. Video games deliver this through escalating challenge and the satisfaction of leveling up. The pull to reach "inbox zero," to finish a course module, to complete a set — these are rewards of the self, where the variability lives in the challenge and the sense of progress.

## Finite Variability Fades; Infinite Variability Endures
A crucial distinction determines whether a variable reward keeps working over the long run. **Finite variability** produces experiences that become predictable once you've exhausted them. Eyal's example is a scripted TV series or a film with a set ending: thrilling the first time, but once you know the outcome the mystery is gone and the desire to return drops. A product built on finite variability can hook users initially and then watch engagement decay as the novelty runs out.

**Infinite variability**, by contrast, is content that keeps changing and regenerating, so the source of novelty never depletes. Eyal contrasts the finished story with a social feed or a multiplayer game: because other people keep generating new content and new situations, the well never runs dry. User-generated content, live interaction, and open-ended play all supply infinite variability, which is why they sustain habits far longer than any fixed, finite experience can. When you're designing for retention, the question is whether your variability has a floor — and if it does, what refills it.

## Autonomy, Reactance, and Scratching the Real Itch
Variable rewards are powerful, but Eyal is careful to bound them with two warnings. The first is about **autonomy**. People resist losing their sense of choice; when a prompt feels coercive, it triggers **reactance** — a pushback that makes them do the opposite of what's demanded. Preserving the user's feeling of agency keeps them engaged; stripping it away breaks the loop. Research on compliance shows that simply affirming a person's freedom to choose — reminding them "but you are free" to decline — measurably increases cooperation, because it restores the sense of autonomy that heavy-handed pressure removes.

The second warning is that variable rewards are not a magic ingredient you can bolt onto anything. The reward must **scratch the user's actual itch** — it has to satisfy the internal trigger that brought the user in, not merely serve the company's metrics. Eyal's cautionary contrast is Mahalo versus Quora: Mahalo tried to drive question-answering with cash payouts, a reward that didn't match users' real motivation and faded; Quora succeeded by using rewards of the tribe — recognition and social standing — which aligned with why people actually wanted to answer. Same behavior, different reward, opposite outcomes.

The lesson is that a variable reward works only when it fits the user's genuine desire. Reward the wrong itch and no amount of unpredictability will build a lasting habit. Get the fit right, keep the variability alive, and protect the user's autonomy, and the third phase becomes the part of the loop that users can't quite put down.

## In Practice
- Identify which of the three reward types — tribe, hunt, or self — matches your users' internal trigger, and build the reward around that drive specifically.
- Engineer genuine variability into the reward; a payoff users can fully predict stops pulling them back.
- Check whether your variability is finite or infinite, and if finite, design a source (other users, fresh content) that keeps refilling the novelty.
- Preserve autonomy in every prompt; affirm the user's freedom to decline rather than coercing, to avoid triggering reactance.
- Make sure the reward serves the user's real desire, not just your engagement dashboard — the wrong reward, however variable, won't stick.
- As a user, when a feed or game keeps you longer than you meant, name the reward type pulling you (social approval, the next hit, one more level) and decide if the itch it scratches is one you actually have.
