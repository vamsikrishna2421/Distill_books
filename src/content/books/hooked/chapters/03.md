# Action: Making the Behavior Easy

## Key Ideas
- **B = MAT**: a behavior happens only when **motivation**, **ability**, and a **trigger** converge at the same moment; miss any one and the action fails.
- Raising **ability** (making the action simpler) is usually faster and cheaper than raising motivation.
- Simplicity is set by the scarcest of six resources: **time, money, physical effort, brain cycles, social deviance, and non-routineness**.
- Fogg's three core motivator pairs: **seek pleasure / avoid pain**, **seek hope / avoid fear**, **seek social acceptance / avoid rejection**.
- Cognitive **heuristics** lift action without new features: **scarcity**, **framing**, **anchoring**, and the **endowed progress effect**.
- The design instinct should be to remove friction from the target behavior before trying to pump up desire.

## Three Things Must Line Up at Once
The **Action** phase is the simplest behavior a person takes in anticipation of a reward, and Eyal grounds it in Stanford researcher BJ Fogg's model, compressed into the formula **B = MAT**: Behavior equals Motivation times Ability times a Trigger. The word "times" matters — this is a convergence, not a sum. For any action to occur, the person must have sufficient **motivation** to do it, sufficient **ability** to do it easily, and a **trigger** present to prompt it, all in the same instant. If motivation is high and ability is high but no trigger fires, nothing happens. If the trigger fires and motivation is high but the action is too hard, nothing happens.

This gives designers a diagnostic checklist. When users aren't taking the action you want, one of the three legs is missing, and your job is to find which. Often teams reflexively assume the problem is motivation — users just don't want it enough — and pour effort into persuasion, incentives, and messaging. Eyal argues that's usually the wrong leg to grab.

## Simplicity Beats Persuasion
Between motivation and ability, Eyal is emphatic: increasing **ability** — making the behavior easier — is the higher-leverage move. Boosting motivation is slow, expensive, and unreliable; you're trying to change how someone feels. Boosting ability is often just design work: strip steps, remove decisions, cut the effort until doing the thing is nearly frictionless. The products that win the action phase tend to be the ones that ask for the least.

Ability, in Fogg's framing, is simplicity, and simplicity is a function of the user's scarcest resource in the moment. Eyal lists six:

- **Time** — how long the action takes.
- **Money** — the financial cost.
- **Physical effort** — the labor involved.
- **Brain cycles** — the mental effort and thought required.
- **Social deviance** — how accepted or unaccepted the behavior is by others.
- **Non-routine** — how much the action breaks from the user's existing routines.

The key insight is that simplicity is bounded by whichever of these the user has least of right now. A busy executive is short on time; a student is short on money; a first-time user is short on the routine that would make an action feel natural. To increase ability you find the binding constraint and relax it. Facebook's early one-click login attacked brain cycles and time; Google's single, empty search box asks for almost nothing on any of the six axes. Every removed field, every eliminated decision, every second saved raises ability and makes the action more likely to fire when the trigger comes.

Motivation still has a role, and Fogg names three core motivator pairs that pull behavior: people act to **seek pleasure and avoid pain**, to **seek hope and avoid fear**, and to **seek social acceptance and avoid rejection**. These are useful for shaping the emotional framing of an action. But Eyal's ordering stands: when action is missing, reach for simplicity first and motivation second.

## Heuristics That Nudge Action Without New Features
Beyond the raw mechanics of MAT, Eyal catalogs cognitive **heuristics** — quirks in human judgment that designers can use to increase the perceived likelihood or value of an action without building anything new. Four stand out.

The **scarcity effect**: we assign more value to things that appear scarce. Eyal cites a 1975 experiment by Worchel and colleagues in which people rated cookies in a jar. Cookies from a jar holding only two were rated more desirable than identical cookies from a jar of ten — and, strikingly, cookies were valued even more highly when the researchers reduced the count from ten to two in front of participants than when there had only ever been two. Perceived scarcity, and the direction of change toward it, shifts value. Products signal scarcity with limited quantities, countdowns, and "only a few left" cues.

**Framing**: the mind's perception of an experience is shaped by how it's presented, sometimes overriding the objective facts. Eyal points to wine studies in which the identical wine is rated as tasting better when subjects are told it is expensive. The frame — the price tag, the packaging, the label — alters the actual experienced quality, not just the reported opinion. How you present an action changes how it feels to take it.

**Anchoring**: people latch onto one piece of information — often the first number they see — when making a decision, and judge everything else relative to it. A high "original" price makes a sale price feel like a deal; an anchor set early biases every later comparison.

The **endowed progress effect**: people are more motivated to finish a goal the more progress they believe they've already made. In the classic car-wash loyalty study, customers given a card needing eight stamps but pre-stamped with two "free" ones completed the card far more often than customers given a card needing only six stamps from zero — even though both required six purchases. The illusion of a head start pulls people toward completion. Products manufacture this feeling with profile-completion bars, onboarding checklists already partly filled, and progress meters that start above empty.

None of these heuristics manufactures motivation from nothing; they tilt perception at the margin so the action clears the bar. Used well, they're the difference between a user who almost acts and one who does.

## In Practice
- When an action isn't happening, diagnose which leg of B = MAT is missing before you spend a dollar — usually it's ability, not motivation.
- List the six simplicity factors for your key action and identify the one your target user is scarcest on, then redesign to relax that specific constraint.
- Cut fields, steps, and decisions from your core flow relentlessly; treat every removed tap as a real gain in the odds the behavior fires.
- Use scarcity, framing, anchoring, and endowed progress honestly to ease genuine actions, not to trick users into ones they'd regret.
- As a user, notice when "only a few left," a countdown, or a near-complete progress bar is pushing you — the pressure is engineered, and naming it defuses it.
- Add friction on purpose to habits you want to break: log out, delete the app, bury the icon — you're lowering your own ability to act on the trigger.
