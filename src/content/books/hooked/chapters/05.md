# Investment: The Work That Keeps Users

## Key Ideas
- The **investment** phase asks users to put a bit of effort *in*, and that small deposit is what pulls them back for the next loop.
- Investment comes **after** the reward, unlike the action phase, which comes before it — the timing is deliberate.
- Investments create **stored value** in five forms: **content, data, followers, reputation, and skill**, each making the product better with use.
- The **IKEA effect** and **effort justification** mean we over-value what we've worked to build, and our desire for **consistency** keeps us returning to it.
- Investments **load the next trigger**, setting up a future external prompt so the loop restarts on its own.
- Stacked investments raise **switching costs** and produce **escalating commitment** — a retention engine that compounds.

## A Little Work, at the Right Moment
The fourth phase of the Hook Model is the **investment**, and it is the one most often skipped by product teams, which is exactly why Eyal spends a chapter on it. An investment is a bit of work the user puts into the product — and the reason it matters is that people who invest in a service are far more likely to return to it. The behavior is not about extracting value from the user in the moment; it's about depositing something that makes the next loop more likely and the product more valuable.

Timing is everything, and Eyal draws a sharp line between action and investment. The **action** phase asks for the simplest possible behavior and comes *before* the reward — it's the price of entry, kept as frictionless as possible. The **investment** comes *after* the reward has been delivered, when the user is satisfied and primed to give a little back. Asking for effort at this moment — when the user has just been rewarded and feels good about the product — is far more effective than asking up front. You reward first, then invest, because a satisfied user is a generous one.

The investment is also different in kind: it's an appeal to a longer-term payoff rather than an immediate one. The user does a little work now — curating, rating, following, customizing — in anticipation of a better experience later. That forward-looking quality is what distinguishes it from the quick, reward-seeking action.

## Investments Store Value That Compounds
What makes investment uniquely powerful is that, unlike physical goods that depreciate, the value a user puts into a habit-forming product tends to make the product *better* over time. Eyal identifies five forms of **stored value** that users deposit through their investments.

**Content**: the songs a user adds to a playlist, the photos uploaded, the documents created. The more content a user pours in, the more valuable and personalized the service becomes, and the more they'd lose by leaving.

**Data**: the information a user accumulates — activity history, preferences, records. A service that has years of your data is one that fits you better than any newcomer could, because the newcomer starts from zero.

**Followers**: the social connections built up — the people who follow you and whom you follow. A network you've spent years cultivating is not portable; it lives inside the product, and rebuilding it elsewhere is daunting.

**Reputation**: the standing a user earns — seller ratings, review scores, karma, badges. Reputation is stored value that took real effort to accumulate and would vanish on switching, which is why marketplaces with rating systems are so sticky for their most active participants.

**Skill**: the proficiency a user develops in operating the product. Learning the shortcuts and workflows of a tool is an investment; once mastered, moving to a competitor means starting the learning curve over.

Each of these makes the service more tailored and more capable the more it's used — a product that improves with investment, rather than wearing out.

## Why We Over-Value What We Build
Behind the mechanics sits a set of cognitive tendencies that make investment disproportionately powerful. The first is the **IKEA effect**, named for the flat-pack furniture that customers assemble themselves. Eyal draws on Dan Ariely's research showing that people place higher value on things they've partially created with their own labor — whether furniture or folded origami — than on identical items made by someone else. Labor breeds love. When users put work into a product, they value it more than its objective worth, simply because they built part of it.

Closely related is **effort justification** and our drive for **consistency**. Having invested effort, we tell ourselves the effort was worthwhile, and we act in ways consistent with our past behavior to avoid the discomfort of contradiction. Psychologists call the discomfort of holding conflicting beliefs **cognitive dissonance**; one way we resolve it is by adjusting our attitudes to match what we've already done. If I've spent hours building a profile, curating a library, or growing a following, quitting would imply that effort was wasted — so I keep going, and I like the product more to justify staying.

These biases mean the investment phase doesn't just add functional value; it reshapes how the user feels about the product, binding them to it psychologically as well as practically.

## Loading the Next Trigger and Escalating Commitment
The most ingenious property of the investment phase is that a well-designed investment **loads the next trigger** — it plants the seed of a future external cue that will restart the loop. This is the structural difference between a habit-forming product and one that must keep buying attention: the user's own investment generates the next prompt.

The examples are precise. When you **follow** someone, you're investing effort now to load future triggers — their posts will appear in your feed later, pulling you back. When you **invite** a friend, you set up a future notification when they join or respond. When you tag a photo, curate a board, or set a preference, you're configuring the product to send you a relevant prompt down the line. The investment today manufactures the trigger tomorrow, and the loop becomes self-sustaining rather than externally fueled.

Layer these loops and you get **escalating commitment**. Each pass through the Hook Model deposits a little more stored value and asks for a little more investment, so the product becomes progressively more valuable and progressively harder to abandon. **Switching costs** climb: leaving now means forfeiting years of content, data, followers, reputation, and skill. Eyal's point is that retention built this way compounds. A product that only rewards is fighting to keep users every single day; a product that also collects investment lets its users tie themselves in, loop by loop, until leaving feels like a loss. That, more than any single feature, is what turns a habit into a moat.

## In Practice
- Add an investment step that comes after the reward, not before — ask for effort when the user is satisfied and primed to give a little back.
- Design investments that deposit stored value (content, data, followers, reputation, or skill) so the product visibly improves the more it's used.
- Make sure each investment loads a future trigger — following, inviting, tagging, or customizing should set up the next prompt automatically.
- Track escalating commitment and switching costs as retention signals; compounding investment, not daily rewards, is what makes leaving feel costly.
- Respect the IKEA effect ethically: let users build real value they'd genuinely miss, not sunk-cost traps engineered purely to hold them.
- As a user, notice when you keep using something mainly because you've poured years into it; ask whether the stored value still serves you or just holds you.
