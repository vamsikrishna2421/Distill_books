# The Morality of Manipulation

## Key Ideas
- The Hook Model is a neutral tool; the same techniques build healthy habits or harmful ones, so builders carry real responsibility.
- The **Manipulation Matrix** sorts makers by two questions: *Does it materially improve the user's life?* and *Would the maker use it themselves?*
- The four quadrants are the **Facilitator**, the **Entertainer**, the **Peddler**, and the **Dealer**.
- The **Facilitator** — building something you'd use that also helps users — is the ethical sweet spot and the strongest position for success.
- The **would-you-use-it test** is the quickest gut-check: if you wouldn't use it, you're probably not helping anyone.
- Habit-forming techniques demand ethical intent because they can be aimed at health and learning or at addiction and exploitation.

## Powerful Techniques Demand a Conscience
Eyal pauses the how-to to confront the obvious discomfort: the methods in this book work, and methods that reliably shape behavior can be pointed at good ends or bad ones. A technique that gets someone to exercise, save money, or learn a language is the same technique, mechanically, that gets someone to gamble away a paycheck or doomscroll past midnight. The Hook Model doesn't come with a moral valence built in; the builder supplies that.

This is why Eyal refuses to let designers hide behind "I just build the tools." The person constructing the hook decides what behavior it drives and for whose benefit. Manipulation — using persuasion to change behavior — isn't automatically wrong; medicine, education, and good design all "manipulate" behavior toward better outcomes. What separates ethical from unethical isn't the presence of persuasion but its purpose and its honesty. To make that judgment concrete rather than a matter of vague conscience, Eyal offers a framework.

## The Manipulation Matrix
The **Manipulation Matrix** is a two-by-two grid built from two questions the maker must ask honestly.

The first: **Does the product materially improve the user's life?** Not "could someone enjoy it" but does it make the user genuinely, measurably better off. The second: **Would I, the maker, use the product myself?** This is a proxy for belief — if you wouldn't use what you built, you probably don't really think it helps.

Crossing these two questions yields four roles, and Eyal names each:

**The Facilitator** builds a product they would use themselves *and* that materially improves users' lives. Both answers are yes. This is the ideal position: the maker understands the user because the maker *is* a user, and the product genuinely helps. Eyal argues facilitators have the highest chance of success, because deep, first-hand understanding of the problem is the surest foundation for a product people truly want.

**The Entertainer** builds something they would use themselves but that does *not* materially improve users' lives — it entertains rather than betters. This isn't inherently wrong; entertainment has value. But Eyal notes it's a weaker position, often producing products that are fun yet fleeting, and makers here should be honest that they're in the amusement business, not the improvement business.

**The Peddler** believes the product improves users' lives but would *not* use it themselves. This gap is a warning sign. The peddler is designing for a person they don't understand from the inside, often convinced they're helping while lacking the empathy to know. Eyal treats this as ethically shaky and commercially risky — a maker prescribing a medicine they'd never take.

**The Dealer** builds something that neither improves users' lives *nor* appeals to the maker. Both answers are no. This is pure exploitation — hooking people on something the maker knows to be worthless or harmful. It is the one quadrant Eyal condemns outright as indefensible.

## Use the Would-You-Use-It Test
The fastest way to place yourself on the matrix is the **would-you-use-it test**: honestly ask whether you'd be a genuine user of your own product. It collapses the two questions into one intuitive check, because a maker who truly uses and values their product usually clears both bars — they believe it helps and they're living proof.

Eyal's guidance is to aim squarely for the Facilitator quadrant. Build for yourself, build something you believe genuinely improves lives, and you land in the position that is both the most ethical and the most likely to succeed. The alignment is not a coincidence: products built by makers who deeply understand and share the user's need tend to be better products.

The test also functions as an ongoing filter, not a one-time verdict. As a product evolves — as growth pressure pushes toward darker patterns, as monetization tempts you to exploit the very habits you built — the question "would I still use this, and does it still help my users" keeps you honest. The moment the honest answer tips toward no, you've drifted from Facilitator toward Peddler or Dealer, and it's time to course-correct.

## Building Habits People Can Be Glad They Have
Eyal closes the ethical discussion by widening the lens. Habit-forming technology is not going away; the techniques are known and will be used. The question is what they're used for. Directed well, these methods can help people build the habits they wish they had — exercising, saving, learning, connecting meaningfully. Directed poorly, they manufacture compulsions people resent and can't escape.

Builders, therefore, carry a duty that Eyal doesn't let them dodge: to use these powerful methods to help users live better by their *own* definition, not to trap them for the company's benefit. The best habit-forming products are ones users would consciously endorse if they stepped back and reflected — habits they're glad to have, not ones they'd disable if they could. That standard, more than any legal or metric threshold, is the one Eyal asks makers to hold themselves to. It also gives users a clear question to turn on the products in their own lives: is this serving a goal I actually endorse, or is it serving itself?

## In Practice
- Run the would-you-use-it test on your own product honestly; if you wouldn't use it, treat that as evidence you're not truly helping users.
- Place your product on the Manipulation Matrix by answering both questions — does it materially help, and would you use it — and name the quadrant you land in.
- Aim for the Facilitator position by building for a need you personally share, which strengthens both the ethics and the odds of success.
- Re-run the matrix as growth and monetization pressures mount; watch for drift from Facilitator toward Peddler or Dealer and correct it.
- Ask whether users would consciously endorse the habit you're building if they reflected on it — build habits people are glad to have.
- As a user, judge the apps in your life by the same test: does this materially improve my life on my own terms, or is it a Dealer's product I'd be better off deleting?
