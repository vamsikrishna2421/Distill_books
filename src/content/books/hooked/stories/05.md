# Investment: Your Own Labor Becomes the Lock

Three gears of the machine are turning now, friend. The trigger sparks. The effortless action follows. The variable reward lands — or deliciously doesn't. Most builders would stop here and call it a loop.

But the masters add a fourth step, and it is the strangest one — because it breaks the first rule of design. After rewarding the user... the product asks them to *work*.

Fill in a profile. Follow five people. Save a playlist. Rate the driver. Nothing big — a pinch of effort, always *after* the reward, when the brain is warm and agreeable. It looks like a favor to the app. It is actually the deepest hook of all. Tonight I show you why the work is the trap.

## The furniture you love because you built it

![Hands placing the final wooden block onto an intricate handmade model on a workbench in warm lamplight](/story-art/hooked/05-build.jpg)

Behavioral scientists ran a mischievous experiment. They had people fold origami — amateurs, making frankly mediocre cranes and frogs — then asked them to bid on their own creations, while other people bid on the same pieces.

::: guess
The makers and the strangers looked at the very same folded paper. How differently do you think they valued it?
---
The makers bid roughly **five times more** — valuing their wobbly cranes near what experts' work fetched, while strangers saw them for the scrap they were. Researchers call it the **IKEA effect**: we irrationally treasure what our own hands helped build, the way a bookshelf you assembled feels somehow *yours* in a way no store-bought shelf does. And it has a sly cousin, discovered by Benjamin Franklin himself: wanting to win over a hostile rival, Franklin didn't do *him* a favor — he asked to *borrow a rare book from the man's library*. The rival lent it... and warmed to Franklin ever after. Because the mind hates contradiction: *I did this man a favor... so I must like him.* We don't invest in what we love, friend — it runs the other way. **We love what we've invested in.** Every ounce of effort you pour into a product is quietly running this machinery on yourself.
:::

## The app that gets better the longer you stay

Now watch what your small labors *build*. Eyal's phrase is **stored value** — and it inverts everything we know about owning things.

A car loses value the day you drive it home. A phone scratches, a sofa sags — the physical world depreciates with use. But habit-forming products do the opposite: *they appreciate.* Every use deposits something that makes the product more useful tomorrow than it was today — and the deposits come in five currencies.

**Content**: every playlist you curate, every photo you upload, every note you file makes the service more yours — a music app with ten years of your playlists is not an app, it is your musical autobiography. **Data**: the finance app that finally has all your accounts linked, the fitness app holding five years of your runs — accuracy and history you cannot re-create elsewhere. **Followers**: an audience built tweet by tweet over years — walk away and you start at zero. **Reputation**: the five-star seller rating, the pristine driver score — trust earned transaction by transaction, worthless anywhere else. **Skill**: the hundred hours you spent mastering an editing tool — switching rivals means becoming a beginner again, and nobody volunteers for that.

See the common shape? None of these five can be transferred, and none can be bought — only *deposited, by you, over time*. The product didn't lock you in. **You built the lock yourself, one small investment at a time — and the same walls that make it valuable make it expensive to leave.**

## The trap that reloads itself

And here is the fourth step's final elegance — the investment doesn't just store value. It winds the spring for the *next* pass through the loop.

Send a message... and you have loaded a reply — a future notification, a trigger with your name on it. Follow someone... and their next post will come find you. Swipe right... and the possibility of a match is now pending, a little firecracker waiting to go off in your pocket. Post anything, anywhere... and every like it might gather is a trigger already addressed to you.

This is why the loop is a *cycle* and not a line: **each investment plants the seed of the next trigger.** The product no longer needs to interrupt you on its own schedule — your own past actions summon you back. And with each pass — trigger, action, reward, investment, trigger — the loop runs a little smoother, the external prompts matter a little less, until the machine hums along on feelings and stored value alone. That is a habit, fully installed: a wheel you built, spinning under its own weight.

## Reading it from both sides of the glass

For the builder: don't ask for the investment before the reward — warm brains are generous brains, so let the value land first, then invite a small deposit. Start tiny; commitment escalates on its own. And design every investment to do double duty: store value *and* load the next trigger.

For the human: audit your locks. For each app that holds you, name what you've actually stored — the playlists, the streaks, the followers, the ratings — and ask the ruthless question: *is this treasure, or is it just my own effort whispering that it must have been worth it?* Some stored value is real wealth — genuinely useful history and craft. Some is a sunk cost wearing a crown, keeping you loyal to a product you'd never choose fresh today. Telling those two apart, honestly, is how an adult decides what stays on their phone. And the streak, friend — ask especially about the streak, that little number engineered to make *stopping* feel like losing something. Losing what, exactly? Count it.

## Before you go

So there it is — the whole machine, assembled before your eyes. Trigger. Action. Variable reward. Investment. Around and around, until checking becomes a reflex and the reflex feels like choice.

And now that you've seen the gears, a colder question walks into the room — the one this book cannot end without answering. This machinery works. It works on children. It works on the lonely. It works on *you*. So: who should be allowed to build it? Where is the line between a product that helps people live better... and a beautifully engineered addiction? ## Key takeaways

- The fourth step asks for work *after* the reward — small effort, warm brain, no resistance.
- The IKEA effect: we love what we've invested in, not the other way around — effort manufactures attachment.
- Stored value makes products appreciate with use: content, data, followers, reputation, and skill — none transferable.
- You build the lock yourself: the switching cost of leaving is everything you deposited.
- Every investment loads the next trigger — messages summon replies, follows summon posts; the loop reloads itself.
- Audit your locks honestly: real treasure or sunk cost in a crown? Especially the streaks.
