# Trigger: What Sparks the Behavior

Last time you noticed the automatic thumb. Tonight I show you what fires it — because no behavior in the world begins by itself. Every check, every scroll, every open starts with a spark. The engineers call it a **trigger**, and there are two kingdoms of them: the ones that live in the world... and the one that eventually lives in *you*.

That migration — from the world into your head — is the entire secret of habit-forming products. Watch closely.

## The four sparks of the outer world

![A city street at dusk crowded with glowing billboards and phone screens, every surface calling for attention](/story-art/hooked/02-street.jpg)

Before any habit exists, the spark must come from outside. Eyal calls these **external triggers**, and their signature is that the instruction rides inside them: a button that says log in, a notification that says someone commented. Prompt and command arrive together.

They come in four flavors, and the distinction is money, not vocabulary. **Paid triggers** — advertising — buy attention by the ounce; fine for acquiring users, ruinous for retaining them: *you cannot buy your way to a habit.* **Earned triggers** — press, virality, a featured spot — are free in dollars, costly in effort, impossible to summon on demand; they spike and fade. **Relationship triggers** — one human showing another: the invitation, the shared link, the recommendation — can light real wildfires when use naturally spreads person to person. And **owned triggers** — the app icon on your home screen, the newsletter you subscribed to, the push alert you agreed to — are the real estate *you granted* in your own pocket, and once granted, they can fire again and again at zero marginal cost.

The division of labor: paid and earned get users in the door. Owned and relationship bring them back. But hear the deeper truth — even a full arsenal of external triggers is a *rented* foundation. They are training wheels. Their whole purpose is to walk the user toward something far more durable.

## The spark that moves inside

Every notification, every email, every icon-glance is a repetition — and repetition forges association. Fire the external spark often enough beside a feeling, and one day the product no longer needs to call you.

**You call it.**

That is the **internal trigger**: a cue delivered by your own mind — a thought, a routine, or most powerfully, an *emotion*. The instruction that used to arrive in a notification is now encoded in your memory. You feel a certain way, and reaching for the product is simply what your hand does next. This is the moment a product transitions from something you are *told* to use into something you *reach* for — and the moment its economics transform, because a trigger firing from inside the user's head costs the company nothing, forever.

## The engine runs on discomfort

So which emotions make the strongest internal triggers? Here Eyal's answer is uncomfortable, and it deserves to be heard in full daylight.

::: guess
You might guess joy, curiosity, delight — the good feelings. What kind of emotion do you think actually powers most of the checking, scrolling, and refreshing you do in a day?
---
The negative ones. **Boredom. Loneliness. Frustration. Uncertainty. Indecision. The fear of missing out.** Watch the pattern once and you cannot unsee it: we open Instagram when bored. We Google the *instant* uncertainty stings. We open social feeds at a pang of loneliness; we refresh when FOMO flares. These small dark feelings arise dozens of times a day, they are mildly painful, and they demand relief *now* — which makes them superb engines for automatic behavior. The user is not deciding to use a product. The user is reaching for an exit — and the product has positioned itself as the nearest door. So when your hand drifts toward your phone tonight, pause one second and name the feeling underneath. It is almost never "I want the app." It is almost always a small discomfort looking for the fastest way out.
:::

## Five whys down to the feeling

Fine — but *which* emotion does a given product soothe? For that, Eyal borrows a drill from Toyota's factory floor: the **5 Whys**. Ask why a user does the behavior; then ask why of the answer; five layers down, the rationalizations run out and you hit bedrock.

::: guess
Eyal's example: Julie uses email to "stay in touch with colleagues." Ask why five times. Where do you think the chain bottoms out?
---
Why email? To share information. Why? To know what's happening at work. Why does that matter? To know whether her work is valued — whether she still *belongs*. Why does that matter? Because at the bottom of the chain sits not a feature but a fear: **the fear of losing connection.** That is the drill's lesson: users will always tell you they want features; the real trigger lives several layers beneath, in a feeling. Build for the feeling — the boredom, the loneliness, the fear of not belonging — and one day the product fires from inside the user's own head. And as a user, run the same drill on yourself: five whys under any app you can't stop opening will teach you more than any screen-time report.
:::

## Two moves before we go on

For the builder in you: inventory your triggers by the four types, confirm you aren't funding a habit with paid sparks, and earn — never assume — the permission for owned ones. The maturing habit shows up as users returning *unprompted*.

For the human in you, one act of quiet rebellion tonight: open your notification settings and turn off everything nonessential. Every push alert you disable removes one company's wire into your attention. They needed your consent to place it there. You can take it back in four taps.

## Before you go

So the spark fires — from a billboard, a push alert, or a pang of loneliness. The user's hand moves toward the product. And here the machine meets its second gear, and a beautiful, ruthless question: *what determines whether that motion actually completes?*

The answer is an equation — motivation, ability, and prompt in a precise dance — plus the reason every successful app looks almost insultingly simple. Next time: **Action**, and the art of making the behavior so easy that not doing it takes more effort than doing it.

## Key takeaways

- Every behavior begins with a trigger; external ones carry their instruction, internal ones fire from your own mind.
- Paid and earned triggers acquire; owned and relationship triggers retain — and all external triggers are training wheels.
- The habit is complete when emotion replaces notification: the user reaches, unprompted.
- Negative emotions — boredom, loneliness, uncertainty, FOMO — are the heavy lifters; products become exits from small discomforts.
- Drill five whys beneath any behavior to find the real feeling; build (or understand yourself) at that layer.
- Reclaim your attention: every nonessential notification you disable removes one hook.
