# Action: Making the Behavior Easy

The spark has fired, friend. The notification glowed, or the little pang of boredom whispered — and the hand began to move. Now comes the moment engineers obsess over more than any other: the razor-thin instant between *wanting* to do something... and actually *doing* it.

Because here is the uncomfortable truth this chapter is built on: most sparks die. Most impulses fizzle before they become action. And the products that own your habits are the ones that learned exactly why — and removed every reason for the fizzle.

## The equation that runs your thumb

![A finger about to tap a single glowing button on a phone screen in a dark room, the gesture effortless](/story-art/hooked/03-tap.jpg)

Eyal borrows his engine from the Stanford behavior scientist **B.J. Fogg**, who compressed all of human doing into one formula: **B = MAT**. A Behavior happens when three things collide in the same moment — **Motivation** (how much you want it), **Ability** (how easy it is), and a **Trigger** (the prompt we met last time). Remove any one of the three, and the behavior simply does not happen. No willpower involved, no character judgment — just an equation missing a term.

Motivation, Fogg says, runs on three ancient levers: seeking pleasure and avoiding pain; seeking hope and avoiding fear; seeking social acceptance and avoiding rejection. Advertising pulls these levers all day. But motivation is expensive to raise and impossible to hold high — you cannot keep a human excited forever.

Which brings us to the chapter's great secret.

::: guess
A builder has budget to improve exactly one term of the equation — pump up users' *motivation*, or increase their *ability* by making the product easier. Which one does Eyal say wins, almost every time?
---
**Ability. Ease beats desire.** Increasing motivation is costly, slow, and unreliable — persuading humans to want something more is one of the hardest jobs on earth. But shaving steps off a task is cheap, permanent, and it works on everyone at once. This is why the biggest companies of our age are, at bottom, *simplicity machines*: Google made finding anything one box; the iPhone made photography one swipe; Amazon made buying one click. Each took a behavior people already mildly wanted and made it so easy that the mild want was *enough*. When a product feels like it "read your mind," what really happened is humbler — someone counted every step between urge and reward, and deleted all but one.
:::

## The six walls between wanting and doing

So what exactly makes something "hard"? Fogg names six factors of simplicity — six currencies a task can overcharge you in: **time** (how long it takes), **money** (what it costs), **physical effort** (the labor involved), **brain cycles** (how much thinking it demands), **social deviance** (how odd it looks to others), and **non-routine** (how far it sits from your existing habits).

The rule: a chain is as weak as its most expensive link. Find whichever of the six your user is *shortest* on — that scarcity, not the average, is what kills the behavior. The office worker has money but no time; the student has time but no money; the tired commuter has neither brain cycles nor patience. Design for the scarcest resource in the moment of the trigger.

And once you know the six walls, you see the demolition everywhere: sign-up forms became "Log in with Google" — time and brain cycles deleted. Sharing became one retweet button — effort deleted. Buying became stored cards and biometric approval — money made *painless*, which should give you a small chill.

## The lens that tilts the equation

There is a third character in this chapter, quieter than motivation and ability but wired deeper: **perception**. Because humans do not weigh the world objectively — the brain takes shortcuts, and the shortcuts can be designed around.

::: guess
Eyal describes phenomena that bend behavior without changing the product at all — the same item, the same effort, yet people act differently. Can you name one trick of framing that does this?
---
He gives several, and each is a lever hiding in plain sight. **The scarcity effect**: the same cookie tastes more desirable from a nearly-empty jar — "only 3 left in stock" is manufacturing value out of emptiness. **The framing effect**: a world-class violinist earned pocket change busking in a subway station, then thousands in a concert hall days later — same music, different frame, different perceived worth. **The anchoring effect**: the "was $499, now $299" tag makes $299 feel like a gift, though the anchor may never have been real. And **the endowed progress effect**: a loyalty card with two stamps already filled in gets completed far more often than an empty one requiring the same purchases — because feeling *already started* is motivation for free. None of these change the task. They change the mind looking at it. Your sense of "how much I want this" is softer clay than you think.
:::

## Reading it from both sides of the glass

For the builder in you, this chapter is a working checklist. Take your product's core action and walk the path from trigger to done, counting every step. Then ask Fogg's question of each: which of the six simplicity factors is my user scarcest in *right now* — and delete accordingly. Test ease before you spend a cent on motivation; a simpler flow beats a louder pitch.

For the human in you, run the equation in reverse — because it works both ways, and this is where the chapter quietly hands you your life back. Every behavior you wish you did more, make *easier*: the book on the pillow, the running shoes by the door, the guitar out of its case on a stand. Every behavior you wish you did less, make *harder*: the app logged out after every use, the phone charging in the kitchen overnight, the icon buried three folders deep. You will not out-motivate a machine built by a thousand engineers to be effortless. But you can out-*design* it — with friction as your bodyguard.

## Before you go

So the trigger fires, the equation resolves, the action completes — the scroll, the tap, the open. And now the machine faces its most delicate task of all. Because if what happens next were merely *pleasant*, you would grow bored. Predictable rewards, it turns out, are forgettable rewards.

The genius — and the menace — of habit-forming products lives in the next step: what the slot machine knows about your brain that you don't. Why *not knowing* what comes next is more compelling than any prize. Next time: **Variable Reward** — the itch, the scratch, and the beautiful uncertainty in between.

## Key takeaways

- B = MAT: behavior needs motivation, ability, and a trigger in the same moment — remove one and it doesn't happen.
- Ease beats desire: increasing ability is cheaper and more reliable than pumping motivation — simplify first.
- Audit the six costs — time, money, effort, brain cycles, social deviance, non-routine — and fix the scarcest one.
- Perception bends the equation: scarcity, framing, anchoring, and endowed progress change behavior without changing the task.
- As a user, run it in reverse: add friction to what owns you, remove friction from what serves you.
- The next gear is the deepest: predictable rewards bore the brain — uncertainty is the real engine.
