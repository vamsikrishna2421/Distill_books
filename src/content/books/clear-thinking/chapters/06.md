# Execute, Monitor, and Judge the Process — Not the Outcome

## Key Ideas
- A premortem — imagining your plan has already failed and working backward to explain why — surfaces risks that ordinary "what could go wrong?" brainstorming misses, because prospective hindsight makes failure concrete instead of hypothetical.
- Tripwires are if-then commitments made in advance — like a mountaineer's turnaround time — that take execution decisions away from your future, compromised self and hand them to your present, clear-headed one.
- Commander's intent lets you delegate execution without micromanaging: communicate the goal and the reasoning behind it, and people on the ground can adapt when reality diverges from the plan.
- Luck contaminates outcomes, so a good result never proves a good decision and a bad result never proves a bad one; judging by outcomes alone teaches you the wrong lessons.
- A decision journal — written at the moment of choice, before results arrive — is the only reliable defense against hindsight bias and the only honest way to audit your process.
- Process quality is the single variable you fully control, and unlike luck, it compounds: better process today produces better positions, options, and decisions tomorrow.

## Run a Premortem: Assume Failure, Then Explain It

Most plans get stress-tested with a polite question — "does anyone see any problems?" — asked in a room where optimism and social pressure suppress dissent. The **premortem**, developed by research psychologist **Gary Klein**, inverts the exercise. Instead of asking what *might* go wrong, you announce that the project is finished and it *has* failed — completely — and give everyone a few minutes to write down every reason why.

The shift sounds cosmetic but changes what the brain does. Research on **prospective hindsight** (Deborah Mitchell, J. Edward Russo, and Nancy Pennington) found that imagining an event as having already occurred, rather than as merely possible, boosted people's ability to correctly identify reasons for outcomes by roughly 30 percent. Certainty unlocks specificity: "we failed" prompts concrete causes — the key supplier flaked, the timeline assumed no one got sick, the champion left the company — where "we might fail" prompts vague hedging.

The premortem also fixes a social problem. In a normal planning meeting, the skeptic pointing out flaws looks disloyal. In a premortem, finding failure modes *is the assignment*, so the most junior person can voice the concern everyone privately shares. Parrish frames this as designing around the social default (chapter 1) rather than pretending it doesn't exist.

Run it in three steps. First, state the frame: it is one year from now and the decision was a disaster. Second, have every participant independently generate reasons — independence matters, because group discussion anchors everyone on the first ideas raised. Third, use the list to strengthen the plan: eliminate the preventable causes, build buffers against the unavoidable ones, and convert the remaining risks into monitoring signals. You will not catch everything. But a plan that has already survived its own imagined autopsy fails less often, and fails smaller, than one protected only by hope.

## Tripwires: Decide Now What Your Future Self Must Do

Execution is where the four defaults come back for revenge. Mid-project, you will be tired, invested, rushed, and emotionally attached — exactly the vulnerable states that hijack judgment. The fix is the **tripwire**: a predetermined condition, set while you are still thinking clearly, that automatically triggers a predetermined response.

Parrish's canonical example is the mountaineer's **turnaround time**. Experienced climbers decide, before leaving camp, the exact hour at which they will turn back regardless of how close the summit looks. The rule exists because the moment of temptation is the worst possible moment to weigh the choice: at altitude, oxygen-starved, having spent years and tens of thousands of dollars, "just thirty more minutes" feels irresistible — and climbers who ignore turnaround times, as in the 1996 Everest disaster, descend in darkness and storms, and some never descend at all. The tripwire works precisely because it removes in-the-moment deliberation. The decision was already made by a smarter version of you.

Good tripwires have two parts: a bright-line signal (a date, a dollar amount, a metric threshold — something you cannot argue with) and a pre-committed response (exit, escalate, pause, reassess). "We'll revisit if things get bad" is not a tripwire; "if we haven't hit 100 paying users by March 1, we shut it down" is. Vague conditions invite the very rationalization they were meant to prevent.

The strongest version ties your hands entirely — the Ulysses move of making the bad option unavailable rather than merely discouraged. Give a colleague standing authority to enforce the tripwire, automate the response, or make reversal require sign-off from someone outside the emotional blast radius. Since sunk costs, ego, and inertia all pull toward "keep going," assume your future self will lobby against the rule, and design the rule so the lobbying fails.

## Commander's Intent: Delegate the Goal, Not the Steps

No plan survives contact with reality, and you cannot be everywhere the plan meets reality. The military's answer is **commander's intent**: rather than issuing detailed orders that collapse the moment conditions change, commanders communicate the objective, the reason it matters, and the constraints — then trust subordinates to improvise the "how" when circumstances shift.

Applied to execution, this means every person carrying out a piece of your decision should be able to answer two questions without calling you: *What outcome are we actually trying to achieve?* and *Why?* When the supplier misses the shipment or the client changes scope, someone who knows only their task list stalls and escalates; someone who knows the intent adapts on the spot and usually adapts correctly.

This solves the bottleneck problem that kills execution in most organizations. If every deviation from plan routes back to the decision-maker for approval, execution runs at the speed of one person's inbox, and the defaults — especially ego, which whispers that only you can decide correctly — keep it that way. Communicating intent converts a fragile chain of instructions into a resilient network of people optimizing for the same result.

It also raises the quality of what comes back to you. People executing against intent flag genuine conflicts with the goal instead of burying you in trivial exceptions, because they can distinguish the two. The discipline is on the leader's side: writing down intent forces you to articulate what actually matters about the decision, which frequently exposes fuzziness you would otherwise discover only after failure. If you cannot state the intent in a few sentences, the problem is not delegation — it is that you have not finished deciding (chapter 4's definition principle, revisited at the execution stage).

## Good Decisions Are Not Good Outcomes

The most important idea in the book's decision section is a distinction most people never make: decision quality and outcome quality are different things, linked only loosely, because **luck** sits between them. You can make an excellent decision and get a terrible result; you can make a reckless one and get rich. A drunk driver who arrives home safely did not make a good decision, and a well-reasoned bet that loses was not a mistake.

Judging decisions purely by results — what poker players call *resulting* — teaches systematically wrong lessons. Reward the reckless bet that happened to pay off and you train yourself (and your organization) to take more reckless bets, right up until variance collects. Punish the sound decision that got unlucky and you teach people to stop making sound decisions and start managing appearances. Over time, outcome-only judgment selects for gamblers and politicians rather than thinkers.

Think of it as a two-by-two. Good process with a good outcome is earned success. Good process with a bad outcome is bad luck — repeat the process. Bad process with a bad outcome is at least an honest teacher. The dangerous quadrant is bad process with a good outcome: dumb luck disguised as skill, which reinforces exactly the behavior most likely to ruin you later.

The practical conclusion follows from what you control. You do not control outcomes — the world injects randomness into every result. You control only the process: how you defined the problem, what options you generated, what criteria you used, what safeguards you built. And process, unlike luck, **compounds**: each well-made decision leaves you in a better position, and position determines your future options (the book's opening argument, now completed). Evaluate yourself and your people on process visible *at the time of the decision*, and let outcomes serve as data about the world rather than verdicts on your worth.

## The Decision Journal: Your Defense Against Hindsight Bias

You cannot judge your process later if you cannot remember it accurately — and you cannot. **Hindsight bias**, the "I knew it all along" effect, quietly rewrites memory so that whatever happened seems obvious in retrospect. After outcomes arrive, your brain edits the story: uncertainty you genuinely felt disappears, reasons you never considered get backfilled, and lucky guesses become foresight. Learning from experience is impossible when the experience keeps getting revised.

The **decision journal** is Parrish's countermeasure. At the moment of a significant decision — before any results exist — write down: the situation as you see it, the variables that matter, what you decided, the alternatives you rejected, what you expect to happen (with rough probabilities where you can), and, critically, how you feel physically and mentally. That last item matters because vulnerable states — tired, rushed, angry (chapter 3) — leave fingerprints on decisions, and the journal is how you learn to spot yours.

Then, when the outcome arrives, review the entry. The comparison answers the question resulting can't: was this a good decision, or just a good outcome? If your reasoning was sound and the world zigged, no process change is needed. If you got what you wanted for reasons you never anticipated, you got lucky — treat the win with suspicion. Over dozens of entries, patterns emerge that no single decision reveals: domains where you're overconfident, information you habitually ignore, moods that correlate with your worst calls.

The journal only works if it is honest and contemporaneous. Written after the fact, it becomes another instrument of hindsight bias. Kept faithfully, it turns your own history into a training set — the raw material for the safeguards, rules, and self-knowledge the earlier chapters describe. Judge the process, record the process, improve the process; the outcomes, on average and over time, take care of themselves.

## In Practice
- Before your next significant commitment, run a ten-minute premortem: write "it's a year from now and this failed completely," list every cause independently before discussing, then patch the plan against the top three.
- Attach a tripwire to any ongoing bet you're emotionally invested in — a specific metric, date, or dollar threshold plus a pre-committed response — and tell someone else so you can't quietly renegotiate it.
- When you delegate anything this week, state the commander's intent in two sentences: the outcome you need and why it matters. Then let the person choose the steps.
- Start a decision journal today: for each significant decision, record the situation, your reasoning, expected outcomes with rough probabilities, alternatives rejected, and your physical/mental state at the time.
- Schedule a review of past journal entries whose outcomes are now known, and sort each into the process/outcome two-by-two — hunt especially for bad-process wins you've been crediting to skill.
- Audit one recent judgment you made of someone else's decision: did you evaluate their process with the information they had, or just the result? Re-grade it on process.
