# The Man With Two Lamps

Last time I promised to answer a puzzle. Chanakya taught that knowledge is the supreme wealth — so why did he fill page after page with advice about *money*?

Here is why. Before he was the most powerful man in India, Chanakya was a poor teacher's son. And he had watched, up close, what an empty purse does to a good person. The world did not pause to admire his father's learning or his honesty. It simply looked past him, the way you look past a beggar at the market.

Chanakya never forgot that. And out of it came the most honest chapter in his whole book. Pull your blanket closer — tonight's story has a cold wind in it, but it ends warm.

## The tree full of birds

![At dusk, birds stream away from a bare, fruitless tree as a lone traveller watches](/story-art/chanakya-neeti/03-tree.jpg)

Near Chanakya's village stood a great fig tree. In the fruiting season it was the noisiest place in the district — hundreds of birds feasting, singing, fighting for branches. A boy once said to him: "Look how they love that tree!"

Then the season turned. The fruit ran out.

::: guess
When the last fruit was gone, what do you think the birds did?
---
They left. Every single one, within days. No farewell, no loyalty, no memory of a thousand meals. They simply flew to the next tree that had fruit. Chanakya wants you to stare at that empty tree until it stings — because, he says, people behave the same way. Birds do not nest in a barren tree. Crowds do not gather around an empty purse. Support flows toward strength. It does not flow toward need.
:::

Harsh? Yes. Chanakya says it *should* sting — because most of us make one specific, dangerous mistake: we assume the goodwill we enjoy in good times will carry us through the bad ones. Much of it will not. Plenty of the warmth around you is quietly conditional on your staying strong.

But watch what lesson he draws. Not bitterness. Not "trust no one" — remember, he built his life on a few deeply tested friendships. The lesson is *preparation*: since rescue is exactly what the world is least reliable at providing, build yourself into someone who does not need rescuing. Reserves. Skills. A name for competence. Strength first — then the birds can come and go as they please, and it will never break you.

And this is why he refuses to be shy about money. Wealth, he says plainly, is the ground floor of dignity. It buys you the standing to be taken seriously, and the freedom to say *no* to a bad deal. Poverty does not make you noble; it ties your hands — and it ties them tightest exactly when you most need them free. So take earning seriously. Not because money is the goal of life — but because it is the platform under nearly every other goal you have.

## The grain and the flood

How much should you keep? Chanakya's answer comes from the farmer's calendar.

Every farmer in his world stored grain in the good harvest, because the monsoon does not send a warning letter. The flood year comes when it comes. And here Chanakya notices something sly about human nature: the moment when saving is *easiest* — money flowing, life comfortable — is exactly the moment it feels most unnecessary. And the moment you desperately need reserves is exactly the moment you can no longer build them.

That mismatch is why so many capable people get wrecked by perfectly predictable troubles. They treated the harvest as permanent and ate all of it.

So his rule is simple and a little uncomfortable: save when it feels pointless, *because that is the only time you can*. Live below your best year. Guard what you have from leaks and impulse as carefully as you earned it. You are storing strength in the season of plenty so that the season of scarcity — whenever it arrives — cannot break you.

## The man with two lamps

![Chanakya at his writing desk at night, one oil lamp burning and a second lamp beside it](/story-art/chanakya-neeti/03-lamps.jpg)

Now for my favourite legend about this man. It is told that a visitor once came to see Chanakya late at night and found him working by the light of a small oil lamp. As they finished the kingdom's business and began to chat as friends, Chanakya paused — put out the burning lamp — and lit a *different* one standing beside it.

The visitor was baffled. Two identical little lamps!

::: guess
Why would the most powerful minister in India blow out one lamp and light another, just to chat?
---
The first lamp, Chanakya explained, burned oil paid for by the state treasury — so it burned only for the state's work. The moment the conversation turned personal, he switched to the lamp filled with oil he had bought with his own money. That is the whole man in one gesture: money is a *tool with a purpose*, and every coin knows its job. And it is his darkest warning made visible: trade your integrity for money — sacrifice the imperishable for the perishable — and you will end up losing both.
:::

The two lamps also solve the puzzle at the heart of this chapter. Chanakya just told you to save like a farmer — yet he pours scorn on the *hoarder*. He mocks the miser who starves beside a full granary, who gets all the anxiety of guarding wealth and none of the good it can do. Money that only sits, he says, is not a triumph. It is a loss wearing a proud face — an unused tool, rusting.

So which is it — save or spend? Neither, and both. Chanakya is not pro-saving or pro-spending; he is **pro-purpose**. Money hoarded for nothing and money scattered on nothing are the same mistake in different clothes. Wealth should be *in motion*: protecting your family, funding your work, backing the people and aims you believe in. Saved when it should be. Deployed when it should be. Every lamp burning the right oil.

## How to give

Which brings us to the gentlest skill in this chapter — and Chanakya treats it exactly like a skill: giving.

He believed in generosity. He also believed most people do it badly. Scattering money to feel generous, he says, wastes the gift and sometimes causes harm. Real giving has two disciplines.

First: *aim it*. A gift to a deserving hand — someone who will truly use it and value it — multiplies. The same gift to the wrong hand is thrown away, and may even reward exactly what should not be rewarded. Before you give, ask the uncomfortable question: will this actually help, or does it just relieve my urge to look kind?

Second: *time it*. Help that arrives at the right moment — when it genuinely changes someone's road — is worth ten times the same help arriving late or carelessly. And never give so much that you hollow out your own foundation, because a giver who ruins themselves has nothing left to give anyone.

Do it right, Chanakya says, and something lovely happens. A flower's scent can only travel with the wind — but a good person's influence spreads in every direction at once. That is the finish line of all this unsentimental money talk: earn seriously, save wisely, spend with purpose, give with aim — and you become the tree that *chooses* to feed the birds, instead of the one praying they'll stay.

## The last pages of the ledger

The complete telling keeps three more entries from the old man's money ledger — including the one with a built-in expiry date.

First, on dirty money. Chanakya wrote: *wealth earned by crooked means may stay ten years — but in the eleventh it vanishes, root and all.* Do not read "ten years" as accounting; read it as a pattern he had watched over and over from inside a treasury: ill-gotten gains *feel* permanent for years, long enough for the owner to relax — and then the same crookedness that earned the money attracts the partner, the enemy, or the ruler who takes it all, plus the tree it grew on. Clean earning is not just virtue. It is the only kind that compounds without a fuse burning inside it.

Second, his brightest verse about giving — a quartet grandmothers could sing: *charity puts an end to poverty; right conduct puts an end to misery; discretion puts an end to ignorance; and scrutiny puts an end to fear.* Look at the first line twice, because it is strange: charity ends *poverty*? His meaning runs deeper than arithmetic — the giving hand is the hand that has stopped believing in its own scarcity, and that belief, more than any coin, is what poverty actually is.

And third, the famous warning with three names in it. *Too much of anything is ruin,* he wrote: *it was extraordinary beauty that got Sita kidnapped, extraordinary pride that got the demon-king Ravana killed, and extraordinary charity that destroyed the generous king Bali. Excess — everywhere — should be avoided.* Even virtues become vices at full throttle: generosity that hollows the giver, confidence that curdles into pride, even beauty that draws the wrong eyes. The old man's money chapter ends where his whole philosophy lives — in proportion. Everything with a purpose; nothing without a limit.

## Before you go

So the man who called knowledge the supreme wealth also kept two lamps on his desk — because he knew that dignity needs a floor, floods need grain, and money is only ever as good as its purpose.

But money raises a harder question, and you may have felt it hiding inside tonight's story. When your tree is full of fruit, the birds *will* come — friends, flatterers, partners, hangers-on. Some are treasures. Some are thieves with warm smiles. How did Chanakya — the man who trusted no one untested — decide who got a seat by his fire?

Next time: his field guide to friends and enemies — the four tests of a true friend, the friend who is worse than an enemy, and the enemy who is worth more than a friend.

## Key takeaways

- Money is the floor under your dignity — take earning seriously, without apology.
- Support flows toward strength, so build reserves that make rescue unnecessary.
- Save when it feels unnecessary — that is the only time saving is possible.
- Money is a tool: neither hoard it nor scatter it; give every coin a purpose.
- Give with aim and timing, from strength — and never trade your integrity for money.
- Crooked money carries a burning fuse — only clean earning compounds safely.
- Even virtues ruin at full throttle: keep proportion in generosity, confidence, everything.
