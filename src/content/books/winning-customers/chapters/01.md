# The New Growth Equation

## Key Ideas

- Acquisition economics have inverted: blended e-commerce CAC is up ~40% in two years (222% over eight), pushing winners toward referral, organic, and product-led channels.
- Retention beats acquisition mathematically — reward redeemers spend 25% more, and software companies at or above 100% net revenue retention grow more than twice as fast.
- Personalization is now both a baseline expectation (71% of consumers expect it) and a measurable growth lever worth a 10–15% revenue lift.
- Retailers are becoming media owners ($69.3B in US retail-media ad spend by 2026) and membership businesses ($46.4B in US membership fees).
- Omnichannel and hybrid beat pure-play: D2C has plateaued at ~19% of US retail e-commerce, and B2B buyers split evenly across in-person, remote, and self-service.

Before examining individual tactics, it helps to understand the structural forces that have reshaped commercial strategy since 2023. These forces apply, with local variation, across consumer retail, B2B, and services alike.

## Acquisition economics have inverted

The default growth motion of the 2010s — buy traffic on Google and Meta, convert it, repeat — has been undermined by rising costs and privacy-driven targeting loss. In consumer e-commerce, average blended acquisition cost now sits somewhere between roughly $42 and $84 per customer depending on the measurement basis, and one widely cited benchmark set puts the increase at about 40% over just two years, with blended CAC up 222% across the last eight years. Paid search alone runs $50–$130 per acquired customer in e-commerce, and an order of magnitude higher — $1,200 to $2,000 — in B2B software.

The strategic consequence is a flight to lower-cost, higher-trust channels. Referral is repeatedly identified as the cheapest acquisition route, at around $40–$65 per customer. Organic social now drives brand discovery for 29% of consumers, and up to 98% of shoppers consult reviews before buying. The best product-led software businesses acquire the majority of their users — 53% — through organic search, plus another 13% through product-driven referrals, largely bypassing paid media.

## Retention beats acquisition — mathematically

If winning a new customer is getting more expensive, keeping and growing an existing one becomes disproportionately valuable. The evidence is consistent across sectors. In loyalty programs, active members spend 10% more than enrolled-but-inactive members, reward redeemers spend 25% more, and top programs lift redeeming-member revenue 15–25% a year, per McKinsey.

In software, the median company with net revenue retention at or above 100% grew 48% year over year in the first half of 2024 — more than twice as fast as companies below that line — and low-retention firms churn at double the rate. Expansion revenue, not new logos, now supplies 40% of growth at software firms with $15–30M or more in recurring revenue.

## Personalization is table stakes and a growth lever

Personalization has crossed from differentiator to expectation: 71% of consumers expect personalized interactions and 76% are frustrated when they do not get them. It also pays. McKinsey finds personalization most often delivers a 10–15% revenue lift — 5–25% depending on the company — and that faster-growing firms derive 40% more of their revenue from it than slower-growing peers.

Generative AI is now compounding those gains. One large North American retailer generated $400M of value in a single year from pricing improvements, plus a further $150M from gen-AI-enabled targeted offers.

## Every retailer is becoming a media owner and a membership business

Two monetisation models have gone mainstream. Retail media networks — retailers selling advertising against their own shopper data — are forecast to grow from $58.8B to $69.3B in US ad spend between 2025 and 2026, with Amazon and Walmart capturing more than 89% of the incremental dollars.

In parallel, paid membership has become a category in its own right: US paid retail membership-fee revenue is forecast at $46.4B in 2025, up 10.8%, with Amazon alone taking 51.8%. Paid-loyalty members are twice as likely as free-program members to increase their spending after joining.

## Omnichannel and hybrid beat pure-play

The direct-to-consumer thesis — that brands could win by cutting out intermediaries — has largely plateaued. US D2C e-commerce is forecast to level off at around 19% of total retail e-commerce by 2025 and stay flat through 2028, and several former D2C darlings have retreated into wholesale.

The same lesson holds in B2B, where McKinsey's "rule of thirds" shows buyers wanting in-person, remote, and self-service interaction in roughly equal measure, and hybrid selling models generating up to 50% more revenue than traditional ones.

## In Practice

- Audit your channel mix: if paid media is the foundation rather than the amplifier, your CAC exposure is structural.
- Compute your own retention math — what a 5-point NRR improvement is worth versus the same spend on acquisition.
- Treat personalization as a revenue program with a P&L, not a marketing feature.
- Ask whether your customer data could support a media or membership revenue stream — or whether you should optimise as an advertiser on someone else's.
