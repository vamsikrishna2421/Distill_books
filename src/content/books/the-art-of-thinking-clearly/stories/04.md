# Lies You Tell Yourself

Fourth night, friend — and tonight we come to the chapter Dobelli says runs *underneath all the others*. The crowd can only fool you from outside. Money can only warp what it touches. But tonight's traps live closer than your own skin, because tonight's liar... is the one telling your story.

I warn you now, gently: every trap tonight will feel like it describes *other people*. That feeling is the trap working.

## The sequence that fooled almost everyone

![A person at a desk by lamplight writing in a notebook, surrounded by scattered papers, one page held up to the light](/story-art/the-art-of-thinking-clearly/04-darwin.jpg)

Psychologist Peter Wason showed people three numbers — **2, 4, 6** — and said: there is a hidden rule behind this sequence. Propose your own triples, and I'll tell you whether each fits. Find the rule.

Nearly everyone had the same thought: *add two each time.* And then — here is the fatal move — they tested it by proposing 8-10-12. Fits. 20-22-24. Fits. 100-102-104. Fits! Confident, they announced the rule... and were wrong. The real rule was simply *ascending numbers*. Any climbing triple fit — 1-7-49, 3-9-11. One test like 5-4-3, one attempt to *break* their own theory, would have revealed everything. Almost nobody tried to break it.

That is **confirmation bias**, and Dobelli crowns it the father of all misconceptions: the mind hunts for agreement, not truth. We collect yeses — and only a *no* ever teaches anything. Investors see only the news that blesses their stocks; two economists read the same data as proof of opposite theories; "good things come to those who wait" survives because it is too vague for any outcome to refute. And the defense comes from a great scientist's private discipline: **Charles Darwin forced himself to write down every observation that contradicted his theory within thirty minutes** — because he knew his brain would otherwise quietly delete it by dinner. The more a theory pleased him, the faster he wrote its objections down. Do that with your own darlings — your beliefs about your career, your marriage, your investments. Hunt the evidence *against*. Murder your darlings before the world does it for you, expensively.

## Ninety-eight percent sure

Now a small test researchers have run thousands of times. People are asked for ranges they are *98% certain* contain the true answer — the length of the Nile, say. Give a wide enough range and you cannot miss.

::: guess
If people were honestly calibrated, the truth would escape their "98% certain" ranges just 2% of the time. How often did it actually escape?
---
**Roughly 40% of the time** — miscalibrated by a factor of twenty. That is **overconfidence**: not a gap in ability, but a gap between what you know and what you *think* you know. It decorates every corner of life: 84% of French men rate themselves above-average lovers — a mathematical impossibility for 34% of them; majorities of drivers, students, and founders all place themselves in the top half. Two details make it truly dangerous. First, *experts suffer it more than laypeople* — the economist's five-year oil forecast is wildly overconfident, just dressed in credentials. Second, it has no mirror twin: underconfidence barely exists in the wild. And the overconfident person isn't bluffing — they sincerely believe the inflated number, which is why you cannot feel it from inside. The fix is mechanical: take every plan, every forecast — *especially your own* — and discount it. For big projects, build a pessimistic scenario **twice as harsh** as the one that feels realistic. The one that feels realistic is already the inflated one.
:::

## The button connected to nothing

In 1965, Jenkins and Ward sat people before two switches and a blinking light and asked how much their switching controlled the blinking. The light was *random* — the switches were connected to nothing. The subjects confidently reported they had influence.

Friend, the city you live in exploits this daily. Many pedestrian crossing buttons — famously in New York — are **disconnected from the signal entirely**. Many elevator "door close" buttons do nothing. Some offices install dummy thermostats so workers can "adjust" a temperature that never changes — and complaints drop, because pressing feels like power. That is the **illusion of control**, the same wiring that makes dice players throw hard for high numbers and soft for low, and lottery players prefer numbers they chose themselves — as if choosing changed the odds. Dobelli's dry suspicion: even central bankers may be flipping a very expensive placebo switch on an economy too complex to steer.

The move is triage: list what you *genuinely* influence in your biggest problem — usually a short list — and pour everything into it. For the rest — markets, weather, other people's opinions — stop flipping the disconnected switch. The light blinks as it will.

## Heads I'm a genius, tails it was bad luck

An A student credits intelligence and hard work. The same student, handed an F, blames an unfair exam. Notice: the *grade* changed, but the flattering explanation machine never did.

That is **self-serving bias**, and it wears a thousand suits. Annual reports credit brilliant management in good years and "market headwinds" in bad ones — no board ever writes *we decided poorly*. It survives even transparent randomness: in studies where scores were assigned *arbitrarily*, high scorers concluded the test was valid, low scorers that it was meaningless. And the household version is a quiet comedy: ask two spouses separately what share of the housework each does, and the answers reliably total **far above 100%** — both honestly overcounting.

Why it matters: a mind that never registers its failures never corrects them. So import honest critics — keep one blunt friend whose unflattering verdicts you treat as data, not insult. And after your next failure, write the version of events where *you* were the main cause. Then check which story the evidence supports. It will hurt precisely as much as it helps.

## The astrologers in suits

Philip Tetlock ran the largest test of expert prediction ever attempted: ten years, 284 professional experts, **82,361 forecasts** about politics and economics, all scored against reality.

::: guess
These were credentialed, confident, well-paid professionals predicting their own fields. How did they do against random chance — and what was the relationship between an expert's fame and their accuracy?
---
They performed **barely better than chance** — a simple extrapolation rule beat many of them. And fame ran *backwards*: **the more prominent the pundit, the worse the forecasts**, because media careers reward confident drama, not calibration. This is the **forecast illusion**, and Dobelli's diagnosis is double. Some things genuinely can be forecast — short-horizon, stable systems. But complex systems — markets, oil prices, wars, which technology wins — are chaotic at the root, and expertise cannot fix chaos. Worse, the incentives are broken: a forecaster pays *no price* for being wrong; the failed prediction is simply replaced by a fresh one, and nobody keeps score. Predictions are free bets — upside if right, nothing if wrong. So apply two filters to every confident forecast: *what does this person gain from my believing them?* and *what is the historical hit rate for this type of prediction?* For most complex systems it is indistinguishable from guessing — or as Galbraith said, economic forecasting exists to make astrology look respectable.
:::

## Two more flattering mirrors

Two last mirrors, quickly, before the fire burns down. The **introspection illusion**: looking inward feels like reading the truth of your own heart — but self-examination is mostly *after-the-fact storytelling*, a narrator inventing motives once the deed is done. Nothing feels more certain than your own convictions, which is exactly why we assume anyone who disagrees must be ignorant, stupid, or wicked. Treat your inner certainty with the same skepticism you'd give a stranger's claims. And the **not-invented-here syndrome**: your recipes taste better, your team's solution seems obviously superior, your idea shines — *because they are yours*. The symmetry test cuts through it in one stroke: **would you rate this idea as highly if a competitor had proposed it?** If not, the glow isn't quality. It's ownership — the endowment effect, wearing a thinking cap.

## Before you go

Six traps tonight, and one face beneath them all: a private storyteller who files your wins under *skill*, your losses under *bad luck*, your beliefs under *obvious truth*, and your guesses under *knowledge* — then assures you, warmly, that no such storyteller exists. You cannot fire him, friend. You can only audit him: write down the contradicting evidence, widen the ranges, drop the disconnected switches, keep one blunt friend, and murder a darling now and then.

But tomorrow — tomorrow we catch the storyteller at his grandest work. Not editing your opinions... editing your *past*. Why every biography reads like destiny, why the winners you admire are a graveyard's survivors talking, and why your clearest memories may be his finest forgeries.

## Key takeaways

- Confirmation bias is the father of all errors: only a "no" teaches — hunt disconfirming evidence Darwin-style, within thirty minutes.
- Overconfidence: your "98% certain" is wrong 40% of the time — discount every plan, and double the harshness of the pessimistic scenario.
- Illusion of control: many buttons are connected to nothing — triage what you truly influence and drop the ritual switch-flipping.
- Self-serving bias: the grade changes, the flattering story doesn't — write the version where you were the cause, and keep one blunt friend.
- Forecast illusion: 82,361 expert predictions barely beat chance, and fame predicted *worse* accuracy — ask about incentives and hit rates.
- Introspection and ownership both flatter: cross-examine your inner narrator, and rate your ideas as if a rival had proposed them.
