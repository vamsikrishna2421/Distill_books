# Lies You Tell Yourself

## Key Ideas
- **Confirmation bias** is the mother of all thinking errors: you filter new information so it fits your existing beliefs and quietly delete whatever contradicts them.
- You systematically overrate your own knowledge and abilities — when people give ranges they are "98% sure" contain the right answer, the truth falls outside them 40% of the time.
- You believe you influence outcomes you demonstrably cannot control, which is why placebo buttons at crosswalks and in elevators exist at all.
- When things go well you credit your talent; when they go badly you blame circumstances — and this self-serving accounting survives even hard evidence to the contrary.
- Expert forecasts of complex systems (markets, politics, oil prices) barely beat random guessing, yet forecasters pay no price for being wrong.
- Neither looking inward (introspection) nor favoring your own inventions (not-invented-here) is a reliable guide to truth; both flatter you rather than inform you.

## Your Mind Hunts for Agreement, Not Truth

**Confirmation bias** is the tendency to interpret new information so that it confirms what you already believe — and to screen out or reinterpret **disconfirming evidence**, the facts that would prove you wrong. Dobelli calls it the father of all misconceptions because it silently corrupts every other judgment you make.

Psychologist Peter Wason demonstrated it with his famous **2-4-6 task**. Subjects saw the sequence 2-4-6 and had to discover the hidden rule by proposing their own triples, receiving only "fits the rule" or "doesn't" as feedback. The actual rule was simply "ascending numbers." Almost everyone hypothesized something like "add two each time" and then tested only sequences that would *confirm* it — 8-10-12, 20-22-24 — instead of trying sequences like 1-7-49 or 5-4-3 that could *refute* it. They collected endless agreement and stayed wrong. The lesson: we test our theories by looking for yeses, when only a no can teach us anything.

Charles Darwin engineered a personal defense. Whenever an observation contradicted his theory, he forced himself to write it down within thirty minutes, because he knew his brain would otherwise "forget" the inconvenient fact within hours. He took disconfirming evidence *more* seriously the more his theory pleased him.

The bias is everywhere: investors who see only news supporting their stock picks, economists who read the same data as proof of opposite theories, believers in aphorisms like "good things come to those who wait" (vague enough that any outcome confirms them), and executives whose strategy "explains" every result after the fact. Religion, ideology, and business punditry all thrive on beliefs framed so no observation can falsify them.

The remedy is deliberate self-sabotage of your own convictions. Write down your beliefs — about your career, marriage, investments, diet — and actively hunt for evidence against each one. As the writing teacher Arthur Quiller-Couch advised authors about their favorite sentences: "Murder your darlings."

## You Are Sure — and Wrong — More Often Than You Think

**Overconfidence** is not about ability; it is about the gap between what you actually know and what you *think* you know. In classic calibration studies, people were asked for ranges they were 98% certain contained the true answer — the length of the Nile, OPEC's annual output, and so on. If people were honestly calibrated, only 2% of true answers would fall outside their ranges. In reality, roughly **40%** did. We are miscalibrated by a factor of twenty.

The effect shows up wherever self-assessment is possible. **84% of French men** rate themselves above-average lovers — statistically, only 50% can be. Surveys find similar majorities of drivers, students, and entrepreneurs placing themselves in the top half. Startup founders rate their own venture's chance of success far above the base rate for their industry, which helps explain why so many businesses launch into markets that cannot support them.

Two details make overconfidence especially dangerous. First, experts suffer from it *more* than laypeople, not less — an economist's estimate of oil prices in five years is wildly overconfident, just dressed in credentials. Second, it is not the mirror image of underconfidence: the reverse effect barely exists. Men are more affected than women, and optimists more than pessimists, but essentially everyone overrates their knowledge.

Note the distinction from deliberate bluffing: the overconfident person genuinely believes their inflated estimate. That sincerity is what makes the error so hard to spot from inside. The practical fix is mechanical, not psychological: take every plan and every forecast — especially your own — and discount it. For big projects, assume a pessimistic scenario twice as harsh as the one that feels realistic, because the felt-realistic one is already inflated.

## Pressing Buttons That Aren't Connected to Anything

The **illusion of control** is the belief that you can influence outcomes over which you objectively have no power. In a classic 1965 experiment, **Herbert Jenkins and William Ward** gave subjects two switches and a light that blinked on and off. The light's pattern was random — the switches did nothing — yet subjects confidently reported that their switching influenced it. Even when told the connection might be zero, people flipping switches felt in charge.

The same wiring explains everyday superstition. Casino players throw dice hard when they want high numbers and softly when they want low ones. Lottery players prefer numbers they picked themselves, as if choice changed the odds. Sports fans gesture at the television as though body English could bend a ball's flight.

Cities and engineers exploit the bias deliberately with **placebo buttons**. Many pedestrian crosswalk buttons (famously in New York) are disconnected from the signal timing; "door close" buttons in many elevators do nothing; some open-plan offices install dummy thermostats so workers can "adjust" a temperature that never changes. Complaints drop because people feel in control, even though nothing they press has any effect. Dobelli suggests even central bankers moving interest rates may be pressing a version of the placebo button on an economy too complex to steer.

The debiasing move is triage: identify the few things you can genuinely influence, and work on those relentlessly. For everything else — markets, weather, other people's opinions, the world economy — accept that outcomes will unfold as they will, and stop wasting effort on ritual switch-flipping.

## Heads I'm a Genius, Tails It Was Bad Luck

The **self-serving bias** is the habit of attributing successes to your own skill and failures to external factors. Students who get an A credit their intelligence and hard work; the same students who get an F blame an unfair exam or a bad teacher. The grade changes; the flattering explanation machine does not.

Corporate life runs on the same asymmetry. Read a few annual reports: good years are explained by brilliant management and strategic foresight, while bad years are the fault of exchange rates, the economy, or "market headwinds." CEOs and boards rarely write "we made poor decisions." Stock analysts and fund managers do the same with their calls.

The bias survives even when the evidence is transparently random. In studies where test scores were assigned arbitrarily, people who received high scores still concluded the test was valid and their performance genuine, while low scorers dismissed the test as meaningless. And in a telling household experiment, when spouses are separately asked what percentage of the housework they do, the two answers reliably total well over 100% — each partner honestly overcounts their own contribution.

Why does this matter? Because a mind that never registers its failures never corrects them. The antidote is to import honest critics: keep a friend or colleague who will tell you unflattering truths and treat their verdict as data, not insult. Better yet, when something goes wrong, force yourself to write down the version of events in which *you* were the main cause — then check which story the evidence actually supports.

## The Prediction Industry Is Selling Coin Flips

The **forecast illusion** is our misplaced faith in expert predictions about complex systems. Psychologist **Philip Tetlock** ran the definitive test: over ten years he collected **82,361 predictions** from 284 professional experts on political and economic events. The result was devastating — the experts performed scarcely better than random chance, and a simple extrapolation rule beat many of them. Worse, there was an inverse relationship between fame and accuracy: the more prominent the pundit, the poorer the forecasts, because media careers reward confident, dramatic claims rather than calibrated ones.

Dobelli's diagnosis has two parts. First, some things are genuinely predictable — short-horizon, stable systems like tomorrow's sunrise or roughly how much weight a diet will shed — while complex systems (stock markets, oil prices, exchange rates, wars, technology winners) are inherently chaotic and resist forecasting no matter how much expertise is applied. Second, the incentive structure is broken: forecasters pay **no price for being wrong**. A pundit who predicted a crash that never came simply issues a new prediction; nobody keeps score. Predictions are free options — upside if right, no downside if wrong.

His proposed reform is a public scorecard: track every forecaster's hit rate the way we track athletes' statistics, and listen only to those with verified records. Until that exists, apply two filters to any prediction you hear. Ask what incentive the forecaster has — a banker predicting rates, a CEO predicting her industry, a journalist predicting elections all profit from attention, not accuracy. And ask what the historical hit rate is for that *type* of prediction; for most complex systems, it is indistinguishable from guessing. John Kenneth Galbraith's line about economic forecasters — that they exist to make astrology look respectable — captures the appropriate level of trust.

## Two More Flattering Mirrors

The **introspection illusion** is the belief that looking inward gives you reliable knowledge of your own motives and beliefs. It doesn't: internal reflection is largely after-the-fact storytelling, and nothing feels more convincing than your own convictions — which is why we assume anyone who disagrees must be ignorant, stupid, or malicious. Since your self-reports are constructed rather than observed, treat inner certainty with the same skepticism you would apply to a stranger's claims, and be your own toughest cross-examiner.

The **not-invented-here syndrome** (NIH) is the tendency to overvalue ideas simply because they are yours — your own recipes taste better, your team's solution seems obviously superior, and companies routinely reject external innovations that outperform their in-house ones. The test is symmetry: would you rate this idea as highly if a competitor had proposed it? Periodically borrow outside solutions deliberately, and let others judge your ideas' quality — from a distance, yours are rarely as exceptional as they feel.

## In Practice

- Write down your core beliefs about work, money, health, and relationships, and spend one session this week actively searching for evidence *against* each — Darwin-style, recording contradictions before your brain deletes them.
- Before your next estimate or plan, widen your uncertainty range dramatically and build in a pessimistic scenario about twice as harsh as the one that feels realistic.
- List what you genuinely control in your current biggest problem; act only on that list and consciously drop the ritual effort spent on everything else.
- After your next success *and* your next failure, write both explanations — "it was me" and "it was circumstances" — and ask a blunt friend which one the evidence supports.
- Ignore any forecast about markets, prices, or politics unless the forecaster has a tracked, verifiable hit rate; always ask what they gain from your believing them.
- When evaluating an idea you produced, ask whether you would rate it as highly if a rival had proposed it — and once this month, deliberately adopt an outside solution instead of building your own.
