# You Get What You Expect: Expectations, Price, and the Placebo Effect

Seventh night, friend — and tonight Ariely comes for the last thing you trust completely. Not your wallet. Not your willpower. Your *senses*. The taste on your tongue, the pain in your knee, the sharpness of your own mind — surely those, at least, report the truth?

Pull up a stool. We begin in a student pub at MIT, where a bartender is quietly dropping vinegar into the beer.

## Vinegar in the beer

![A cozy pub counter with two identical glasses of beer under warm light, an eyedropper of dark liquid poised over one](/story-art/predictably-irrational/07-beer.jpg)

At the Muddy Charles pub, Ariely's team offered free samples of two brews: ordinary Budweiser, and the mysterious **"MIT Brew"** — which was the *same* Budweiser laced with a few drops of balsamic vinegar. When tasters knew nothing, a funny thing happened: **most preferred the vinegar beer.**

Then came the real experiment — not about vinegar, but about *when you learn about it*.

::: guess
One group was told about the vinegar BEFORE sipping. Another was told AFTER sipping — then even offered vinegar to add themselves. Same beer, same fact. How differently did the two groups judge it?
---
The knew-before group wrinkled their noses and overwhelmingly rejected the MIT Brew. The told-after group **liked it just as much as the blind tasters** — and happily added more vinegar themselves. Same beer; same information; *only the timing differed* — and that is the night's foundation stone: expectations are not a reporting bias you apply afterward. **Knowledge delivered in advance becomes part of the sensory experience itself.** The knew-before group genuinely tasted worse beer; once the tongue has ruled, the same fact is nearly powerless to revise the verdict. So describe the dish with evocative provenance and diners *taste more*; reveal the offbeat ingredient after the plates are clean and you lose nothing. And for high-stakes judgment, the discipline writes itself: when you must evaluate fairly — a wine, a résumé, a proposal — **strip the labels first**, because "I'll just ignore the brand" is a promise no brain can keep. The mechanism runs both ways, friend: expect the movie to be wonderful and your pleasure is *genuinely* greater. Expectations are self-fulfilling prophecies operating at the level of perception itself.
:::

And the packaging works on the brain like the words do. The same team ran a free coffee stand where odd condiments — cloves, orange peel, paprika — sat either in Styrofoam cups with handwritten labels... or in elegant glass on a brushed-metal tray. *Nobody touched the condiments either way.* But with the fancy tray nearby, drinkers rated the identical coffee as significantly better and said they'd pay more. Ambience leaked into flavor. And the industrial version sits in every fridge: in blind tests **Pepsi beats Coke**; with labels showing, **Coke wins** — and neuroscientist Read Montague found the mechanism in an fMRI scanner. Blind sips of either cola light the brain's basic reward center equally. But *knowing it's Coke* additionally ignites the DLPFC — the region of memory and association — and reported pleasure rises. Decades of advertising built neural machinery that adds real enjoyment the sugar water can't. The brand's advantage isn't a lie told to surveys, friend. **It's measurable brain activity.** Even performance obeys: Asian-American women subtly reminded of their ethnicity outperformed on a math test; the same women reminded of their gender underperformed — stereotypes act on the people they describe. Prime yourself deliberately before anything that matters.

## The $2.50 painkiller

Now the boldest question: can a *price tag* reach into your body? Ariely's team invented **Veladone-Rx** — a majestic-sounding painkiller with a slick brochure, which was in reality a vitamin C capsule. Volunteers took calibrated electric shocks, swallowed the pill, and were shocked again.

::: guess
For one group the brochure priced Veladone at $2.50 per dose. For another, a discount flyer cut it to 10 cents. Same sugar pill, same shocks. What happened to the pain relief?
---
At $2.50, **nearly all** participants reported meaningful relief. At 10 cents... **only about half.** The price tag alone roughly halved a placebo's power — and the discount hurt most among people who had recently lived with real pain, exactly those with the most at stake. This is the **placebo effect** wearing a price sticker, and it runs on two engines: *belief* — confidence in the drug, the doctor, the ritual — and *conditioning* — the body has learned to release its own internal pharmacy, endorphins and opiate-like compounds, in response to cues that predicted relief before. Most of medical history ran on this engine; powdered mummy and bloodletting "worked" because patients expected them to. Price is simply the modern cue: expensive implies potent — so expensive partially *is* potent. And the corollary should genuinely unsettle you, friend: **discounts degrade real outcomes.** The patient steered to the marked-down generic may extract measurably less relief from chemically identical molecules — not from gullibility, but because the brain's self-treatment system answers the signal the price sends.
:::

The effect reaches cognition too: students who drank a full-priced "mental acuity" energy drink solved word puzzles as expected — students who bought the *identical* can at a discount solved roughly **30% fewer**. And then, the most dramatic demonstration on record: Houston surgeon J. Bruce Moseley ran a controlled trial of arthroscopic knee surgery — some patients got the real operation; others got **sham surgery**: anesthesia, three superficial cuts, a theatrical splash of saline, forty simulated minutes. Over two years, the sham group reported *as much pain relief and walked as well* as the genuinely operated. A procedure performed hundreds of thousands of times a year was working, apparently, at the strength of the *expectation of surgery* — and the grander the ritual, the stronger the spell.

## The ethics of belief

So what do we do with a force like this? Ariely refuses the easy answer of stamping it out — because if a sugar pill or a confident bedside manner triggers *real* endorphin release and *real* recovery, the benefit is not fake; only the mechanism is misdescribed. Physicians already use it quietly. Ban it outright and you discard genuine healing; announce it too loudly and you may kill the belief that powers it.

Two dilemmas remain genuinely hard. The **generic-drug problem**: if paying less measurably reduces relief, steering the poorest patients to the cheapest option may cost them real therapeutic value despite identical chemistry — the fix is not higher prices but *managed expectations*: frame generics as fully potent, and never dwell on the discount at the moment of swallowing. And the **testing problem**: Moseley's trial proves some accepted surgeries are pure ritual — but discovering which ones requires sham operations. Ariely argues the discomfort is worth it: weigh a few trial participants against millions undergoing expensive, risky theater because we declined to ask.

For you, friend, the lever cuts both ways — so hold it knowingly. You *can* be exploited: marketers know glossier trappings and higher prices will make you experience more benefit, and price accordingly. But you can also **self-deal**: the nice glassware genuinely improves the wine; the full-priced version of what matters genuinely works better; the confident doctor genuinely heals more. Buying extra efficacy from your own brain is legitimate. The only mistake is pretending you're not holding the lever.

## Before you go

So: vinegar ruins beer only when announced in advance; a metal tray improves coffee; Coke wins in the brain, not the mouth; a price tag halves a painkiller; and three fake incisions matched real surgery for two years. Your senses never report the world raw, friend — they serve it seasoned with everything you believed walking in. Blind the labels when you must judge; polish the trappings when you want joy.

One night remains — and Ariely saves his most personal question for last. Not how we misjudge prices or tastes... but why *good people cheat* — just a little. Why an honor code stops dishonesty that locks can't. Why we'll steal a can of Coke from the office fridge but never the dollar it costs. ## Key takeaways

- Expectations physically change experience: vinegar disclosed before ruins the beer; disclosed after, it doesn't — timing is everything.
- Blind the labels for fair judgment ("I'll ignore the brand" is impossible); reveal odd ingredients after the tasting.
- Brands add real pleasure via real brain machinery — and trappings work at near-zero cost: upgrade the glassware you drink from.
- Price is a placebo: $2.50 relieved pain twice as often as the 10-cent identical pill, and discounts dragged puzzle scores down 30%.
- Sham knee surgery matched real surgery for two years — the grander the ritual, the stronger the spell.
- Hold the lever knowingly: never file your medicine as "the cheap knockoff," prime yourself with competence, and buy efficacy from your own brain when it matters.
