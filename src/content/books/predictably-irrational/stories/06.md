# The High Price of Ownership and the Doors We Keep Open

Sixth night, friend — and it begins in a tent city on a university lawn.

At Duke, basketball tickets are so precious that students camp for *weeks* — tents, sleeping bags, shifts through the cold — just to enter a **lottery** for Final Four seats. Everyone in that line wants a ticket equally desperately. Everyone suffers equally. And then pure chance divides them: some win tickets... most don't.

Ariely and Ziv Carmon saw what that lottery had accidentally built: a perfect experiment. Two groups of *identical* students — same devotion, same weeks in tents — separated only by a coin flip. So the researchers picked up the phone and posed as ticket scalpers.

## Fourteen to one

![A tent city of students camped on a university lawn at dawn, one student holding up a golden ticket while others look on](/story-art/predictably-irrational/06-tickets.jpg)

::: guess
The scalpers asked the losers: what would you pay for a ticket? And asked the winners: what would you sell yours for? Same game, same seats, interchangeable students hours earlier. How far apart were the numbers?
---
Losers would pay about **$170** — they spoke of the money in terms of beer, books, a chunk of the semester's budget. Winners refused to sell below an average of **$2,400** — they spoke of a once-in-a-lifetime memory that would define their college years. **Fourteen to one** — and not a single mutually acceptable price existed anywhere between the two groups. Remember: hours earlier, these were the *same people in the same line*. Ownership itself — not preference, not information, not even the ticket's beauty — carved the chasm. That is the **endowment effect** in its purest laboratory form, and it runs your life daily: home sellers price in their memories along with the drywall; used-car sellers see road trips where buyers see rust; and if you've ever been baffled that nobody will pay "what it's worth" for something of yours — this is why. What it's worth *changed the moment it became yours.*
:::

Three quirks drive the spell. **We fall in love with what we have** — possessions become vessels of memory and identity, and no buyer shares your history or will pay for it. **We fixate on the loss, not the gain** — loss aversion again: selling registers as *losing the experience*, and anticipated loss demands enormous compensation. **We assume the other side sees it our way** — the seller expects the buyer to feel the sentiment; the buyer expects the seller to see the scratches; both leave convinced the other is unreasonable. And two amplifiers, friend, that should sound familiar from other nights: **effort inflates ownership** — build it, plant it, assemble it yourself and your asking price climbs regardless of merit. And ownership extends to **ideas** — once you "own" an opinion about politics, strategy, or code, you defend it with the same loss-averse ferocity. That last one, Ariely warns, is the costliest endowment of all.

## Owners before the receipt

Because ownership warps valuation, sellers labor to make you an owner *before any money moves* — **virtual ownership**, and its channels are all around you.

**Trial periods**: the premium package cheap for three months, the software free for thirty days. The rational plan is enjoy-then-downgrade — but once the trial starts, you own the upgrade in your mind, and canceling registers as *loss*. The temporary state quietly becomes permanent, at full price. **Money-back guarantees**: they exist to get the thing into your living room, because once it sits there, returning it feels like giving something up — and almost nobody does. **Auctions**: the longer you're the high bidder, the more the item feels already yours — so when a rival outbids you in the final minutes, you're no longer weighing a purchase; *you're defending a possession*, which is how bidding wars end far above anyone's original limit. Even **advertising** runs the channel: picture yourself in the car, on the beach, wearing the watch — imagined ownership triggers real attachment, and attachment triggers loss aversion at the moment of decision.

The defense is a single mental stance: **approach every transaction as a nonowner.** Would you buy your house today at your asking price? Would you sign up for the gold package fresh, at full cost? Set your maximum bid *before* the auction and quit the moment it's passed. And treat every free trial as a purchase decision made in advance — because psychologically, friend, it was never free.

## The doors that eat your clicks

Now ownership's stranger cousin. Ariely and Jiwoong Shin gave MIT students a simple game: 100 clicks, three doors on screen. Enter a door, click inside, earn small cash per click — rooms paying differently. The winning strategy is obvious: find the best room and *stay*.

Then the twist: any door left unvisited for 12 clicks **shrank and vanished forever.**

::: guess
Nothing about the payouts changed — the best room still paid best, and staying put still maximized earnings. What did players do when the doors started shrinking?
---
They **panicked** — frantically hopping between rooms, spending precious clicks just to keep all three doors alive, sacrificing about **15% of their earnings** for options they mostly never used. And here is what makes it pure irrationality: the behavior persisted even when players were *told every room's exact payout* (so the spare doors held zero information)... and even when a vanished door could be **resurrected for a single click** — meaning nothing was ever truly lost. The mere *sight of an option dying* was intolerable. Now look up from the game, friend, at the expensive versions: the dying relationship kept on life support while better ones wait; the children shuttled through every sport and instrument so no "potential" closes; the gadgets, memberships, side projects, and career maybes we pay maintenance on — in time and attention, the scarcest currencies we hold — for doors we will never walk through. And the cruelest line in the chapter: running between doors is itself a choice, and it usually forecloses the biggest door of all — **doing one thing well.**
:::

## Burn the boats

In 210 BC, the Chinese general Xiang Yu led his army across the Yangtze — then ordered the boats **burned** and the cooking pots smashed. Retreat impossible; even lingering impossible. His soldiers had exactly one open door: *forward*. They won nine consecutive battles.

What looks like recklessness was a commitment device — by destroying options, he concentrated every ounce of his army's energy where it mattered. And it is the chapter's prescription, because it is the exact opposite of our instinct. Options feel like assets; in truth, every open door charges rent — the committee you keep attending, the skill you keep half-maintaining, the ambiguous almost-relationship, the backup career you'll never pursue. So run the inventory, friend: list every door you're paying to keep ajar... and formally close two you know, honestly, you will never walk through. Resign. End it. Stop the cards. And when two remaining options are genuinely close, impose a deadline and *pick either* — past a certain point, deliberating costs more than the difference between the choices; the donkey starves between two equal bales.

The two biases of tonight are one bias, seen twice: the ticket-holder who won't sell and the player who won't let a door die are both overvaluing *what they currently hold* — a possession, an option, an opinion — because letting go registers as loss. The pain of closing is real, friend. It is also predictable, overstated, and temporary. Decide what deserves your full commitment. Burn the boats behind it. Let the other doors click softly shut — and take back your clicks.

## Before you go

So: chance owners demanded fourteen times what chance non-owners would pay; trials, guarantees, and auctions make you an owner before the receipt; open doors eat 15% of your winnings; and a general who burned his boats won nine straight battles. Price everything as a nonowner. Close the doors yourself, before they close you.

## Key takeaways

- The endowment effect: chance owners wanted $2,400 for tickets chance non-owners valued at $170 — ownership itself creates the gap.
- Three quirks: we love our history with things, we feel the sale as a loss, and we assume the other side sees it our way.
- Virtual ownership arrives before the receipt: trials, guarantees, high-bidder status, and vivid ads — decide as a nonowner, with limits set in advance.
- The door game: players burned 15% of earnings keeping worthless options alive — option-maintenance is a real cost paid in time and attention.
- You own opinions too — and effort-built ideas carry the strongest attachment; argue the opposing case in writing first.
- Burn the boats: inventory your open doors, close two you'll never use, and give the saved clicks to the one door that matters.
