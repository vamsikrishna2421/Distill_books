# The Cost of Zero Cost: Why FREE! Makes Us Irrational

Third night, friend — and tonight's law hides in the most beautiful word in any language of commerce. Not *cheap*. Not *discount*. The magic syllable: **FREE.**

You think you understand it. You think free is just the lowest possible price — the far end of the same dial. Tonight Ariely proves it is nothing of the sort. Zero is not a price. Zero is a *spell* — and he cast it with two chocolates on a table.

## One cent that flipped a crowd

![A small table with two bowls of chocolates, elegant truffles beside foil-wrapped kisses, a crowd of hands reaching](/story-art/predictably-irrational/03-chocolate.jpg)

Ariely's team set up a chocolate stand offering exactly two choices, one per customer. A **Lindt truffle** — genuinely luxurious Swiss chocolate — for 15 cents, roughly half its real cost. And an ordinary **Hershey's Kiss** for 1 cent.

Faced with this, people behaved like sober economists: **73% took the truffle** — a spectacular bargain — and 27% the Kiss. Sensible crowd.

::: guess
Then the researchers lowered each price by exactly ONE cent: truffle 14 cents, Kiss... FREE. The truffle's price advantage stayed identical — 14 cents apart, before and after. What happened to the choices?
---
**The crowd flipped: 69% now grabbed the free Kiss**, abandoning the objectively better truffle deal. One cent — the *same* one cent off both — reversed the majority. And when the team wondered whether coin-fumbling was the real cause, they reran it in a cafeteria checkout line where the price just rode the lunch bill: same reversal. Zero itself was doing the work. Here's the companion puzzle to feel it in your own chest: choose between a **free $10 Amazon gift card**... or a **$20 card for $7**. Most people snatch the free ten — though the priced option nets $13 against $10, strictly better. Going from 2 cents to 1 cent barely moves a market; going from 1 cent to *zero* detonates it. Standard economics treats price as a continuous dial. Your brain treats FREE! as a categorically different state — a hot button that switches off arithmetic.
:::

Why? **Loss aversion**, wearing a party hat. Every purchase, however small, opens a door to regret — pay 14 cents and maybe the truffle disappoints, maybe you overpaid. But a free thing *seems* to have no possible downside — "no chance of loss" gets misread as "high value." The perception is false, of course: we queue an hour for a free cone worth minutes, haul home pens and keychains we'll never touch, overeat at buffets because each helping is "free." And note the effect's true danger, hiding in the truffle data: FREE! doesn't just make us take worthless things. **It makes us pass up valuable ones.** Beside a free option, every priced alternative competes against an unearned psychological subsidy — and the comparison is never fair again.

## The franc that broke the spell

Businesses knew the spell before science measured it — and two corporate episodes mark its exact boundaries.

When **Amazon** introduced free shipping above a threshold, sales jumped sharply in every country — customers gladly tossed a half-wanted second book into the cart to escape the shipping charge. Every country... **except France**.

::: guess
French sales stayed completely flat while the rest of the world surged. The culprit was not French rationality. What had the French division done differently?
---
They had priced shipping not at zero but at **1 franc** — about 20 cents. *Nearly* free. And nearly free, friend, is just another cheap price: it triggers ordinary cost-benefit thinking, and ordinary thinking said *no thanks*. When France later moved to genuinely free shipping, its sales surged like everyone else's. The gap between one franc and zero francs — economically trivial — was behaviorally enormous, and it maps the spell's edge precisely: **only exactly zero flips the switch.** The second episode shows the spell's dark side: in 1996, AOL moved from hourly billing to a flat monthly rate — making each additional online hour feel free. Executives forecast a modest bump and added a little capacity. Demand *exploded overnight* — subscribers who had rationed hours now never logged off; the network jammed; customers nationwide got busy signals for weeks while AOL begged capacity from competitors. They had planned for a price cut. They had actually deployed FREE! — and free obeys different laws. If you ever forecast anything, never model zero as merely a very low price. You will be wrong, sometimes ruinously.
:::

## Casting the spell for good

Here is the hopeful turn: a button this powerful can be pressed for good. If society wants people to do something beneficial, make it **free** — not cheap, *free*. A $10 copay on a cholesterol screening looks negligible on paper, but it drags the decision into ordinary cost-benefit territory — where inertia and unpleasantness win, and people skip the test. Drop the copay to exactly zero, and participation leaps far beyond what ten dollars explains — with enormous downstream savings from disease caught early. Same for green incentives: literally-free registration for electric cars moves more drivers than an equivalent rebate, because FREE! recruits the emotional brain to the cause. Policymakers who insist on token charges "so people have skin in the game" are unknowingly forfeiting most of the behavioral leverage.

And for your own defense, one elegant trick — the **one-cent test**: when a free option sits beside a paid one, mentally add a single cent to the free thing... and see if your preference survives. Would you still take the Kiss at one cent? Still add the extra book if shipping were $3.90 regardless? *If the answer flips, the zero was doing the deciding* — not the value. Then price everything at net worth: what you get minus what you give — in time, clutter, calories, attention, forgone alternatives. Free is sometimes genuinely the best deal, friend. But you can't know until you run the comparison zero was designed to stop you from running. **FREE! is a source of excitement, not information — and excitement should be priced at what it's worth: nothing.**

## Key takeaways

- Zero is not a price — it's a categorical switch: 2¢→1¢ barely moves demand; 1¢→0 detonates it.
- FREE! exploits loss aversion: "no chance of loss" gets misread as "high value" — and pulls you off better deals.
- Only exactly zero casts the spell: Amazon's 1-franc France stayed flat; genuine free surged; AOL's "free hours" melted the network.
- Never forecast zero as a low price — free changes how people consume, not just how much.
- Use it for good: exactly-free screenings and incentives move people far beyond what the waived dollars explain.
- The one-cent test: add a mental cent to any free option — if your preference flips, the zero was deciding, not the value.
