# Everything Is Relative: How Comparisons Steer Your Choices

A new fire tonight, friend — and a new guide. Dan Ariely was a young man recovering from terrible burns in an Israeli hospital when he began noticing something strange: the nurses, kind and expert, made systematically wrong decisions about his pain — the *same* wrong decisions, every time. Not random mistakes. *Patterned* ones. He spent the rest of his life chasing that pattern, and his discovery names the whole book: we are irrational — but **predictably** so. Our mistakes follow laws.

Tonight, the first law — and it hides in a magazine subscription page that should have made no sense at all.

## The option nobody wanted

![Three subscription boxes on a magazine page, a hand hovering between them under warm lamplight](/story-art/predictably-irrational/01-decoy.jpg)

Ariely was browsing the Economist's website when he saw a curious offer. Web-only subscription: **$59**. Print-only: **$125**. Print *plus* web: **also $125**.

Read that again, friend. Print-only and print-plus-web cost *exactly the same*. Nobody on earth should choose print alone. So why print it at all? Ariely suspected the "useless" option wasn't useless — it was doing the most important work on the page. He took the ad to a hundred of his MIT students.

::: guess
With all three options, 84% of students chose the expensive $125 combo. Then Ariely deleted the useless print-only option — the one nobody had chosen anyway — and asked a fresh hundred students. What happened?
---
**The majority flipped.** With the "useless" option gone, only 32% took the combo — 68% now chose the cheap web-only deal. An option *nobody wanted* had moved most of the crowd toward the expensive package. That is the **decoy effect**. Here's the machinery: humans have no internal meter for absolute worth — we don't know what a subscription, a car, or a bottle of wine is "really" worth. We judge by *comparison*, and only comparisons that are *easy*. Print-plus-web versus web-only is hard to compare. But print-plus-web versus print-only? Trivially easy — same price, strictly more stuff! That one easy comparison feels like a verdict, and the combo wins the whole contest — though nothing about the real options changed. The realtor plays it with houses: two similar colonials, one with a bad roof, beside a hard-to-compare modern — you'll buy the intact colonial, because it's the one you can confidently judge *better than something*. Watch for the pattern everywhere: three options, one clearly dominated. Then ask the question the seller hopes you won't: **what would I choose if only the two real options existed?**
:::

## The bread machine that sold its little brother

Williams-Sonoma once launched a home bread machine at $275 — and almost nobody bought it. Shoppers had no reference point: is $275 a lot for a bread machine? Do I even need one? The product sat.

Then a marketing consultant suggested something devious: add a *second*, deluxe bread machine — bigger, about 50% more expensive. The deluxe barely sold. **Sales of the original took off.** The expensive model was never really a product, friend — it was a *comparison*, installed beside the original to make $275 look sensible. Restaurants run the identical trick: that eye-watering entrée at the top of the menu exists mostly so the second-most-expensive dish feels like your own prudent idea — and that's the dish that pays the rent.

And now the mischievous part. Ariely wondered: does this work on *people*? He photographed students, paired faces of similar attractiveness — call them Tom and Jerry — and used Photoshop to make a slightly distorted, less handsome twin of one. Then he handed out sheets with three photos: Tom, Jerry, and one "ugly" twin, asking who they'd rather date. Whichever normal face had its distorted double on the sheet was chosen **about 75% of the time**. Jerry beside ugly-Jerry looks like a catch; the same Jerry beside ugly-Tom, unremarkable. The comic tactical tip writes itself — bring a slightly-less-dazzling version of yourself to the party. The sobering one matters more: *whoever assembles the lineup you see* — marketer, broker, menu designer — can quietly install a decoy and tilt your "free" choice before you enter the room.

## The $7 that changes size

Now watch relativity bend money itself. Suppose a pen costs $25 at your local shop, and a 15-minute drive away it's $18. Most people make the drive — seven dollars is seven dollars! Now suppose a suit costs $455 nearby and $448 across town.

Same 15 minutes. Same $7. **Almost nobody drives.** Because the mind computes *percentages*, not dollars — a big saving on a pen, a rounding error on a suit — when the only rational question is: *is 15 minutes of my life worth $7, full stop?*

The same anesthesia works on add-ons. Paying $5,000 for a wedding buffet, a $200 soup upgrade feels like nothing — though you would never, ever pay $200 for soup on its own. Big purchases numb us to amounts that are relatively small and absolutely large: the $2,000 upgrade on a $25,000 car, the "small" accessories riding on a home purchase. The defense is conversion: **evaluate every add-on as a standalone purchase.** Would I pay $200, by itself, for this soup? And Ariely adds one more escape at the category level: shopping for a car after years on a motorcycle, he found himself expertly comparing sedan versus sedan — never asking the *harder*, truer question: car versus motorcycle versus something else entirely. We optimize within whatever comparison set we're handed. Step back once, friend, and check who handed it to you.

## The salary that turned poor

Now relativity's heaviest toll — not on your wallet, on your *contentment*. The old joke says a man is satisfied with his salary if he earns more than his wife's sister's husband. Research says the joke is a law: happiness with income tracks *position among visible peers* far more than purchasing power. Ariely met young bankers earning hundreds of thousands who felt genuinely poor — the officemate earned more.

::: guess
In 1993, U.S. regulators forced public companies to disclose CEO pay, expecting the transparency to shame runaway compensation downward. CEO pay stood at about 131 times the average worker's. What did disclosure actually do?
---
**Pay exploded — to roughly 369 times the average worker's.** Disclosure handed every CEO a public leaderboard, and each one now had a *visible benchmark to beat*; comparison, once illuminated, became an engine of escalation. And your life runs the same ratchet, friend: media and mobility keep *enlarging the comparison circle* — you no longer measure your house against the neighbors' but against the ones on your screen; the raise thrills until you learn a colleague got more; each new car or kitchen resets the baseline so yesterday's luxury becomes today's floor. This is why more income so reliably fails to buy more contentment: **the reference point travels with you.** The people who feel rich are the big fish in small circles — never simply the biggest numbers. Wanting more is rarely a fact about what you have. It is a fact about what you've recently *seen*.
:::

## Choosing your own comparisons

So here is the first law's escape hatch, and it is surprisingly hopeful: comparison is automatic — you cannot stop it — but the comparison *set* is controllable. Own it.

**Break the circles that make you miserable.** If a glossier feed, richer friends, or a neighborhood fantasy only raise your baseline, shrink your exposure — sometimes the cheapest cure for wanting more is *seeing less*. **Widen the frames sellers have narrowed**: delete the decoy and re-decide; convert every relative judgment back to absolute dollars and hours. And **decide your numbers in advance** — what salary, what house, what enough actually looks like for your life — so that a colleague's figure or a menu's top line cannot redefine your satisfaction after the fact. Relativity will never stop steering, friend. The only question is whether the hands on the wheel are yours.

## Before you go

So: you have no meter for absolute worth, only comparisons; decoys herd you through easy contrasts; percentages disguise identical dollars; and the comparison circle — not the bank balance — decides how rich you feel. First law of predictable irrationality: **whoever controls the lineup controls the choice.**

But tomorrow's law is stranger still. Because sometimes there is no lineup at all — no comparison anywhere in sight — and your mind still needs a number. Where does it get one? Tomorrow: why students bid real money on wine based on *the last two digits of their own social security numbers* — and why the first price you ever paid for anything may still be silently pricing your life today.

Next time: **anchors — the arbitrary numbers that rule you.**

## Key takeaways

- No internal meter: value is always judged by comparison, and easy comparisons beat true ones.
- The decoy effect: an option nobody chooses can flip the majority — find the dominated option, delete it, re-decide.
- Evaluate add-ons standalone: $200 is $200, whether beside soup or beside a wedding.
- Dollars, not percentages: 15 minutes for $7 is the same deal on a pen as on a suit.
- Comparison circles set your contentment: CEO pay disclosure tripled the ratchet — shrink the circles that make you feel behind.
- You can't stop comparing, but you choose the set: curate your comparisons instead of inheriting them.
