# You Get What You Expect: Expectations, Price, and the Placebo Effect

## Key Ideas
- Expectations don't just bias how we describe an experience — they physically change the experience itself, from the taste of beer to activity in the brain's pleasure centers.
- Timing is everything: information received before an experience reshapes perception, while the same information received afterward barely dents it.
- Priming works even when it's invisible — stereotypes, brand logos, and fancy presentation all shift performance and enjoyment without our awareness.
- Price acts as a placebo: identical painkillers, energy drinks, and even surgeries deliver more real benefit when we believe they cost more.
- Discounts carry a hidden tax — paying less can literally make a product work less well, which poses a genuine problem for generic drugs and cost-cutting in medicine.
- Because beliefs shape outcomes, honest actors face an ethical dilemma: a well-managed expectation is a real therapy, but it often depends on a small deception.

## Vinegar in the Beer: Expectations Rewrite the Experience Itself

Ariely, Leonard Lee, and Shane Frederick ran a taste test at the Muddy Charles, an MIT pub. Patrons sampled two free brews: ordinary Budweiser and the **"MIT Brew"** — the same Budweiser laced with a few drops of balsamic vinegar. When tasters knew nothing about the secret ingredient, most preferred the vinegar-spiked beer.

The twist came with disclosure. Patrons told about the vinegar *before* sipping wrinkled their noses and overwhelmingly chose the plain Budweiser. But patrons told about the vinegar *after* tasting — and then offered vinegar to add themselves — liked the MIT Brew just as much as the blind tasters had. Same beer, same information; only the timing differed.

The lesson is that expectations are not a post-hoc reporting bias. Knowledge delivered in advance becomes part of the sensory experience: the "knew-before" group genuinely tasted worse beer. Once the tongue has rendered its verdict, the same fact is almost powerless to revise it.

This explains why presentation matters so much in daily life. Describe a dish with evocative language and exotic provenance, and diners will taste more; mention the offbeat ingredient only after the plates are clean, and you lose nothing. It also suggests a discipline for high-stakes judgment: when you must evaluate something fairly — a wine, a job candidate, a policy proposal — strip away the labels first. Expectations, once installed, do their work below the level of conscious control, which is why "I'll just ignore the brand" is a promise nobody can keep.

The same mechanism runs both directions. Expect a movie to be great because critics raved, and you will enjoy it more — the pleasure is real, not imagined. Expectations are self-fulfilling prophecies operating at the level of perception itself.

## Trappings, Brands, and the Brain: Coke, Coffee, and fMRI

If expectations change taste, the *packaging* of an experience should change it too. Ariely's team set up a free coffee stand at MIT and offered unusual condiments — cloves, orange peel, sweet paprika — alongside the ordinary sugar and milk. Nobody touched the strange add-ins either way. But when the condiments sat in elegant glass-and-metal containers on a brushed-metal tray, drinkers rated the coffee itself as significantly better, said they'd pay more for it, and were more likely to want it served in the cafeteria than when the identical condiments sat in Styrofoam cups with handwritten labels. Ambience leaked into flavor.

Brands run the same trick at industrial scale. In blind taste tests, Pepsi reliably beats Coke — the basis of the old "Pepsi Challenge" — yet with labels visible, Coke wins. Neuroscientist **Read Montague** put this paradox in an fMRI scanner. Sipping either cola blind activated the **ventromedial prefrontal cortex (VMPFC)**, a basic reward area, about equally. But when participants knew they were drinking Coke, the **dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (DLPFC)** — a region tied to higher-order memory and associations — lit up as well, and reported pleasure rose. Coke's decades of advertising had built neural machinery that adds enjoyment the sugar water alone cannot. The brand advantage is not a lie people tell surveyors; it is measurable brain activity.

Expectations even shape performance through **stereotype priming**. In a Harvard study by Margaret Shih, Todd Pittinsky, and Nalini Ambady, Asian-American women took a math test after subtle reminders of one identity or the other: those primed with their ethnicity (stereotyped as math-strong) outperformed a control group, while those primed with their gender (stereotyped as math-weak) underperformed it. Relatedly, John Bargh showed that people unscrambling sentences seeded with elderly-themed words afterward walked measurably more slowly down the hall. Stereotypes act not only on observers but on the people they describe — and this is one reason opposing sides in a political or ethnic conflict can watch the same event and honestly perceive different realities. The only reliable fix is the blind-taste-test move: neutral, label-free presentation, often via a third party.

## The $2.50 Painkiller: Price as Placebo

Does what we *pay* set expectations powerful enough to change physiology? Ariely, Baba Shiv, Ziv Carmon, and Rebecca Waber invented **Veladone-Rx**, a fake opioid painkiller presented with a slick brochure — in reality a vitamin C capsule. Participants received calibrated electric shocks, took the pill, and were shocked again.

When the brochure priced Veladone at $2.50 per dose, nearly all participants reported meaningful pain relief. When a discount flyer cut the price to 10 cents per dose, only about half did. Same placebo, same shocks; the price tag alone roughly halved the drug's "effectiveness." The discount hurt most among people who had recently experienced real pain — precisely those with the largest stake in their medicine working.

This is the **placebo effect** (Latin for "I shall please") harnessed by commerce. Placebos work through two mechanisms: *belief* — confidence in the drug, doctor, or procedure — and *conditioning* — the body learns to release its own pharmacy, including endorphins and other opiate-like compounds, in response to cues that have predicted relief before. Most of medical history ran on this engine: powdered mummy, toads' eyes, and bloodletting "worked" because patients expected them to. Price has simply become a modern cue: expensive implies potent, so expensive partially *is* potent.

The unsettling corollary is that discounts degrade real outcomes. A patient who buys marked-down or generic medication may extract genuinely less relief from chemically identical molecules — not because of gullibility, but because the brain's self-treatment system responds to the signal price sends.

## Discounted Drinks and Sham Surgery: How Deep Does It Go?

The price-placebo effect extends beyond pain into cognition. Ariely's team sold students **SoBe Adrenaline Rush**, an energy drink marketed as boosting mental acuity, then had them solve word-jumble puzzles. Students who paid full price performed as expected — but students who bought the identical drink at a steep discount solved substantially fewer puzzles, roughly 30 percent fewer. The effect grew when the researchers hyped the drink's claims beforehand: the more participants were led to expect from the beverage, the more the discount dragged performance down. "You get what you pay for" turns out to be less a warning about quality than a description of how our minds price-adjust reality.

Then there is the most dramatic demonstration on record. Houston orthopedic surgeon **J. Bruce Moseley** ran a controlled trial of arthroscopic knee surgery for osteoarthritis, published in the *New England Journal of Medicine* in 2002. Patients were randomized to real debridement, real lavage, or **sham surgery** — anesthesia, three superficial incisions, a theatrical splash of saline, and forty simulated minutes in the operating room. Over two years of follow-up, the placebo group reported just as much pain relief and walked just as well as patients who received the genuine operations. A procedure performed hundreds of thousands of times a year, at billions of dollars in cost, apparently worked no better than the *expectation* of surgery.

The pattern is consistent: the grander and costlier the intervention — a prestigious surgeon, a high-tech operating room, a premium price — the stronger the placebo it generates. Which raises two hard questions. Should sellers and physicians *use* this force deliberately? And how do we protect the promise of cheap generics and discounted care if discounting itself erodes the benefit?

## The Ethics of Belief: Placebos, Generics, and Honest Deception

Ariely refuses to treat the placebo effect as mere error to be stamped out. If a sugar pill or a confident bedside manner triggers real endorphin release and real recovery, the benefit is not fake — only the mechanism is misdescribed. Physicians already exploit this quietly, prescribing antibiotics for viral colds or vitamins as tacit placebos. Banning the practice outright would discard genuine healing; embracing it openly might destroy the belief that powers it.

Two dilemmas follow. First, the **generic-drug problem**: if paying less measurably reduces relief, then steering patients — often the poorest ones — toward the cheapest option may cost them real therapeutic value, even though the chemistry is identical. The fix is not higher prices but managing expectations: framing generics as equally potent, and understanding when discounts should be de-emphasized at the moment of consumption.

Second, the **testing problem**: Moseley's trial shows that some accepted treatments are pure placebo, yet running sham-surgery experiments means cutting people open and giving them nothing. Ariely argues the discomfort is worth it. Without placebo-controlled tests of procedures, society pays enormous sums — and accepts surgical risks — for rituals. The ethical calculus should weigh the few participants in a trial against the millions who undergo useless operations when we decline to ask the question.

For individuals, the takeaways cut both ways. You can be exploited: marketers know a higher price and glossier trappings will make you experience more benefit, and they price accordingly. But you can also self-deal: choosing the branded pill, the ritual, the confident doctor, or simply the full-price version of something that matters is a legitimate way to buy extra efficacy — from your own brain. Expectations are a lever; the only mistake is pretending you're not holding it.

## In Practice
- Before judging anything important — a wine, a resume, competing proposals — blind yourself to brands, names, and prices first, and add the labels only after you've formed an opinion.
- When serving something unconventional, reveal the surprising ingredient after people taste it, not before; disclosed early, it will genuinely taste worse to them.
- Upgrade the trappings of experiences you want to enjoy more: nice glassware, plating, and setting demonstrably increase real pleasure at near-zero cost.
- Prime yourself deliberately before performance: invoke an identity or track record associated with competence, and avoid rehearsing negative stereotypes about yourself.
- If a medication or treatment matters to you and belief affects outcomes, don't dwell on how little it cost — and never mentally file your medicine as "the cheap knockoff."
- In a dispute where both sides "saw" different facts, bring in a neutral third party or strip identifying details before evaluating claims — expectations distort perception on both sides.
