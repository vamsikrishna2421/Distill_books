# Hot States and Broken Promises: Arousal, Procrastination, and Self-Control

## Key Ideas
- In a calm, rational "cold" state, we systematically and severely underestimate how differently we will think, judge, and behave once we are aroused, angry, hungry, or craving — a blind spot called the hot-cold empathy gap.
- Ariely and Loewenstein's Berkeley study showed sexually aroused men were dramatically more willing to endorse odd, unsafe, and even immoral acts than the same men predicted about themselves minutes earlier while calm.
- Because willpower reliably fails inside a hot state, effective self-protection means avoiding or pre-structuring the situation before the hot state begins — not planning to resist mid-temptation.
- Procrastination is the same failure in slow motion: we make cool-headed plans (save, exercise, work steadily), then abandon them when present impulses flare.
- Ariely's three-class deadline experiment found externally dictated deadlines produced the best work, no deadlines the worst, and self-imposed deadlines fell in between — proof that people know they procrastinate and can fight it with binding commitments, though imperfectly.
- Precommitment devices — automatic payroll savings, self-limiting credit cards, simplified maintenance bundles — work by taking the decision away from our unreliable future selves.

## The Berkeley Arousal Study: Meet Your Hot-State Stranger

Dan Ariely and George Loewenstein recruited male UC Berkeley students to answer a battery of questions twice: once in a normal, calm frame of mind, and once while sexually aroused (self-stimulating alone, answering on a shrink-wrapped laptop). The questions covered three areas: the appeal of unusual sexual activities, willingness to behave immorally to obtain sex (such as slipping a woman a drug or persisting after she says no), and willingness to have unprotected sex.

The gap between the two states was enormous. Compared with their own cold-state answers, aroused participants rated odd sexual activities (from unusual partners to fetish scenarios) roughly **72 percent** more appealing. Their stated willingness to engage in morally dubious behavior to get sex jumped about **136 percent**. Their willingness to skip condoms rose about **25 percent** — even though every one of them, when calm, insisted safe sex was non-negotiable.

The crucial finding is not that arousal changes behavior — everyone suspects that. It is that participants **could not predict** the change. In their cold state, each man believed he knew himself; his aroused self turned out to be, in Ariely's framing, almost a different person, one whose preferences and moral limits he could not foresee. Prevention, protection, and conservatism vanished from the aroused mind's radar entirely.

This is the **hot-cold empathy gap**, Loewenstein's term for our inability, in one emotional state, to accurately imagine our decision-making in another. It cuts both ways: the calm self can't picture the aroused self, and the aroused self can't summon the calm self's caution. And it generalizes far beyond sex — hunger, anger, frustration, jealousy, and drug craving are all **hot states** that hijack judgment while convincing us we're still in control. Every experienced state is temporary, but decisions made inside it are often not.

## Why "Just Say No" Fails: Sex Ed, Teen Drivers, and Policy

If the calm self cannot predict the hot self, then strategies that rely on in-the-moment restraint are built on sand. Sex education that teaches teenagers to stop at the brink assumes the cold-state teen will still be running the show at the critical moment. The Berkeley data say otherwise: by the time passion arrives, the person who attended the abstinence lecture is functionally absent.

Ariely draws two practical conclusions. First, **avoid the flame, not just the burn**: the reliable intervention is walking away before temptation escalates, because once a hot state ignites, resistance is a losing bet. Second, **prepare in the cold state for the hot one**: teens should carry condoms even if they sincerely intend abstinence, because the intention will not survive arousal but the condom in the pocket will. Rejecting preparation as "planning to sin" mistakes how the mind actually works.

The same logic applies to other hot states. A teen driver's judgment degrades when the car fills with excited friends and loud music, so the fix is structural — graduated licenses, passenger limits, or cars programmable to restrict speed or behavior when conditions deteriorate — not lectures about responsibility. An angry person should adopt a personal rule to make no decisions and send no messages until the anger passes. Even public policy carries the lesson: officials making choices about war, punishment, or emergencies while frightened or enraged are subject to the same gap, which argues for cooling-off periods and precommitted rules over discretion in the heat of the moment.

The deeper point unsettles the classical picture of one coherent, rational self. We are more like two selves sharing a body — a **Dr. Jekyll** who makes plans and a **Mr. Hyde** who breaks them — and wisdom consists of Jekyll designing the environment so Hyde has fewer opportunities to take the wheel.

## The Deadline Experiment: Dictated Beats Self-Imposed Beats None

Procrastination (from the Latin for "putting off until tomorrow") is the hot-cold gap stretched across weeks: we coolly resolve to save, diet, or work steadily, then surrender each day to the immediate pull of spending, dessert, or distraction. To test what actually counters it, Ariely (with Klaus Wertenbroch) ran an experiment on three sections of his semester-long consumer-behavior class at MIT, each required to submit three papers.

The three sections got different deadline regimes. The **dictated** section had three instructor-set deadlines spaced evenly through the semester. The **no-deadline** section could hand in all three papers any time up to the last class, with no penalty for waiting. The **self-imposed** section had to commit, in the first week, to their own binding deadlines for each paper — with a grade penalty for lateness — even though the rationally optimal choice was to set all three at semester's end and keep maximum flexibility.

Two results stand out. First, most students in the self-imposed section did **not** choose the "rational" all-at-the-end option; they voluntarily spaced their deadlines, implicitly admitting they procrastinate and reaching for a tool against it. Second, the grades ranked cleanly: the dictated-deadline class earned the best grades, the no-deadline class the worst, and the self-imposed class landed in the middle.

Why the middle? Students who spaced their self-set deadlines well performed as strongly as the dictated group; the section's average was dragged down by classmates who didn't space them properly. The lessons compress neatly. One: procrastination is near-universal, and unrestricted freedom is its accomplice — the no-deadline students had the most flexibility and did the worst. Two: people who acknowledge their weakness can substantially defeat it with **precommitment** — binding, penalty-backed constraints chosen in advance. Three: self-designed constraints are better than none but worse than a well-designed external "authoritarian" structure, so borrowing or engineering good defaults beats improvising them.

## Precommitment Devices: Outsourcing Willpower to Design

The deadline result generalizes: whenever a good outcome requires many small acts of restraint, the winning move is to remove the repeated decision entirely. Ariely offers three templates.

**Automatic savings.** Resolving to save whatever is left at month's end fails, because the hot-state spender gets to the money first. The fix is a standing payroll deduction that routes money to savings before it ever reaches the checking account — a dictated deadline for your finances. The same architecture could be pushed further, from employer defaults to schemes where committing in advance to save future raises makes saving painless.

**The self-control credit card.** Cards are engineered to exploit hot states: instant gratification now, abstract payment later, feeding massive household consumer debt. Ariely's proposed antidote lets users precommit, in a cold state, to their own spending rules — caps by category (say, $50 on coffee, $200 a month on clothes), timing restrictions, and self-chosen penalties for breaches, from the card simply declining, to an email alerting a spouse, to an automatic transfer of the excess into savings. He pitched the idea to executives at a major New York bank; they listened with apparent excitement and never called back — consumer over-borrowing, after all, was their profit engine.

**Ford's maintenance bundles.** Ford found customers were skipping service because its schedules were bewildering — the recommendations across its engines and vehicles multiplied into roughly 18,000 vehicle-and-service combinations. Honda's dealers thrived by collapsing maintenance into three simple, uniform "menu" bundles hit at regular intervals of about 5,000 miles. Ford copied the simplification, deliberately sacrificing some engineering precision, and service compliance climbed. The principle: a technically suboptimal plan people actually follow beats a perfect plan they ignore — a template equally suited to medicine, where bundled, scheduled, deposit-backed checkups would outperform a long list of ideal-but-ignored screening recommendations.

Across all three, the pattern is the same: don't negotiate with your future hot-state self. Bind it in advance.

## In Practice
- Identify your recurring hot states — anger, hunger, arousal, late-night browsing, craving — and adopt one rule for each that triggers *before* the state starts (leave the situation, delay the decision 24 hours, don't shop hungry).
- Prepare in cold blood for hot moments: carry the condom, delete the delivery app, keep no junk food in the house — assume your in-the-moment willpower will be worth nothing.
- Convert one big open-ended goal into dictated deadlines this week: break it into dated milestones, and attach a real penalty or an accountability partner to each.
- Automate your savings now — set a payroll or automatic monthly transfer so the money moves before you can spend it.
- Build your own self-control credit card: set category spending caps, card locks, or alerts in your banking app, and choose a concrete consequence for breaching them.
- When designing plans for yourself or others, trade optimality for compliance, Ford-style: fewer, simpler, bundled steps on a fixed schedule beat a perfect regimen nobody follows.
