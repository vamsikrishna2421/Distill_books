# Everything Is Relative: How Comparisons Steer Your Choices

## Key Ideas
- Humans rarely know what anything is worth in absolute terms; we decide by comparing options placed side by side, which makes our choices manipulable by whoever controls the lineup.
- The **decoy effect** shows that adding an obviously inferior version of one option makes that option look like a bargain and dramatically shifts choices toward it — in Ariely's Economist experiment, from 32% to 84%.
- We compare only what is easy to compare and ignore what is hard to compare, so a strategically placed "slightly worse" alternative steers decisions in stores, on menus, and even in dating.
- Relativity governs happiness, not just shopping: satisfaction with salary, house, and vacation depends on the reference points around us, and modern life keeps expanding those reference points upward.
- Because comparison is automatic but the comparison set is not, the practical defense is to deliberately choose what you compare against — narrow the circle when it breeds envy, widen it when it exposes bad deals.

## The Economist Experiment: How a Useless Option Moves the Crowd

Dan Ariely noticed a strange subscription offer on the Economist's website: web-only access for $59, print-only for $125, and print-plus-web for the same $125. The print-only option seemed pointless — nobody should take print alone when print plus web costs exactly the same. But Ariely suspected it wasn't pointless at all; it was there to make the combo look irresistible.

He tested the ad on 100 MIT Sloan students. With all three options, 16% chose web-only and 84% chose the $125 combo; no one chose print-only. Then he removed the "useless" print-only option and asked another 100 students. The choices flipped: 68% now took the cheap web-only subscription and only 32% took the combo. An option nobody wanted had shifted the majority toward the expensive package.

This is the **decoy effect**, also called **asymmetric dominance**. When people face options A and B that are hard to compare directly, adding **-A** — a version clearly worse than A but not clearly worse than B — makes A suddenly easy to evaluate. A dominates -A, that comparison feels like a verdict, and A wins against B too, even though nothing about A or B changed.

The deeper principle: we don't have an internal meter that tells us the absolute value of things. We don't know what a subscription, a car, or a bottle of wine is "really" worth. Instead we estimate value relative to whatever else is in view, and we gravitate toward comparisons that are easy — similar items, one clearly better — while avoiding comparisons that are hard. A realtor exploits this by showing you two similar colonials, one with a damaged roof, alongside a contemporary house: you'll likely buy the intact colonial, because it's the one you can confidently judge superior to something.

## Bread Machines and Blind Dates: Relativity in the Wild

Williams-Sonoma provides the classic retail case. When the company first offered a home bread machine for $275, almost nobody bought it — shoppers had no reference point for what a bread machine should cost or whether they needed one. Then, on a marketing consultant's advice, the company added a larger, deluxe model at roughly 50% higher price. The deluxe version barely sold, but sales of the original machine took off. The expensive model wasn't really a product; it was a comparison that made $275 look reasonable.

Restaurants use the same lever: a high-priced entrée at the top of the menu mostly exists to make the second-most-expensive dish feel like a sensible choice — and that's typically the one that drives revenue.

Ariely then showed the effect works on people, not just products. He photographed students, paired faces of similar attractiveness (call them Tom and Jerry), and used Photoshop to create a slightly distorted, less attractive version of one — an "ugly Tom" or "ugly Jerry." He handed MIT students sheets with three photos — Tom, Jerry, and one distorted decoy — and asked whom they'd rather date. Whichever normal face had its distorted twin on the sheet was chosen about 75% of the time. Jerry next to ugly Jerry looks like a catch; the same Jerry next to ugly Tom does not.

The tactical implication is almost comic: if you want to look good at a party, bring a friend who resembles you but is slightly less attractive (or slightly less witty). You become the dominant option in an easy comparison. The sobering implication is that anyone assembling the options you see — a marketer, a broker, a menu designer, a salary negotiator — can quietly install a decoy and tilt your "free" choice before you make it.

## Why Relativity Runs So Deep — and What It Costs You

Relativity isn't a quirk of shopping; it's how human perception works. Just as your eyes judge brightness and size only by contrast with surroundings, your mind judges prices, salaries, and life outcomes only against nearby reference points. That's why the same $7 savings feels different in different frames: most people will drive 15 minutes across town to save $7 on a $25 pen, but won't make the same drive to save $7 on a $455 suit. The dollars are identical; the percentage — the relative frame — differs. Rationally, the only question is whether 15 minutes is worth $7, full stop.

The frame also distorts add-on spending. Once you're paying $5,000 for a catered wedding buffet, a $200 soup upgrade feels trivial; alone, you'd never pay $200 for soup. Big purchases anesthetize us to relatively small amounts that are absolutely large. The same mechanism traps people during upgrades: leaving a mid-range option for a slightly better one always looks cheap next to the money already committed.

Relativity even shapes career-level decisions through **arbitrary comparison sets**. Ariely describes shopping for a car after years on a motorcycle: dealers and instincts steered him toward comparing sedan versus sedan, when the real question — car versus motorcycle versus something else entirely — was harder and therefore skipped. We optimize within the comparison set that's handed to us instead of asking whether it's the right set.

The cost of all this is real money and misallocated attention, but the mechanism is predictable. Once you know the decoy pattern — three options, one clearly dominated — you can spot it in subscription tiers, phone plans, popcorn sizes, and real-estate showings, and ask the question the seller hopes you won't: what would I choose if only the two genuinely different options existed?

## Salary Envy and the Expanding Circle of Comparison

Relativity's heaviest toll is on life satisfaction. H. L. Mencken quipped that a man is satisfied with his salary if he earns "more than his wife's sister's husband" — the comparison we can't avoid seeing. Research bears this out: happiness with income tracks position relative to peers far more than absolute purchasing power. Ariely recounts hearing from young bankers earning hundreds of thousands who felt genuinely poor because officemates earned more.

Policy can backfire on exactly this mechanism. In 1993, U.S. securities regulators forced public companies to disclose executive pay, expecting transparency to shame CEO compensation downward — it had already climbed from about 36 times the average worker's pay in 1976 to about 131 times. Instead, disclosure gave every CEO a public benchmark to beat. Pay ratcheted upward as each executive compared against the best-paid peers, and the multiple soared to roughly 369 times the average worker. Comparison, once visible, became an engine of escalation.

Ordinary life runs the same ratchet. Modern media and mobility keep enlarging our **comparison circles**: we no longer measure our house against the neighbors' but against the ones on television; a raise thrills us until we learn a colleague got more. This is why more income so often fails to buy more contentment — the reference point moves with us. Ariely's account of the two Adamses (an executive and a mailroom clerk in a New Yorker cartoon-like reversal) and of friends who traded high-paying jobs for cheaper cities makes the point concrete: the people who felt rich were the ones whose circles made them the big fish, not the ones with the biggest absolute numbers.

The escalation also feeds the upgrade treadmill. Each new car, kitchen, or neighborhood resets the baseline, so yesterday's luxury becomes today's floor. Wanting more is less a fact about what we have than a fact about what we've recently seen.

## The Defense: Choose Your Own Comparisons

The decoy effect and salary envy share one vulnerability: comparison is automatic, but the comparison set is controllable. Ariely's prescription is not to stop comparing — we can't — but to take deliberate ownership of what we compare against.

First, break circles that make you miserable. If a bigger house, richer friends, or a glossier feed only raises your baseline, shrink the circle: spend less time around the reference points that manufacture dissatisfaction. A person considering a house or salary upgrade should ask whether they're closing a real gap in their life or merely responding to an expanded circle. Sometimes the cheapest fix for wanting more is seeing less.

Second, widen frames that sellers have narrowed. When you catch a three-option lineup with one dominated choice, mentally delete the decoy and re-decide. When a $200 add-on rides on a $25,000 purchase, evaluate it as a standalone $200 decision: what else could that money buy? Converting relative judgments back into absolute ones — dollars, hours, alternatives — strips the frame of its power.

Third, control escalation at its source. As with the Economist's print-only option, the most dangerous options in your life may be ones you'd never choose, placed there to move you. Notice who assembled the menu. Ariely's broader claim, developed through the rest of the book, is that these irrationalities are systematic and repeatable — **predictably irrational** — which is precisely what makes them defensible. You can't rewire perception, but you can decide which contrasts it feeds on. That single move — curating your comparisons instead of inheriting them — turns relativity from a lever others pull into one you hold.

## In Practice
- Before any three-tier purchase (subscriptions, phone plans, menu items), find the dominated decoy, remove it mentally, and choose again between the remaining real options.
- Evaluate add-ons and upgrades as standalone purchases: ask "Would I pay $200 for this by itself?" rather than "Is $200 much next to $25,000?"
- Judge savings in absolute dollars, not percentages — 15 minutes of effort for $7 is the same deal on a pen as on a suit.
- Audit one comparison circle this week (a feed, a peer group, a neighborhood fantasy) that reliably makes you feel behind, and cut your exposure to it.
- When negotiating salary or weighing a raise, decide in advance what number meets your actual needs and goals, so a colleague's figure can't redefine your satisfaction.
- Before optimizing within a category (which sedan?), step back and confirm the category itself: widen the comparison set to include the genuinely different alternatives.
