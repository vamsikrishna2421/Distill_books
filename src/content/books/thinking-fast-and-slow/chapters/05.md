# Regression to the Mean and Taming Predictions

## Key Ideas
- Extreme performances — very good or very bad — are usually followed by more ordinary ones, purely because luck averages out; no cause is needed for this reversion.
- Whenever the correlation between two measures is less than perfect, regression to the mean is mathematically guaranteed; correlation and regression are two views of the same fact.
- Our causal, story-loving minds invent explanations for regression effects (punishment "works," praise "backfires," magazine covers "jinx" athletes), leading us to false beliefs about what causes what.
- Intuitive predictions are typically nonregressive: we match the extremity of the forecast to the extremity of the evidence, ignoring how weakly the evidence actually predicts the outcome.
- Kahneman's four-step correction anchors every prediction on the baseline average, then moves toward the intuitive estimate only in proportion to the true correlation between evidence and outcome.
- Corrected, regressive predictions will never foresee extreme outcomes — a cost worth paying unless you are rationally hunting for rare, high-payoff extremes, as venture capitalists do.

## Praise, Punishment, and the Flight-Instructor Illusion

Teaching Israeli air force instructors, Kahneman heard a seasoned officer object to the research finding that reward beats punishment. In the officer's experience, cadets he praised for a clean maneuver usually flew worse next time, while cadets he screamed at after a botched one usually improved. His conclusion: praise corrupts, punishment works.

Kahneman recognized the flaw instantly, calling it "one of the most satisfying eureka experiences of my career." The officer was watching **regression to the mean**. Any single flight maneuver reflects skill plus a large dose of random variation. A cadet praised after an unusually good maneuver was probably above his own average — so his next attempt would likely be closer to that average, i.e., worse, regardless of the praise. A cadet berated after an unusually bad attempt was probably below his own average and due to improve, regardless of the shouting.

The instructor was not wrong about the sequence of events; he was wrong about the cause. Performance fluctuated randomly around each cadet's true skill level, and the instructor had attached a causal story — his own feedback — to fluctuations that needed no cause at all.

The trap generalizes viciously. Because we naturally punish others after bad outcomes and reward them after good ones, and because outcomes then regress toward normal either way, life itself teaches us the false lesson that punishment is effective and praise is not. The feedback the world gives us is rigged against kindness: we are statistically guaranteed to feel rewarded for harshness and disappointed by generosity, even when neither has any effect.

Kahneman demonstrated the point with coins: officers tossed coins at a target twice, blind to feedback. Those who did well on the first throw mostly did worse on the second, and vice versa — regression with no praise or blame anywhere in sight.

## Success Equals Talent Plus Luck

Kahneman compresses the logic into two formulas: **success = talent + luck**, and **great success = a little more talent + a lot of luck**. Any performance extreme enough to attract attention almost certainly contains an outsized helping of luck, and luck, by definition, does not persist.

Consider two golfers on day one of a tournament: one shoots 66, the other 77. The 66 almost certainly combined above-average talent with a good day; the 77 combined below-average talent with a bad day. The best prediction for day two is that both move toward the field's average — the leader scores worse, the laggard scores better — not because of pressure or motivation, but because yesterday's luck won't repeat.

This is the entire explanation for the **Sports Illustrated jinx**: athletes who make the cover often decline afterward. You only make the cover after an exceptional stretch, which means luck was running unusually hot; performance then regresses, and observers blame overconfidence or media pressure. Similarly, a ski-jump commentator who says a jumper is "tense protecting his lead" after a weaker second jump is narrating pure regression: an excellent first jump predicts a merely good second one, no psychology required.

The pattern hides everywhere causal thinking operates. Highly intelligent women tend to marry men less intelligent than themselves — a fact that invites theories about intimidation or preference, but which is a pure statistical necessity: whenever the correlation between spouses' intelligence is imperfect (it is), extreme scorers on one side will pair with less extreme scorers on the other, on average. A business whose terrible quarter triggers a management shakeup will usually improve next quarter whether or not the shakeup did anything. Regression effects wear causal costumes, and System 1 buys the costume every time.

## Imperfect Correlation Guarantees Regression

Francis Galton discovered regression in the 1880s while studying heredity in seeds and human height: children of unusually tall parents are tall, but less tall than their parents; children of short parents are short, but less short. He called it "regression toward mediocrity" and needed years — and the era's best statisticians — to grasp what it meant.

The modern statement is compact. The **correlation coefficient** between two measures expresses the proportion of shared factors that determine both, from 0 (nothing shared) to 1 (fully determined by the same factors). Height of parents and children correlates about .50; SAT scores and college GPA around .60; income and education in the US around .40. And the key theorem: **wherever the correlation between two measures is less than 1, there will be regression to the mean.** Correlation and regression are not two phenomena; they are two descriptions of one fact.

This matters practically because regression effects masquerade as treatment effects. Depressed children given an energy drink for three months will reliably improve — because "depressed children" is an extreme group selected for low scores, and extreme groups drift toward the mean over time regardless of treatment. The improvement is real but uncaused by the drink. This is precisely why credible research requires **control groups**: the control group is expected to improve by regression alone, and only improvement beyond the control's counts as a treatment effect.

Kahneman notes that regression is deeply unintuitive even for trained scientists — it took two hundred years after Gauss for anyone to notice it, and researchers still routinely mistake regression artifacts for causal findings. The mind demands a causal story; regression has none. It is a consequence of selection and luck, and statements about it ("smart women marry down") sound either trivially causal or bafflingly empty when correctly explained as statistics.

## Intuitive Predictions Are Too Extreme

Asked to predict outcomes, System 1 does not forecast — it evaluates the evidence and translates that evaluation directly into a prediction. Kahneman's example: Julie, now a university senior, read fluently at age four. What is her GPA? Most people answer around 3.7 or 3.8, and they reach it by **intensity matching**: reading at four is roughly as impressive as a 3.7 GPA is high, so that becomes the forecast.

This substitutes an easy question (how impressive is the evidence?) for the hard one (what does this evidence actually predict?). The prediction is **nonregressive**: it treats the evidence as if it correlated perfectly with the outcome. In reality, early reading age correlates weakly with adult academic performance — perhaps .30 — so most of Julie's GPA is determined by factors we know nothing about, and our forecast should sit much closer to the average GPA.

Kahneman's remedy is a four-step procedure for **taming intuitive predictions**:

1. **Start with the baseline.** Identify the average outcome for the relevant reference class — e.g., the mean GPA of university seniors. This is what you would predict knowing nothing about Julie.
2. **Form your intuitive estimate.** Determine the outcome that matches your impression of the evidence — the 3.7 that intensity matching delivers.
3. **Estimate the correlation** between your evidence and the outcome — here, how strongly reading precocity predicts adult GPA, perhaps .30.
4. **Move from the baseline toward your intuition by that proportion.** With a correlation of .30, move 30% of the distance from the average GPA toward 3.7. With a correlation of 0, stay at the baseline; with 1.0, trust your intuition fully.

The same procedure disciplines any numeric forecast — a candidate's future job performance from a dazzling interview, a firm's growth from one strong year. It anchors the forecast on what usually happens and lets the evidence pull it away only as far as the evidence deserves.

## The Price of Unbiased Forecasts — and When to Pay It

The four-step correction produces predictions that are unbiased but deliberately unexciting: they hug the average and will never call an extreme outcome. That is a genuine cost. An admissions officer using regressive predictions will never forecast that any applicant earns a 4.0, even though some student surely will. Moderation is the price of accuracy: unbiased predictions err in both directions equally, but they refuse to gamble on rare cases from thin evidence.

Kahneman distinguishes prediction from evaluation. It is perfectly legitimate to say the evidence about a start-up founder is outstanding — that is an assessment of what you see now. The error is letting that evaluation slide unmodified into a forecast of distant outcomes, which depend heavily on factors the evidence cannot reach. Confusing the two is the signature move of System 1, and it explains our willingness to predict rare, extreme events from weak evidence — the same representativeness sin behind base-rate neglect (Chapter 4).

There are principled exceptions. If your payoffs are asymmetric, extreme predictions can be rational even when they are usually wrong. A venture capitalist needs to identify the rare company that returns a hundredfold; missing it costs far more than backing several duds. For such players, systematically overweighting long shots is a defensible policy — but it should be a chosen policy, adopted with eyes open, not the default output of an intensity-matching intuition.

For everyone else, two symmetrical failures are worth diagnosing. If your forecasts are as extreme as your impressions, you are not regressing at all. If, chastened, you refuse to depart from the baseline no matter what, you are throwing away real information. Corrected prediction is effortful System 2 work: find the baseline, judge the evidence, weight it honestly, and accept forecasts that feel too tame. They are usually right precisely because they feel that way.

## In Practice

- Before praising or blaming anyone for an extreme result, ask how much of it was luck; expect their next result to be more ordinary either way, and don't credit your feedback for the change.
- When any treatment, coaching, or intervention is applied after an unusually bad stretch, demand a control-group comparison before believing the subsequent improvement was caused by it.
- For every numeric forecast this week (hire, budget, sales, timeline), write down the baseline average for similar cases first, and start your estimate there rather than from the evidence.
- Apply the four steps explicitly: baseline, intuitive estimate, honest correlation guess, then move only that fraction of the distance toward your intuition.
- Keep evaluations and predictions separate: say "this candidate interviewed brilliantly" without letting it become "this candidate will perform brilliantly."
- If you must bet on extremes (venture-style, asymmetric payoffs), do it as a stated policy with sized stakes — not because one vivid piece of evidence felt compelling.
