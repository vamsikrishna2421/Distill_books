# The Associative Machine

## Key Ideas
- System 1 works by **associative activation**: one idea triggers a spreading cascade of linked ideas, emotions, and even physical reactions, almost none of which reaches conscious awareness.
- **Priming** studies suggest that incidental exposure to words, images, or objects can measurably shift behavior — though many of the most famous demonstrations have struggled to replicate and should be held loosely.
- **Cognitive ease** is System 1's quality signal: anything that is easy to read, familiar, repeated, or encountered in a good mood feels more true, more likable, and less risky — regardless of whether it actually is.
- Repetition alone breeds belief. The **illusion of truth** means familiarity is easily mistaken for accuracy, which is why propaganda and marketing lean on it.
- System 1 continuously maintains a model of what is normal, registers surprises against it, and instantly invents causal stories — we perceive causes and intentions directly, the way we perceive colors.
- Because causal explanations come effortlessly while statistical thinking does not, we systematically over-explain random events and under-use base rates.

## One Idea Lights Up a Web of Others

Read the two words "bananas" and "vomit" together and something unpleasant happens automatically: your face flickers with disgust, your memory retrieves sickness-related ideas, and for the next few minutes you will be quicker to recognize words like "nausea" — and temporarily wary of bananas. Nothing was asserted, yet your mind built a causal mini-story linking the two words.

This is **associative activation**. Kahneman, following a long line from Hume onward, describes memory as a vast network of linked ideas — concepts, memories, emotions, bodily states. Activating one node spreads activation to its neighbors, which activate their neighbors in turn. The process is fast, parallel, and mostly invisible: you are conscious of only a fraction of the ideas your own mind has just stirred up.

Two features matter for everything that follows. First, the cascade is **coherence-seeking**: System 1 links activated ideas into the most sensible story it can, suppressing alternatives. Second, it runs both ways — ideas prime actions, and actions prime ideas. This two-way traffic between mind and body is called the **ideomotor effect**, and it produces the strangest findings in the book.

## Priming: The World Nudges You Without Permission

In John Bargh's famous "Florida effect," students who unscrambled sentences containing words like *Florida*, *forgetful*, *bald*, and *wrinkle* subsequently walked measurably more slowly down the hallway — primed with old age, they behaved old. The effect also reversed: students asked to walk slowly became faster at recognizing aging-related words. Similarly, people made to smile by holding a pencil crosswise in their teeth found cartoons funnier, and people induced to nod while listening agreed more with what they heard.

Priming extends to values and social behavior. Kathleen Vohs showed that reminders of money — a screensaver of floating bills, a stack of Monopoly cash — made participants more independent but also more selfish: they persisted longer alone on hard problems, helped a confederate less, and placed chairs farther apart when arranging a meeting. Voters deciding on school funding supported it more when their polling place was a school. In the **Lady Macbeth effect**, thinking about a shameful act increased the appeal of cleansing products — and with striking specificity: people who had *lied on the phone* preferred mouthwash, while those who had *lied by e-mail* preferred soap. In a real office kitchen, an honesty box collected nearly three times more money in weeks when a poster of watching eyes hung above it than in weeks with flowers.

An essential caveat: social priming became a center of psychology's replication crisis. The Florida effect, money priming, and several related results have repeatedly failed to reproduce in larger, preregistered studies, and Kahneman himself later urged the field to clean up the area and conceded he had put too much faith in underpowered studies. The safe takeaway is modest but real: associative activation is genuine and context influences judgment, but the dramatic claim that trivial cues routinely steer major behaviors is not established.

## Cognitive Ease: Easy Feels True, Familiar Feels Good

At every moment System 1 tracks whether processing is flowing smoothly (**cognitive ease**) or laboring (**cognitive strain**). Ease has many causes — a repeated experience, a clearly printed message, a primed idea, a good mood — but it produces one undifferentiated feeling. So the causes get confused: anything easy to process feels familiar, true, good, and effortless, whatever the real reason for the ease.

This creates predictable illusions. Larry Jacoby's "becoming famous overnight" experiment showed that a made-up name like *David Stenbill*, seen once on a list, later feels vaguely familiar — and familiarity gets misread as fame. The **illusion of truth** follows the same logic: repeated statements are easier to process, so they feel truer. You don't even need to repeat the whole claim; repeating the phrase "the body temperature of a chicken" makes people readier to accept any full statement about chicken body temperature.

The practical corollary is that persuasion rides on legibility. Messages in clear high-contrast fonts, in simple words, in rhyme ("woes unite foes" is judged more insightful than its non-rhyming paraphrase), or from easily pronounced sources are believed more. Stocks with pronounceable tickers outperform tongue-twisters in their first week of trading.

The deepest source of ease is sheer repetition. Robert Zajonc's **mere exposure effect** showed that meaningless Turkish-like words printed repeatedly in campus newspapers came to be rated more pleasant, with liking rising with frequency — an effect that works even for stimuli flashed too fast to consciously see, and even in chicks exposed to tones before hatching. Zajonc read it as deep biology: in a dangerous world, a stimulus that has recurred without harm is probably safe.

Strain has an upside: it mobilizes System 2. Students given the bat-and-ball-style puzzles of the Cognitive Reflection Test in a faint, hard-to-read font made fewer intuitive errors (though this result, too, has replicated poorly). And mood modulates the whole machine: in Alice Isen's work, happy participants were more intuitive and creative — better at spotting that *dive, light, rocket* share the association *sky* — while unhappy ones were more vigilant and less error-prone. Good mood loosens System 2's grip; ease and gullibility travel together.

## Norms, Surprises, and the Instant Hunt for Causes

System 1 maintains a running model of your normal world and measures every event against it — surprise is the signal that the model was violated. The model updates fast: when the Kahnemans twice ran into the same acquaintance, Jon, in improbable places, a third improbable meeting would have felt normal, because "meeting Jon in odd places" had become its own category. A first diner wincing at the soup is a surprise; when a second diner winces, the two events retroactively explain each other.

This is the heart of **norm theory** (Kahneman and Miller): events are not judged against expectations formed in advance but against norms recruited *after the fact*. That is why a sentence like "the large mouse climbed over the trunk of the tiny elephant" parses instantly — each noun carries its own norms of size — and why the word "pickpocket" feels almost stated in a story where Jane loses her wallet in a crowded New York street: hearers later misremember it as having been said. Norms also power the **Moses illusion**: asked "How many animals of each kind did Moses take into the ark?", most people answer "two," never noticing it was Noah — Moses is normal enough in a biblical context that System 1 raises no alarm.

Causality gets the same automatic treatment. Albert Michotte demonstrated that we literally *see* causation: when one shape on a screen moves and touches another that immediately moves, observers irresistibly perceive launching, though nothing but sequenced motion exists. Fritz Heider and Marianne Simmel's animation of a bullying triangle, a resisting triangle, and a frightened circle shows we perceive *intention* just as directly — viewers cannot help seeing personalities in geometry, and six-month-old infants already treat such sequences as agent and action. Paul Bloom has argued this born readiness to separate minds from bodies is a root of religious belief.

The cost of this gift is the theme of later chapters: because causal stories come free and statistical reasoning is expensive, System 1 explains random fluctuations with narrative — a bias that drives the errors covered in Chapters 4 and 5, from the law of small numbers to regression to the mean.

## In Practice

- Before an important judgment, audit your context: notice recent conversations, headlines, and moods that may have primed the frame you are bringing, and deliberately consider the opposite frame.
- Treat familiarity as a warning, not a credential — when a claim "just feels true," ask whether you have ever actually seen evidence for it or merely heard it repeated.
- When writing to persuade, maximize cognitive ease: clean fonts, high contrast, simple vocabulary, memorable phrasing, and a pronounceable name for the thing you are pitching.
- When reading to evaluate, do the reverse: slow yourself down — summarize the claim in your own words or check one cited fact — so cognitive strain can wake up System 2.
- Distrust the first causal story you generate for any surprising outcome; before accepting it, ask "could this be noise?" and look for a base rate or comparison group.
- Discount dramatic one-study findings (including the priming classics here): ask whether a result has survived large preregistered replications before you build habits on it.
