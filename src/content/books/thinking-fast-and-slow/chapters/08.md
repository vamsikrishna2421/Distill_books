# Risky Choices: Rare Events, Mental Accounts, and Frames

## Key Ideas
- Rare events are overweighted when they are vivid and easy to imagine (terrorism, lotteries) but underweighted when we learn about them only from personal experience — we buy too much insurance against dramatic risks and too little against ones we've never seen.
- Narrow framing — evaluating each gamble, project, or stock in isolation — makes loss aversion costly; adopting a broad frame and a standing risk policy ("you win a few, you lose a few") captures the favorable odds that accumulate across many small decisions.
- Thaler's mental accounting explains the disposition effect (selling winners, keeping losers) and the sunk-cost fallacy (throwing good money after bad): we manage emotional ledgers, not total wealth.
- Anticipated regret distorts choices because losses caused by action sting more than identical losses from inaction, which biases people toward defaults, omissions, and overpriced peace of mind.
- Preferences reverse depending on whether options are judged alone or side by side, and logically equivalent descriptions (90% survival vs. 10% mortality, opt-in vs. opt-out organ donation) produce dramatically different decisions — frames are not neutral wrappers around fixed preferences.

## Rare Events: Overweighted When Vivid, Underweighted When Merely Experienced

The fourfold pattern (Chapter 7) showed that people overweight small probabilities. Kahneman explains why: a rare event is judged by the fluency and emotional charge of the image it evokes, not by its stated probability. During waves of bus bombings in Israel, Kahneman found himself avoiding buses even though the statistical risk per ride was tiny — System 1 responds to the possibility, not the probability. Cass Sunstein calls this **probability neglect**: once a vivid threat captures attention, worry is almost insensitive to whether the chance is 1 in 1,000 or 1 in 1,000,000. This is why lotteries and insurance both sell — a ticket buys the pleasant fantasy of winning, a policy buys relief from a frightening image.

A key mechanism is **denominator neglect**. In Seymour Epstein's experiments, people offered a chance to draw a red marble preferred an urn with 8 red marbles out of 100 (8%) over one with 1 out of 10 (10%). The mind pictures eight winning marbles and ignores the denominator. The same effect makes "a vaccine that harms 1 child in 100,000" feel scarier than "a 0.001% risk" — the first frame conjures an image of one damaged child; the second is bloodless. Frequency formats ("1 in 1,000") reliably provoke more fear than equivalent percentages, a fact exploited by lawyers and risk communicators alike.

But overweighting has a boundary condition. Ralph Hertwig and Ido Erev showed that when people learn probabilities from **experience** rather than explicit description — sampling outcomes over time rather than reading the odds — rare events are typically *underweighted*, because most samples never contain them. This explains complacency about earthquakes, floods, and financial crashes: people who have never personally experienced the disaster act as if its probability were near zero. The overall rule: rare events are overweighted when explicitly described and vividly imagined, underweighted when they have never been experienced, and choices are driven either way by images and emotions rather than by the numbers.

## Broad Framing and Risk Policies: The Samuelson Bet

Economist Paul Samuelson offered a colleague a coin flip: win $200 or lose $100. The colleague refused the single bet — loss aversion made the possible $100 loss loom larger than the $200 gain — but said he would happily accept 100 such bets. That answer, though intuitive, is inconsistent: a bundle of attractive gambles is built from gambles he rejects one at a time. The bundle of 100 flips has an expected gain of $5,000 with a vanishingly small chance of losing any money, and refusing each flip individually forfeits that value.

The lesson is the difference between **narrow framing** (evaluating each risky decision in isolation) and **broad framing** (seeing each decision as one draw from a lifetime portfolio of similar decisions). Narrow framing plus loss aversion is expensive: it makes people reject many small favorable gambles whose aggregate is clearly worthwhile. Kahneman offers a mantra for tolerable risks: "you win a few, you lose a few" — provided the gambles are genuinely independent, no single loss threatens your total wealth, and you are not facing a long shot with near-certain loss.

The practical tool is a **risk policy**: a standing rule applied routinely to a whole class of decisions, rather than a fresh emotional evaluation of each case. Examples: always take the highest insurance deductible you can afford; never buy extended warranties; check your stock portfolio quarterly rather than daily (frequent checking exposes you to more painful interim losses and triggers useless churn). A risk policy is the decision-making analogue of the outside view in forecasting (Chapter 6): both fight the tendency to treat the current case as unique. Organizations suffer the same disease — executives who each reject a risky project in their own division collectively refuse a portfolio the CEO would love — so leaders should aggregate risks across units instead of letting each manager be loss-averse alone.

## Mental Accounts: The Disposition Effect and Sunk Costs

Richard Thaler showed that people do not treat money as fungible; they keep **mental accounts** — separate ledgers for vacation funds, gas money, or individual stocks — and seek to close each account in the black. This bookkeeping is emotional, not financial.

The **disposition effect** is one consequence. Terrance Odean's trading data showed investors are far more likely to sell stocks that have gained than stocks that have lost, because selling a winner closes a mental account with a profit (pleasure) while selling a loser locks in a failure (pain). This is exactly backward: taxes favor realizing losses, and recent winners tended to outperform recent losers in the short run. The stock's purchase price is irrelevant to what you should do next; only its future prospects matter.

The **sunk-cost fallacy** is the same error at larger scale: continuing to invest in a failing project, marriage, or research program because so much has already been spent. A firm that has sunk $50 million into a doomed project keeps funding it to avoid closing the account at a loss — the losses land on the successor CEO's ledger instead. Kahneman's diagnostic example: two fans drive through a blizzard to a game, one who paid for his ticket and one who got it free. The payer is more determined to go, though the money is gone either way and only the risks of the drive should matter. Boards should note that a manager defending a sunk cost is protecting a personal account, and sometimes the kindest fix is a new manager with no ledger to defend. Economics students learn to ignore sunk costs; it may be the discipline's most valuable practical lesson.

## Regret and Responsibility: Why Action Hurts More Than Inaction

Regret is a counterfactual emotion — it depends on how easily you can imagine having done otherwise — and it is systematically stronger for **actions** than for omissions. Kahneman and Tversky's example: Mr. Paul owns stock in Company A, considered switching to B, and didn't; Mr. George switched from B to A. Both missed the same $1,200 gain, but most people expect George, who acted, to feel far more regret, because his default (staying put) is easy to imagine.

This asymmetry warps decisions. People demand a large premium to trade a vaccine's small risk of harm for a disease's larger risk, because harm caused by their own act of vaccinating feels worse than harm from doing nothing — an anticipation of regret and blame, not a calculation of the child's interests. The same force explains excessive conventionality: losses that follow a normal, default choice attract less regret (and less blame from others) than identical losses following an unusual choice, so money managers buy blue chips and doctors order defensive tests. Consumers pay irrational premiums to avoid future self-recrimination.

Kahneman's advice is not to pretend regret away but to price it deliberately. Anticipate it explicitly ("if this fails, will I torture myself for having acted?"), remember that people generally overestimate how intensely and how long they will feel regret — we are protected by a **psychological immune system** — and never compound a loss with hindsight. If you make a considered decision that turns out badly, refuse the retrospective illusion that the risk was obvious. Be especially wary of long-term thinking: the decision to protect yourself from regret today is a decision your future self will experience, and that self may care more about the outcome you forfeited than the regret you dodged.

## Preference Reversals: Single Versus Joint Evaluation

Preferences are not stable properties waiting to be read off; they are constructed in the moment, and the construction depends on what else is in view. Sarah Lichtenstein and Paul Slovic demonstrated **preference reversals** with two bets: a "P-bet" (high chance of a small prize) and a "$-bet" (low chance of a large prize). Choosing between them, most people take the safe P-bet; asked to price each separately, most assign a higher cash value to the $-bet, because pricing draws attention to the dollar amount. The pattern survived replication in a Las Vegas casino with real gamblers, and economists Grether and Plott, attempting to debunk it, confirmed it instead.

The general principle: **single evaluation** recruits emotional System 1 reactions within whatever category comes to mind, while **joint evaluation** forces a System 2 comparison that can bring the neglected attribute into focus. Christopher Hsee's dinnerware study (Chapter 4) is one instance. Another: people asked separately offer more money to save dolphins than to fund health checks for migrant farmworkers, because each is judged against its own category (animal causes vs. human causes); shown together, nearly everyone gives more to the humans. Similarly, jurors set higher punitive damages for a burglary-related injury than for a fraud when cases are seen alone against their own category norms, but reverse when the cases are compared.

The unsettling implication is that legal and administrative systems built on evaluating one case at a time — one damage award, one fine, one job candidate — systematically produce incoherent results. Joint evaluation is usually (not always) the better guide, because it exposes the comparison your single-case intuition silently ignored. When you can, evaluate options side by side; when you can't, ask what a broader comparison set would reveal.

## Frames Are Not Neutral: Equivalent Descriptions, Opposite Choices

A **framing effect** occurs when logically equivalent statements of a problem evoke different choices. Amos Tversky, Barbara McNeil, and colleagues told physicians about a lung-cancer treatment framed either as "90% one-month survival" or "10% one-month mortality." Surgery was chosen far more often in the survival frame — 84% vs. 50% — and medical training provided no immunity.

The most famous demonstration is the **Asian disease problem**. An outbreak is expected to kill 600 people. Framed as gains, most choose the sure option (save 200 for certain) over the gamble (1/3 chance to save all 600); framed as losses (400 die for certain vs. a 1/3 chance nobody dies), most choose the gamble. The options are identical; only the reference point moves. Risk aversion for gains and risk seeking for losses flip on a wording change. De Martino's fMRI work adds a mechanism: KEEP framings ("keep £20 of £50") versus LOSE framings ("lose £30") activated the amygdala when subjects followed the frame, while subjects who resisted framing showed more activity in conflict-monitoring and frontal regions. "Losses" also hurt more than "costs": a small fee framed as a surcharge for credit cards provokes resistance that a "cash discount" does not.

Framing governs public policy through **defaults**. Eric Johnson and Daniel Goldstein showed organ-donation consent rates near 100% in countries where donation is the default you must opt out of (Austria) versus 12% in a culturally similar opt-in neighbor (Germany) — a life-and-death outcome set by a checkbox. Richard Larrick and Jack Soll showed the MPG frame misleads: upgrading a 12-mpg car to 14 saves more fuel than upgrading a 30-mpg car to 40, which "gallons per 1,000 miles" makes obvious. Since some frame is unavoidable, the honest move is to choose the frame that supports better decisions — and to distrust any preference that dissolves when the problem is reworded.

## In Practice
- Before reacting to a frightening rare risk, write down the actual probability in one consistent format and ask whether your alarm tracks the number or the vividness of the image.
- Adopt one risk policy this week and apply it everywhere: take the maximum affordable insurance deductible, decline extended warranties, and check investment balances no more than quarterly.
- Facing any small favorable gamble, ask "would I accept a hundred of these?" — if yes, take this one, and remind yourself "you win a few, you lose a few."
- Review your holdings and commitments while ignoring purchase price and money already spent: keep only what you would buy or start today, and realize losses when taxes reward it.
- When a decision turns out badly, judge it by the information you had at the time; refuse hindsight-fueled regret, and expect regret itself to fade faster than you predict.
- Reframe important choices at least twice before deciding — restate survival rates as mortality rates, costs as losses, defaults as active choices — and put competing options side by side rather than evaluating each alone.
