# Prospect Theory: Losses Loom Larger

## Key Ideas
- For 300 years, economics followed Bernoulli in assuming people evaluate outcomes by the total wealth they produce; Kahneman and Tversky showed we actually evaluate *changes* — gains and losses relative to a reference point.
- Prospect theory rests on three principles: outcomes are judged relative to a reference point, sensitivity to changes diminishes as they grow, and losses hurt roughly twice as much as equivalent gains please.
- Loss aversion creates the endowment effect: merely owning something raises its value, so sellers routinely demand about double what buyers will pay for the identical item.
- Because we overweight small probabilities and pay a premium for certainty, risk attitudes follow a fourfold pattern: we buy lottery tickets and insurance, settle lawsuits we are winning, and gamble recklessly when all options are bad.
- Goals function as reference points: falling short registers as a loss, which is why cab drivers quit early on busy days and professional golfers putt measurably better to avoid bogey than to make birdie.
- Negativity dominates broadly — threats are processed faster than opportunities, and stable relationships need good interactions to outnumber bad ones by about five to one.

## Bernoulli's Error: We Respond to Changes, Not States of Wealth

In 1738 Daniel Bernoulli proposed that people choose among gambles by the **expected utility** of wealth, not its expected dollar value, and that utility grows ever more slowly as wealth rises. This elegantly explained risk aversion and insurance, and it anchored economic theory for nearly three centuries. It is also wrong in a fundamental way.

Bernoulli's model evaluates only final *states* of wealth. Consider two people who today each have $5 million: Jack got there from $1 million yesterday, Jill from $9 million. Bernoulli's theory says they should be equally happy — obviously false. Jack is elated and Jill despondent, because what matters is the **change** from where each started, not the amount itself.

A second case: Anthony has $1 million, Betty has $4 million. Both face a choice between a sure $2 million and an even gamble between $1 million and $4 million. Utility theory says identical wealth options should produce identical preferences. In reality Anthony likely takes the sure thing (he doubles his wealth) while Betty may gamble (the "sure" option is a certain loss of half her fortune). Their reference points — their current wealth — flip the psychology of the identical choice.

Kahneman calls the field's long failure to notice this **theory-induced blindness**: once you accept a theory as a working tool, its flaws become extraordinarily hard to see, because every anomaly gets explained away rather than counted against it. Utility theory had no way to represent Jill's misery or Betty's sense of loss, so for generations no one asked the question.

The fix Kahneman and Tversky published in 1979 — **prospect theory** — kept Bernoulli's insight about diminishing sensitivity but redefined the carriers of value as gains and losses from a reference point. That single move, plus one new fact about the asymmetry between the two, reorganized the entire study of risky choice and eventually earned the Nobel Prize.

## Three Principles, One Asymmetry: The Architecture of Prospect Theory

Prospect theory is built on three features of System 1's evaluation machinery.

**Reference dependence.** Outcomes are experienced as gains or losses relative to a neutral **reference point** — usually the status quo, sometimes an expectation or entitlement. The same lukewarm water feels hot to a hand coming from ice water and cold to a hand coming from hot water; money works the same way.

**Diminishing sensitivity.** The subjective difference between $900 and $1,000 is far smaller than between $100 and $200, and the same holds for losses. Value curves flatten as amounts grow in either direction.

**Loss aversion.** Losses are weighted more heavily than gains — the value function is steeper on the loss side, with a kink at the reference point. Offered a coin flip that loses $100 on tails, most people demand around $200 on heads before accepting. Across many measurements the **loss-aversion ratio** falls between 1.5 and 2.5, roughly 2:1. As Kahneman puts it, "losses loom larger than gains." Evolution explains why: organisms that treated threats as more urgent than opportunities left more descendants.

These principles jointly predict the classic pattern: people are **risk averse** for gains (a sure $900 beats a 90% chance of $1,000, because the sure thing's value exceeds the diminished value of the larger amount) but **risk seeking** for losses (a 90% chance of losing $1,000 is preferred to a sure loss of $900, because the sure loss hurts disproportionately and diminishing sensitivity blunts the larger loss).

Kahneman is candid about the theory's own blind spots. Prospect theory cannot handle disappointment (a lost 90% chance to win feels worse than a lost 1% chance, though both end at zero) or **regret**, the sting of comparing an outcome to what a different choice would have produced. Simpler theories that explain more usually win anyway — a lesson about science as much as about choice.

## The Endowment Effect: Owning Something Raises Its Price

Richard Thaler, then a graduate student collecting anomalies, noticed a professor who would never pay more than $35 for a bottle of wine yet refused to sell bottles from his cellar for $100. Utility theory says one number should govern both; Thaler saw loss aversion at work and named the result the **endowment effect** — giving up what you own is coded as a loss, acquiring the same thing as a mere gain, and losses are weighted more.

The decisive experiment used coffee mugs at Cornell. Half the students received a mug and could sell; the other half could buy. Standard economics predicts mugs flow to whoever values them most, so about half should trade. Actual trading volume was roughly half that, because **sellers demanded about twice what buyers offered** (roughly $7 versus $3.50). Ownership itself had moved the reference point. A third group, "choosers," who could pick either a mug or cash, valued mugs like buyers — confirming that it is giving up possession, not the pleasure of having a mug, that inflates sellers' prices.

The effect has boundaries. It applies to goods **held for use** — the mug, the wine, your house at a price above what you paid — not to goods **held for exchange**. Shoe merchants feel no pain handing over shoes, and traders in experimental markets behave like Econs. Experience matters too: John List found that seasoned sports-memorabilia traders showed little or no endowment effect, while novices showed it strongly. And desperately poor people show none for money-relevant goods, for a grim reason: living below their reference point, they experience every choice as selecting among losses.

The same asymmetry poisons negotiations and reforms. Concessions I make are my losses and your gains, so both sides feel they are giving up more than they receive. Any reform that creates winners and losers meets outsized resistance, because potential losers fight roughly twice as hard as potential winners push — a force that biases institutions toward the status quo and explains why reforms so often grandfather existing stakeholders.

## The Fourfold Pattern: How Probabilities Get Distorted

Expected utility says a 5% chance should carry 5% of the weight in a decision. It does not. People overweight small probabilities and underweight moderate-to-high ones, and the two ends of the scale carry special power.

The **possibility effect**: moving from 0% to 5% creates a possibility that did not exist, and we pay far more for it than 5% warrants — this sells lottery tickets. The **certainty effect**: moving from 95% to 100% eliminates worry, and we pay heavily for that too. A 95% chance to win $1 million is disturbingly *not* worth 95% of a sure million. Maurice Allais embarrassed a room of eminent economists in 1952 with choice problems (the **Allais paradox**) showing that even they violated their own axioms near certainty.

Kahneman and Tversky measured **decision weights** directly: a 1% probability behaves like a weight of about 5.5; 10% like 18.6; 90% like only 71.2; 99% like 91.2. The gaps at the extremes are the possibility and certainty effects in numbers.

Crossing these probability distortions with gains versus losses yields the **fourfold pattern** of risk attitudes:

1. **High probability of gain** (95% chance to win $10,000): fear of disappointment makes us **risk averse** — we accept unfavorable settlements. Plaintiffs with strong cases settle cheap.
2. **High probability of loss** (95% chance to lose $10,000): the sure loss is intolerable, so we turn **risk seeking** — desperate long-shot gambles. Defendants facing likely defeat litigate on; failing companies double down. This is where losing wars are prolonged and small losses become catastrophes.
3. **Low probability of gain** (5% chance to win $10,000): hope of a large win makes us **risk seeking** — lotteries thrive.
4. **Low probability of loss** (5% chance to lose $10,000): fear of a large loss makes us **risk averse** — we buy insurance at prices well above expected value.

The pattern is costly and exploitable: anyone facing you in repeated negotiations can profit from your certainty premium when you are winning and your gambling streak when you are losing.

## Goals Are Reference Points: Cab Drivers and Golfers

Reference points are not only the status quo — any goal becomes one, and falling short of a goal is a loss while exceeding it is a diminished gain. Two field studies make the point with real money on the line.

New York City cab drivers appear to set a daily income **target**. On busy, rainy days when hourly earnings are high, many quit early once the target is reached; on slow days they drive long hours chasing it. This is exactly backwards from income maximization — they should work longest when the hourly wage is best — but it follows directly once the daily goal is the reference point and stopping short of it feels like a loss.

Economists Devin Pope and Maurice Schweitzer analyzed more than 2.5 million putts by professional golfers. For any given distance and difficulty, par is the reference point: a bogey is a loss, a birdie merely a gain. Loss aversion predicts more effort and focus when putting to avoid bogey than when putting for birdie — and the data confirm it. Par putts succeeded about **3.6% more often** than equivalent birdie putts. Had the pros putted for birdie as well as they putted for par, top players like Tiger Woods would have earned roughly a million dollars more per season. Even elite performers under maximal incentives cannot switch off the asymmetry.

Kahneman folds these findings into a broader claim of **negativity dominance**. Brains detect threats faster than opportunities: a single angry face pops out of a crowd of happy ones, but a happy face does not pop out of angry ones, and fearful stimuli reach the amygdala even when shown too briefly for conscious awareness. Bad information is processed more thoroughly than good, bad reputations form faster than good ones, and John Gottman found lasting marriages need positive interactions to outnumber negative ones by about **five to one**. Loss aversion is one expression of a mind organized to treat bad as stronger than good.

## In Practice
- Before any significant decision, name the reference point you are implicitly using — status quo, expectation, or goal — and ask how the choice would look from a different one.
- Apply the 2:1 test to small risks: if a gamble's upside is at least twice its downside and you will face many such gambles, take it rather than paying the loss-aversion tax on each one.
- When selling a possession, ask what you would pay to buy it today at market price; if your asking price is far higher, the endowment effect is talking, not the asset's value.
- Locate yourself in the fourfold pattern before settling or litigating, insuring or gambling: check whether you are overpaying for certainty (likely gain), taking a reckless long shot (likely loss), or overweighting a tiny probability in either direction.
- Set goals deliberately, because they become reference points: a slightly stretching, specific target recruits loss aversion in your favor, while a padded target invites quitting early like a rained-on cab driver.
- In negotiations, frame your asks around the other side's reference point — present terms as protecting them from losses rather than denying them gains, and expect any concession you request to feel twice as large to them as it looks to you.
