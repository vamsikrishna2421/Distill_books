# Three Razors: Inversion, Occam, and Hanlon

## Key Ideas
- **Inversion** solves problems backward: instead of asking how to succeed, assume the goal (or its opposite) and reason back to what must be true — or identify what guarantees failure and avoid it.
- Edward Bernays sold cigarettes to women not by improving the pitch but by inverting the question — engineering the social conditions under which women would *want* to smoke — proving inversion's power and its ethical dangers.
- Charlie Munger's career rests on the inverted goal: consistently avoiding stupidity beats intermittently chasing brilliance, because subtraction of errors is more reliable than addition of genius.
- **Occam's Razor** says that when competing explanations fit the facts, the one with the fewest assumptions is most likely correct — because every added assumption is another independent chance to be wrong.
- **Hanlon's Razor** says to prefer incompetence over malice as an explanation for harm; malice requires more assumptions (intent plus action), and Kahneman and Tversky's Linda problem shows why our minds systematically overweight such conjunctions anyway.
- All three razors are probability heuristics, not laws: some things really are complex, and some people really are malicious — the razors set your default, not your final verdict.

## Invert, Always Invert: Approach Problems Backward

The nineteenth-century German mathematician Carl Jacobi cracked notoriously hard problems with a single maxim: "invert, always invert." Many problems that resist a frontal assault dissolve when you restate them in reverse. **Inversion** is the deliberate habit of starting your thinking from the end — from the outcome, or from the outcome's opposite — rather than from where you stand.

Inversion runs in two distinct modes. **Mode one: assume the conclusion and reason backward.** Take the thing you want to prove or achieve, treat it as already true (or already false), and ask what conditions would have to hold for that to be the case. This converts a vague forward question ("how do I get there?") into a concrete diagnostic one ("what must exist for 'there' to be real, and which of those conditions are missing?").

**Mode two: define failure and avoid it.** Instead of asking how to guarantee success — usually impossible — ask what would guarantee failure, then systematically eliminate those things. You cannot ensure a great life, but you can reliably avoid the known wreckers: debt spirals, addictions, chronic dishonesty, neglected health. Avoiding the downside is more tractable than engineering the upside, because failure modes are fewer and better documented than success paths.

A structured application is Kurt Lewin's **force field analysis**: identify the problem and your objective, list the forces pushing toward the goal and the forces resisting it, then work on both sides. The inverted insight is that removing an obstacle is often cheaper and more durable than adding more push. Most people only ever press harder on the accelerator; inversion reminds you to check the brakes.

Inversion feels unnatural because our default is additive, forward-marching thought. That is exactly why it is valuable: it surfaces the errors, obstacles, and hidden assumptions that forward thinking glides past.

## Torches of Freedom and the Avoidance of Stupidity

The most famous — and most disturbing — demonstration of inversion comes from Edward Bernays, the father of public relations. In 1929, the American Tobacco Company wanted women to smoke Lucky Strikes, but a strong social taboo kept women from smoking in public. A conventional marketer would ask, "How do we advertise cigarettes to women?" Bernays inverted it: "What would society have to look like for women to smoke on their own?"

He then set about building that society. He staged a contingent of fashionable young women lighting cigarettes — branded **"torches of freedom"** — during New York's Easter Sunday parade, framing smoking as an act of feminist emancipation and making front-page news. He tied cigarettes to the slimness ideal, positioning them as a substitute for sweets. When women resisted the brand because its green package clashed with their wardrobes, he didn't change the package — he ran a campaign to make green the fashionable color of the season, enlisting designers, galas, and magazines. Bernays never argued anyone into smoking; he re-engineered the environment so smoking sold itself. The case shows both the extraordinary leverage of backward thinking and why that leverage demands ethical restraint.

The same logic built one of finance's great innovations. John Bogle inverted the fund industry's question — "how do we beat the market?" — into "how do we avoid everything that makes investors underperform it?" Stripping out high fees, churn, and manager error produced the index fund.

Charlie Munger frames inversion as a life strategy: rather than striving to be brilliant, work at being **consistently not stupid**. He observes that people like him and Buffett have gained enormous long-term advantage this way. Brilliance is scarce, situational, and unrepeatable; the catalog of standard stupidities — overconfidence, envy, leverage, incentives ignored — is stable and learnable. Amateurs win by seeking spectacular wins; professionals win by not losing.

## Occam's Razor: Fewer Assumptions, Higher Probability

**Occam's Razor**, named for the fourteenth-century friar William of Ockham, holds that entities should not be multiplied beyond necessity: when two explanations account for the same facts equally well, prefer the one with fewer assumptions.

This is not aesthetic preference — it is arithmetic. Every explanation is a chain of assumptions, and each assumption carries an independent probability of being wrong. Multiply those probabilities together and the compound likelihood of a many-assumption story collapses fast, while a lean explanation stays plausible. Simpler theories are also easier to falsify, understand, and act on, which makes them better working tools even before you ask whether they're true. Medicine compresses the razor into an adage: when you hear hoofbeats, think horses, not zebras.

Carl Sagan's invisible dragon illustrates the failure mode the razor guards against. Someone claims a dragon lives in their garage; every test you propose is met with a new assumption — it's invisible, it floats so it leaves no footprints, its fire is heatless. Each patch saves the claim at the cost of another unverifiable assumption, until the theory explains everything and predicts nothing. Conspiracy theories work the same way: they survive only by stacking assumptions that each must independently hold.

The razor has real limits, and the book insists on them. It is a **probability heuristic, not a law**: the simplest explanation is the best *bet*, not a guaranteed truth. Some phenomena are irreducibly complex — quantum mechanics is not simple, and no simpler theory matches its predictions. The test is never simplicity alone but simplicity *among explanations that fit all the evidence*. Einstein's dictum captures the balance: make things as simple as possible, but no simpler. When a simple story requires you to ignore data, the extra assumption you're refusing to make is the one you actually need.

Used well, Occam's Razor is a triage tool. Faced with competing explanations, you check the cheap, low-assumption one first — not because zebras don't exist, but because starting with horses saves time and is usually right.

## Hanlon's Razor: Don't Assume Malice When Incompetence Will Do

**Hanlon's Razor** — attributed to Robert Hanlon, with an earlier echo in Robert Heinlein's fiction — instructs: never attribute to malice what is adequately explained by stupidity, carelessness, or incompetence. The driver who cut you off is far more likely distracted than out to get you. The colleague who dropped your project is far more likely overwhelmed than plotting your downfall.

This is Occam's Razor applied to human behavior. Explaining harm by incompetence requires one assumption: the person erred. Explaining it by malice requires at least two: the person acted *and* intended the harm, often with a hidden motive as a third. Fewer assumptions, higher prior probability — and in the real world, error is simply far more abundant than villainy.

Yet our intuition runs the other way, and Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky showed exactly why. In their famous **Linda problem**, subjects read a sketch of Linda — 31, single, outspoken, philosophy major, deeply concerned with social justice — then judged whether it was more probable that she is (a) a bank teller or (b) a bank teller *and* active in the feminist movement. The overwhelming majority chose (b), which is logically impossible: a conjunction of two conditions can never be more probable than either condition alone. This **conjunction fallacy** shows that we judge probability by narrative fit, not by logic. A story with intent — a villain, a motive — feels *more* plausible precisely because it has more vivid detail, when every added detail actually makes it less likely. Assuming malice is the conjunction fallacy in the wild.

The cost of getting this wrong can be historic. In 408 AD, the Roman emperor Honorius interpreted his brilliant general Stilicho's negotiation with the Goth leader Alaric as treachery and had him executed. Stilicho had likely been acting pragmatically, not maliciously; his death drove thousands of his soldiers to defect to Alaric, who sacked Rome two years later. Assuming the worst manufactured the very betrayal Honorius feared.

The razor is a default, not naïveté. Malice exists, and repeated "mistakes" that always break one way deserve suspicion. But leading with charity keeps you rational, preserves relationships, and — unlike paranoia — costs almost nothing when wrong.

## In Practice
- Before starting your next project, run a pre-mortem: assume it failed completely, list the five most likely causes, and build your plan around eliminating them.
- Take one stalled goal and invert it — stop asking "how do I achieve this?" and spend thirty minutes listing everything currently guaranteeing you *won't*, then remove one obstacle instead of adding effort.
- Keep a "stupidity checklist" of your recurring errors (overcommitting, deciding while angry, skipping due diligence) and review it before any significant decision — aim to be not-dumb before trying to be smart.
- When you face competing explanations this week, count the assumptions each requires and investigate the leanest one first; only move to the complex story when the simple one fails the evidence.
- The next time someone's action harms or annoys you, write down the incompetence explanation before the malice one, and act on the charitable version unless a pattern proves otherwise.
- When a theory you hold needs a fresh patch every time it's challenged, treat that as a signal to discard it, not defend it.
