# Wax in Your Ears: Engineering Around Yourself

## Key Ideas
- Knowing about probability biases does not cure them: Taleb admits he is as fooled by randomness as anyone, so the only reliable fix is to engineer your environment rather than train your willpower.
- Neuroscientist Antonio Damasio showed that emotion is not the enemy of rational choice but its engine — patients with damaged emotional circuits could reason perfectly yet could not make even trivial decisions.
- B.F. Skinner's pigeons developed elaborate superstitious rituals from randomly timed food, and traders develop the same "ticks" — causal beliefs manufactured from noise.
- The media is a noise-amplification machine: journalists are paid to attach a cause to every wiggle in the market, manufacturing explanations for movements that are statistically meaningless.
- The practical answer is self-binding, like Ulysses tied to the mast: no news, no real-time price quotes, rare portfolio checks, and hard rules (such as automatic stop-losses) that remove decisions from your emotional self.
- Follow Carneades' skeptical hygiene: treat all opinions as provisional, recognize that your beliefs are path-dependent accidents, never "marry" a position, and — like Soros — reverse yourself without embarrassment.

## Why Knowing Better Doesn't Make You Behave Better

The central confession of the book's third part: Taleb, a professional student of randomness, is fooled by it daily. He knows the mathematics of rare events cold, yet still flinches at a losing day, still feels the pull of a good story over a dry probability. Awareness of a bias and immunity to it are entirely different things.

The reason is architectural. The rational, statistical mind is a thin recent layer sitting on top of an emotional machine built for the savanna, and when the two conflict, emotion usually wins. You cannot lecture your amygdala. Advice of the form "just be more disciplined" fails for the same reason diets fail — it asks the weaker system to police the stronger one.

**Damasio's evidence** makes the point sharper and stranger. In *Descartes' Error*, neuroscientist Antonio Damasio studied patients whose emotional circuitry was damaged (often ventromedial prefrontal lesions) but whose logical intelligence was intact. Far from becoming perfect Spock-like reasoners, they became incapable of deciding anything. Asked to pick an appointment time, one patient could enumerate pros and cons indefinitely without ever choosing. Emotion, it turns out, is the lubricant of decision — it is what terminates deliberation and commits you to an option.

This cuts both ways. We literally cannot decide without feelings, so a purely rational relationship to markets and risk is not just difficult — it is neurologically impossible. The feelings will fire whether you approve of them or not: fear at a red screen, elation at a green one, the itch to act on a headline.

The conclusion Taleb draws is the theme of this whole chapter: since you cannot upgrade the hardware, change the inputs. Do not try to feel nothing when you see a scary price; arrange your life so you do not see the price. The treatment for bias is structural, not psychological. Self-knowledge is worth having, but only because it tells you where to build the walls.

## Superstitious Pigeons and Gamblers' Ticks

In 1948, psychologist **B.F. Skinner** put hungry pigeons in boxes and delivered food at random intervals, disconnected from anything the birds did. The pigeons nonetheless developed elaborate rituals — head-swinging, hopping, turning counterclockwise — each bird apparently convinced that whatever it was doing when food last arrived had caused the food. Random reinforcement manufactured causal belief in a pigeon brain, mechanically and reliably.

Human brains run the same algorithm with better vocabulary. Traders develop **gamblers' ticks**: the lucky tie worn on a winning streak, the desk arranged just so, the cab route taken on the day of a big profit. Taleb confesses his own — catching himself avoiding certain taxi stands or preferring certain seats after good trading days, behaviors his statistical mind knows are absurd but his pigeon-mind keeps generating. The mechanism that produced Skinner's turning birds produces market "wisdom": a pattern paid off twice, therefore it is a law.

The lesson is not that superstition can be eliminated — it can't, because the causality-inferring machinery runs automatically below awareness. The lesson is to grade superstitions by cost. A lucky tie is free; Taleb tolerates his harmless ticks with amusement. A superstition becomes lethal when it is dressed in the language of analysis and given capital: the trader who "knows" a pattern works because it worked in his short, noise-dominated sample is Skinner's pigeon with leverage. (This is survivorship bias and data-snooping from chapter 3 seen from the inside — what pattern-hunting feels like to the hunter.)

The honest posture is to assume by default that any regularity you have personally noticed in a random-rich environment is a tick until proven otherwise. Your brain will keep proposing causes; your procedures must be the filter that refuses to fund them.

## The Media Manufactures Causes for Noise

Journalists face a structural problem: markets move every day, most movement is statistically meaningless noise, but "nothing happened today" is not a publishable story. So the press manufactures causes. The Dow falls and it is "worries about interest rates"; it rises the next morning and, with the same facts in the world, it is "optimism about the economy." Often the identical piece of news is cited to explain movements in opposite directions. These explanations are produced after the move, fitted to it, and would flunk any test of prediction.

Taleb's point is not merely that this commentary is worthless — it is actively harmful, for two reasons. First, it trains you in bad inference. Every "market fell because X" story is a small lesson that every fluctuation has a discoverable cause, which is precisely the pigeon-logic error. Consuming financial news is practicing the bias.

Second, it hijacks your emotions at high frequency. Recall the dentist's portfolio arithmetic from chapter 4: over short intervals, what you observe is almost pure noise, and because losses hurt roughly twice as much as gains please (the loss-aversion asymmetry from chapter 5), frequent exposure to noise produces net emotional damage even when the underlying strategy is winning. The media delivers that noise hourly, wrapped in narrative urgency that makes ignoring it feel irresponsible.

The value of information scales with its age: a fact that would still matter in a year is worth reading; a fact that expires by Friday is almost certainly noise wearing a press badge. Taleb's provocation is that a weekly or monthly digest dominated by old news would make you better informed, in every sense that matters, than a real-time feed. Timeliness — the media's core product — is close to the opposite of signal.

## Wax in Your Ears: The Ulysses Design

Ulysses knew he could not resist the Sirens' song, so he did not try to. He plugged his crew's ears with wax and had himself lashed to the mast. This is **self-binding**: making the disciplined choice once, in a calm moment, in a way that removes the choice from your future, weaker self. It is the master template for everything Taleb actually does about his own irrationality.

His personal noise-avoidance architecture, concretely: no television news; no newspapers for market-relevant information (he permits himself journalism as entertainment, read like gossip, never as input to decisions); no real-time price quotes on his screen — the prices of his own positions are hidden from him during the day; and profit-and-loss statements checked at rare, scheduled intervals rather than continuously. Each rule is the same move — engineering the information environment so the emotional system is never presented with the stimulus it cannot handle.

The statistical justification comes straight from the dentist table: at high checking frequency you see randomness, at low frequency you see performance. So the rare P&L check is not laziness or denial — it is sampling at the frequency where the signal-to-noise ratio is actually favorable. The daily checker feels more diligent and knows less.

Self-binding extends beyond information into action. A **stop-loss** decided in advance — an automatic exit at a predetermined loss point — is a mast-lashing: the calm self commits the panicked future self to a decision it would otherwise agonize over and probably botch. The same logic covers any rule that converts a recurring emotional decision into a one-time structural one: automatic investment contributions, mandates you cannot override intraday, brokers instructed not to call you with "opportunities."

The philosophy underneath is a kind of humble stoicism: dignity does not require winning the internal war, only arranging not to fight it on the enemy's terrain. Taleb is openly unheroic about this. He does not claim to have conquered his impulses; he claims to have starved them of triggers.

## Carneades and the Hygiene of Loosely Held Beliefs

In 155 BC, Rome received the Greek philosopher **Carneades**, head of the skeptical Academy. On one day he delivered a masterful oration in praise of justice; the next day, before the same audience, he argued the opposite case just as convincingly. Cato the Elder was scandalized and hustled the Greek delegation out of Rome — young Romans should not learn that positions are arguable. But Carneades' demonstration carried the idea Taleb builds on: nothing is certain, and **probability** — in its ancient sense of degrees of provisional, actionable opinion — is the best we get. Beliefs should be held as working hypotheses with confidence levels, never as identities.

Modern minds fail this standard through the **path dependence of beliefs**: what you currently think depends on the order in which ideas and experiences arrived, not just on the evidence itself. The position you happened to buy first, the theory you happened to publish, the view you defended at dinner — each becomes an endowment, and abandoning it feels like a loss (loss aversion again, applied to opinions). Taleb's test: if you would not enter the position afresh today at today's price, holding it is pure attachment. Rationality demands your beliefs be indifferent to their own history.

Hence the trading maxim: never **marry your positions**. The graveyard of traders is filled with people who became loyal to a view — who added to losers to prove a point, who needed the market to vindicate them. Corporate and academic life reward consistency, so we import that reward system into domains where it kills.

**George Soros** is Taleb's exemplar of the opposite temperament. Steeped in Popper (chapter 5), Soros treats every position as a falsifiable conjecture, walks around proclaiming his own fallibility, and reverses himself without a flicker of embarrassment — famously flipping from bearish to buying during the 1987 crash aftermath. He is strengthened, not diminished, by contradicting yesterday's self. That is the complete skeptical hygiene: opinions held loosely, updated ruthlessly, and never confused with who you are.

## In Practice

- Delete real-time price and portfolio apps from your phone this week; schedule a single recurring monthly (or quarterly) review, and look at balances only then.
- Replace daily financial news with slow media: choose one weekly or longer-cycle source, and skip any story whose relevance expires within days — treat breaking market commentary as entertainment, never as decision input.
- Write your exit rules before you enter: for any investment or project, pre-commit the loss point or failure condition at which you quit, and automate the exit where possible so your future panicked self isn't consulted.
- Audit one ritual: pick a habit you believe "works," ask whether the evidence is more than a few coincidental successes, then keep it only if it costs nothing.
- Apply the fresh-start test to your largest holding or strongest opinion: if you wouldn't buy it or adopt it today from scratch, reduce or drop it — the history of how you got here is not evidence.
- Practice one public reversal: identify a stated position the evidence has turned against, and change your mind out loud, treating the reversal as competence rather than defeat.
