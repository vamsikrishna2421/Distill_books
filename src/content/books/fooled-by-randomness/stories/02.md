# Skewness: Why Being Right Isn't the Point

Second night with Taleb, friend — and I promised you a confession that sounds like madness.

A weekly trading meeting, New York. Taleb is asked the standard question: *where's the market going?* He answers honestly: up, most likely — he'd put it around seventy percent. Then a colleague, smelling blood, points out the absurdity: *but Nassim... you're heavily SHORT. You're betting millions on a fall you just said probably won't happen.*

The room laughs. Taleb doesn't. Because the room is confusing two things that rule your money, your career, and your health — and almost nobody can tell them apart.

## The bet that contradicts itself

![A lone trader standing calm in a chaotic trading floor, screens red and colleagues shouting around him](/story-art/fooled-by-randomness/02-trader.jpg)

::: guess
How can it be rational to say "the market will probably rise" and bet heavily that it falls — at the same time?
---
Do the arithmetic the room skipped. Say there's a 70% chance the market drifts *up* about 1% — that contributes +0.7. And a 30% chance it *falls* — but falls hard, say 10% — contributing −3. The **expectation** — each outcome times its probability — is decisively negative. The most *likely* single outcome is up; the *expected value* is down; and money is paid on expectation, not on likelihood. That is the whole secret: **probability asks how often; expectation asks how often times how much** — and the moment payoffs are lopsided, the two part ways completely. You can be wrong 90% of the time and grow rich, if your rare wins dwarf your frequent losses. You can be right 95% of the time and be a menace, if your rare wrongs are catastrophic. Analysts graded on "was the call right?" are being graded on journalism. The entire game is: make money when right, lose little when wrong — *frequency of correctness is not the point and never was.*
:::

## The insurance seller's beautiful track record

Now watch how this asymmetry poisons the thing everyone trusts most: the track record.

Imagine a strategy earning a steady dollar every month, smooth as silk — low volatility, gorgeous ratios, five straight profitable years. What could be safer? But the strategy is, in effect, *selling insurance against a rare disaster* — one that strikes once a decade and costs five hundred. The single number that decides whether this strategy is profitable *at all* — the size of the rare loss — **has not yet appeared in the sample.** The beautiful record is measuring noise.

Taleb calls it the **rare-event problem**: rare events cannot be estimated from experience *precisely because they are rare*. And the trap has a cruel tilt — the strategies that *look* best in short windows are disproportionately the ones quietly shorting disaster, because harvesting small premiums is exactly what produces smooth returns. So flip your instincts, friend: **the smoother and more consistent a return stream looks, the more suspicious you should be about what sleeps in the tail.** History books eventually record the rare event. Monthly performance reviews never do.

## Bleed or blow up

Every asymmetric life sits on one side of a trade, and Taleb names the sides without mercy.

The **blowup** profile: small, frequent wins — a rare, ruinous loss. Sell the insurance, collect the premiums, enjoy daily applause, annual bonuses, a swelling reputation... until one morning erases everything, sometimes including the firm. The **bleed** profile — Taleb's own: buy cheap **out-of-the-money options** that pay nothing in normal times and pay explosively in a crisis. Downside capped at the small premiums; upside open-ended. The price is agony by drip-feed: losing a little *almost every day*, sometimes for years — wrong most days, most weeks, most quarters, while colleagues snicker and management asks pointed questions. And since losses hurt roughly twice as much as gains please, the bleed hurts more than the math says it should.

Here is the sting: institutions are *built* to reward the blowup side. Bonuses are annual and never clawed back — the trader who detonates in year seven keeps six years of bonuses; the payoff structure of employment itself is short the rare event. Which is exactly why the patient bleed side can be profitable at all: the market chronically overcrowds the comfortable side of the trade. The question for your own life: which of your smooth, reliable income streams is secretly selling insurance — and can you survive the claim?

## The elegant man who bought every dip

Carlos was everything a bank wants in a trader: cultivated, polished economist, impeccable manners. From 1992 to mid-1998 he made money almost every year in emerging-market bonds, and his signature move never failed: **when the market dipped, he bought more.** The market always came back. His employers credited his brilliant economics.

::: guess
Summer 1998: Russia defaults, and emerging-market bonds collapse. Carlos's signature move meets its first true storm. What did he do — and how did it end?
---
He did what six years of applause had trained him to do: **averaged down.** Bonds fell; he bought. Fell further; he bought more, declaring the market "irrational" and his positions "value." By the time he was fired, he had lost *more than everything he had earned in his entire career* — and John next door, running the same script with leverage, turned years of $250-million profits into roughly $600 million gone in days. Taleb's verdict is the chapter's darkest idea: **survival of the least fit.** These weren't skilled men hit by bad luck — they were *lucky men selected by a random cycle*, promoted for the exact traits that guaranteed eventual ruin: certainty without doubt, marrying positions, no exit plan set in advance, rewriting the story after each loss ("I'm a long-term investor now"), and treating a falling position's cheapness as a reason to buy more. The trait that made the streak possible made the ruin certain. Write your exit *before* you enter — because after you enter, the storyteller takes over.
:::

## The median isn't the message

One more asymmetry, friend — and this one saved a great scientist's spirit. Paleontologist Stephen Jay Gould, diagnosed with a rare abdominal cancer, was told the median survival was **eight months**. Most people hear that as a sentence: *I have eight months.*

Gould read the statistics instead — and saw the distribution was strongly **right-skewed**: yes, half of patients died within eight months... but the other half stretched across *many years*, and a young, optimistic, well-treated patient sat squarely in that long tail. His essay became the slogan: **"the median isn't the message."** He lived another twenty years.

The lesson runs everywhere: "the market usually rises," "profitable in most years," "the average patient survives eight months" — all *how often* statements smuggled in as *how much* answers. Under asymmetry, the typical outcome and the expected outcome can point in opposite directions — a bet can lose in its most representative scenario and still be magnificent, because its rare wins are enormous. Whenever anyone quotes you an average or a median, ask one question first: *is the distribution symmetric?* If not, demand the full shape. The summary may be an inversion of the truth.

## Before you go

So: act on expectation, never on likelihood; distrust smooth track records the way you'd distrust a too-calm sea; choose the bleed over the blowup where you can bear it; write the exit before the entry; and never let a median masquerade as your message.

But a darker question now rises from tonight's graves, friend. Carlos and John had *years* of winning records — and the records meant nothing. So how much of any success is real? ## Key takeaways

- Probability asks how often; expectation asks how often times how much — act on the product, always.
- You can be wrong 90% of the time and rich, or right 95% and ruined — frequency of correctness is journalism.
- The rare-event problem: the smoother the track record, the more suspicious you should be of the unobserved tail.
- Bleed beats blowup: capped downside, open upside — and pre-commit to enduring long strings of small losses.
- Survival of the least fit: random regimes promote the doubt-free — write your exit plan before you enter.
- The median isn't the message: under skew, averages can invert the truth — demand the distribution's shape.
