# Wax in Your Ears: Engineering Around Yourself

Sixth night, friend — and it opens with the bravest sentence a expert can write. Taleb, professional student of randomness, master of the mathematics of rare events, confesses: *I am fooled by randomness daily.* He still flinches at a losing day. He still feels a good story tug harder than a dry probability.

If knowing the traps doesn't free you from them — and it doesn't — then everything we've learned these five nights comes down to tonight's question: **what does?**

The answer is three thousand years old, and it involves a ship, a mast, and a man who knew he couldn't trust himself.

## The man who couldn't lecture his amygdala

![An ancient Greek ship at sea, a man bound to the mast straining toward distant figures on the rocks while sailors row on undisturbed](/story-art/fooled-by-randomness/06-ulysses.jpg)

Why doesn't knowledge cure bias? Because the rational, statistical mind is a *thin, recent layer* sitting atop an emotional machine built for the savanna — and when they conflict, the old machine wins. You cannot lecture your amygdala. "Just be more disciplined" fails for the same reason diets fail: it asks the weaker system to police the stronger one.

And here neuroscience delivers a twist stranger than any philosophy. Antonio Damasio studied patients whose *emotional* circuits were damaged but whose logic was perfectly intact — surely, you'd think, ideal reasoners at last?

::: guess
Freed from emotion, with pure logic remaining — what happened to these patients' ability to make decisions?
---
**They couldn't decide anything at all.** Asked simply to pick an appointment time, one patient enumerated pros and cons *indefinitely* — endlessly rational, unable to ever conclude. Because emotion, it turns out, is not the enemy of decision — **emotion is what terminates deliberation and commits you.** It is the lubricant of choice. Which cuts both ways, friend: a purely rational relationship to risk isn't just difficult, it is *neurologically impossible*. Fear will fire at the red screen; elation at the green; the itch will come with every headline — whether you approve or not. So Taleb draws the only conclusion left, and it is the theme of the whole night: **you cannot upgrade the hardware — so change the inputs.** Don't try to feel nothing when you see a scary price. Arrange your life so you *don't see the price*. The treatment for bias is structural, never psychological; self-knowledge matters only because it tells you where to build the walls.
:::

## Skinner's pigeons and the lucky tie

In 1948, B.F. Skinner put hungry pigeons in boxes and dropped food at *random* intervals — connected to nothing the birds did. The pigeons went religious. One swung its head rhythmically; another hopped; another turned solemn counterclockwise circles — each bird evidently convinced that whatever it was doing when food last arrived had *caused* the food. Random rewards manufactured causal belief, mechanically, in a pigeon brain.

Your brain runs the same algorithm with better vocabulary. Traders wear the lucky tie from the winning streak; arrange the desk just so; take the cab route from the day of the big profit — Taleb confesses his own ticks, absurdities his statistical mind sees and his pigeon-mind keeps generating anyway. The mechanism cannot be deleted; it runs below awareness. So grade superstitions **by cost**: the lucky tie is free — keep it, smile at it. The lethal version is the tick dressed in the language of analysis and given capital — the trader who "knows" a pattern works because it worked twice in a noise-dominated sample is *Skinner's pigeon with leverage*. Default assumption, friend: any regularity you personally noticed in a random-rich environment is a tick until proven otherwise. Your brain will keep proposing causes. Your procedures must refuse to fund them.

## The cause factory

Now, the industrial version of the pigeon. Markets move every day; most movement is statistically meaningless; but *"nothing happened today"* is not a publishable story. So the press manufactures causes: the Dow falls on "interest rate worries," rises next morning — same facts in the world — on "economic optimism." Sometimes the *identical* headline explains movements in opposite directions. All of it fitted after the move; none of it survives a test of prediction.

And Taleb's charge is not that this is worthless — it's that it is **harmful, twice over**. First, it trains you in bad inference: every "market fell because X" is a little lesson that all fluctuations have discoverable causes — you are *practicing the pigeon error* with every article. Second, it hijacks your feelings at high frequency: remember the dentist — short-interval observations are nearly pure noise, losses cost double, so a real-time feed delivers guaranteed emotional damage wrapped in narrative urgency. The test that cuts through it all: **the value of information scales with its age.** A fact still relevant next year is worth reading; a fact that expires by Friday is noise wearing a press badge. A monthly digest of old news would leave you better informed — in every sense that matters — than the ticker.

## Lashed to the mast

Which brings us to the sailor. Ulysses, sailing home, knew the Sirens' song drove men to wreck themselves on the rocks — and knew *he could not resist it*. So he didn't try. **Wax in the crew's ears; his own body lashed to the mast.** The disciplined choice made once, calmly, in a way that *removed the choice* from his future, weaker self.

That is **self-binding**, and it is everything Taleb actually does about his own irrationality. His architecture, concretely: no television news. No newspapers as decision input (journalism as entertainment, read like gossip — fine). **No real-time quotes** — the prices of his own positions hidden from him during the day. Profit-and-loss checked at rare, *scheduled* intervals — because at high frequency you see randomness, at low frequency you see performance; the rare check isn't denial, it's sampling where the signal actually lives. The daily checker feels more diligent and knows less.

And the mast extends beyond information into action: the **stop-loss** written in advance is a lashing — the calm self committing the panicked future self to an exit it would otherwise botch. Automatic investment contributions. Mandates that can't be overridden intraday. Every rule that converts a recurring emotional decision into a one-time structural one. The philosophy underneath is humble, friend, and it is the night's heart: **dignity does not require winning the internal war — only arranging never to fight it on the enemy's terrain.** Taleb doesn't claim to have conquered his impulses. He claims to have starved them of triggers.

## The philosopher who argued both sides

One last teacher tonight — Rome, 155 BC. The Greek skeptic **Carneades** arrives and delivers a masterful oration in praise of justice. The crowd is moved. The next day, before the same audience, he argues the *opposite* case — just as convincingly. Cato the Elder, scandalized, has the Greek delegation hustled out of Rome: young Romans must not learn that positions are arguable.

But Carneades carried the seed Taleb builds on: nothing is certain; the best we get is *degrees of provisional, actionable opinion* — beliefs held as working hypotheses with confidence levels, **never as identities**. We fail this through the **path dependence of beliefs**: what you think now depends on the *order* things arrived — the position you bought first, the view you defended at dinner — each becomes an endowment, and dropping it feels like a loss. Loss aversion, applied to opinions. Taleb's fresh-start test slices clean through: **if you wouldn't enter this position — or adopt this belief — from scratch today, holding it is pure attachment.** Never marry a position; the traders' graveyard is filled with people who needed the market to vindicate them. And the exemplar, once more, is Soros: proclaiming his own fallibility, flipping from bearish to buying in the middle of the 1987 crash aftermath, *strengthened* by contradicting yesterday's self. Practice it small, friend: one public reversal, this month, of a position the evidence has turned against — spoken out loud, as competence, not defeat.

## Before you go

So: emotion decides, so govern the inputs; your noticed patterns are pigeon-ticks until proven; the news is a cause factory teaching you the error hourly; wax and mast beat willpower every time; and beliefs are rentals, never marriages.

One night remains — and it is not about markets at all. Because Taleb knows the truth that no architecture can prevent: someday, despite the wax, despite the mast, despite everything — **fortune will turn anyway.** The rare event finds everyone eventually. And when it does, there is exactly one thing chance cannot repossess. The Romans had a word for it. Tomorrow, the finale: what remains when the dice go against you.

Next time: **the dignity randomness cannot touch.**

## Key takeaways

- Awareness doesn't cure bias: the rational mind is a thin layer on older machinery — change the inputs, not the hardware.
- Damasio's patients: emotion is what lets you decide at all — a purely rational stance toward risk is neurologically impossible.
- Skinner's pigeons live in you: grade your rituals by cost, and treat self-noticed patterns as ticks until proven.
- News manufactures causes for noise and trains the error — the value of information scales with its age; go slow-media.
- Self-binding beats willpower: hide the prices, schedule rare reviews, automate exits — wax in ears, body to mast.
- Hold beliefs like Carneades, not marriages: fresh-start test everything, and reverse yourself out loud like Soros.
