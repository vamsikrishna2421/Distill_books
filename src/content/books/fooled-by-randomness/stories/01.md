# Solon's Warning: Judge the Process, Not the Outcome

A new fire tonight, friend — and this one begins twenty-five centuries ago, in the most dazzling throne room on earth.

The book is *Fooled by Randomness*, written by Nassim Taleb — a trader who spent his career betting on rare disasters and watching luckier men mock him... until the disasters arrived. His subject is the oldest con in the world, the one that needs no con man: **chance itself, dressed up as skill.** And his opening move is a story the Greeks told about the richest king who ever lived.

## The king who demanded to be called happy

![An ancient king in gold robes gesturing proudly across a torchlit treasury heaped with gold, while a plainly dressed old sage looks on unimpressed](/story-art/fooled-by-randomness/01-croesus.jpg)

King Croesus of Lydia — so rich his name still means wealth — once hosted Solon, the wise lawgiver of Athens. Croesus walked him through vault after vault of gold, then asked the question he asked every distinguished visitor, the question he had never once lost: *"Who is the happiest man you have ever seen?"*

::: guess
Solon looked at the richest man alive, standing on a mountain of gold, waiting for the obvious answer. What did he say?
---
He named an obscure Athenian, dead in battle, whose story had *ended well* — and told the astonished king: **"Call no man happy until he is dead."** Meaning: as long as the story is unfinished, whatever fortune gave, fortune can repossess. And fortune did: Croesus lost his son, his kingdom, and very nearly his life on a Persian pyre — where, the legend says, he cried Solon's name from the flames. Taleb takes this ancient warning and forges the book's master rule from it: **that which came by luck can leave by luck** — and therefore a *result*, however golden, tells you almost nothing by itself. What matters is never the outcome. It is the **process** that produced it, judged across all the ways the story *could* have gone.
:::

## The ten million you shouldn't envy

Now Taleb sharpens the blade with a thought experiment. Two men each have ten million dollars. One earned it over thirty years of dentistry. The other won it playing **Russian roulette** — one bullet, six chambers, a rich patron paying ten million per surviving pull.

The bank statements are *identical*. Are the fortunes equal?

Of course not — and here is the tool that shows why. Imagine the world splitting at every uncertain moment into **alternative histories**: all the paths events could have taken. In the dentist's alternative histories, he is almost always... a dentist with savings. In the roulette player's, **five of six histories contain a corpse.** The ten million you can see is one lucky draw from a distribution you can't see — and the quality of a decision is its average across *all* the histories, not the one that happened to happen. Taleb's trade demands this discipline mechanically: **Monte Carlo simulation**, a computer spinning thousands of possible paths for a strategy, revealing how it fares across the worlds — not just in the single world we lived.

Once you have this lens, friend, you cannot unsee it. The friend who put everything on one stock and doubled it: in how many neighboring worlds is he ruined? The executive praised for the bold bet that "paid off": what was the bet's *average* across the worlds where it didn't? Russian roulette with slow bullets is still Russian roulette.

## Nero and John

Taleb gives the lens two faces, living on the same street.

**Nero Tulip** is a trader who has seen the abyss. He earns well but never spectacularly — because he *caps his downside* ruthlessly, refuses trades that could ruin him however juicy the odds, keeps his savings in dull safety, and accepts looking mediocre in boom years. His neighbor **John** is a high-yield trader: leveraged to the sky, printing tens of millions, upgrading houses and cars while his wife wonders aloud why Nero doesn't try harder. For five straight years, John's bank statement humiliates Nero's.

::: guess
Then came the summer of 1998, and the worldwide bond blowup. What happened to each neighbor?
---
**John lost everything** — his fortune, his job, very nearly his mind — because his golden strategy had a bullet in one chamber all along, and 1998 was the chamber. He was never skilled, friend; he was *fitted to a streak* — adapted perfectly to a particular random regime, like a creature evolved for a climate that then changed. **Nero survived** — as he had survived every regime before — because survival was the thing his whole process optimized. That is the payoff of Solon's warning in modern dress: over a long enough life, the resilient modest earner beats the brilliant-looking gambler, not sometimes but *almost always*, because the gambler's alternative histories keep collecting corpses until one of them is his. Judge yourself the same way: not "did it work?" but "in how many of the worlds where I made this exact choice does it end badly?"
:::

## Reading it into your own life

This first lesson asks for one honest renovation of how you keep score. When you win, ask *was that my process, or my chamber coming up empty?* — and refuse full credit for luck. When you lose after deciding well, refuse the blame, too: a good decision that drew a bad world is still a good decision, and outcome-shame will teach you to abandon exactly the discipline that protects you. And when you envy someone's dazzling results — the crypto neighbor, the leveraged cousin — run Solon's audit before you copy them: *is their story finished? What lives in their other histories?*

The rarest skill in a random world is not picking winners. It is **surviving until your process gets the time it needs** — capping every downside that could remove you from the game, and letting the modest, repeatable edge compound while flashier neighbors take their turns at the wheel.

## Before you go

So: call no fortune happy until the story ends, judge every decision across its alternative histories, and envy no bank statement until you've counted the corpses in the neighboring worlds.

But this raises a delicious puzzle, friend. If outcomes mislead — then what about being *right*? Surely the person who predicts correctly most often wins in the end?

Tomorrow, Taleb answers with the strangest confession in the book: a meeting where he told his colleagues the market would probably *rise* that week... while betting heavily that it would *fall*. And he was being perfectly rational. Why you can be wrong ninety percent of the time and grow rich — or right ninety percent of the time and go broke.

## Key takeaways

- Call no man happy until the end: whatever chance gives, chance can repossess — a result alone proves nothing.
- Judge decisions across alternative histories: quality is the average over all the worlds, not the one draw you saw.
- Ten million from Russian roulette is not ten million from dentistry — count the corpses in the neighboring worlds.
- Streak-fitted is not skilled: a random regime promotes whoever matches it, until the regime changes.
- Cap your downside first; survival is the precondition for any edge to compound.
- Score yourself by process: good decision + bad world is still a good decision — and luck deserves no credit.
