# Monkeys on Typewriters: The Survivors You See

Third night, friend — and tonight Taleb performs the most famous demolition in the book. By the end of it, every dazzling track record you've ever envied — the star fund manager, the millionaire author's formula, the analyst who "called the crash" — will look different forever.

But we start smaller. With a woman named Janet, on Park Avenue, who is married to a spectacular success... and feels poor.

## The birds that sing

![A grand Park Avenue apartment lobby at dusk, one modest couple entering while sleek limousines unload wealthier neighbors around them](/story-art/fooled-by-randomness/03-parkave.jpg)

Marc is a Manhattan corporate lawyer earning half a million dollars a year — by any sane measure, a triumph. His wife Janet feels like a failure. Why? Because their Park Avenue building is stuffed with financiers earning ten times more, and by the standard of everyone she *sees*, they are the poor family on the block.

Her misery, Taleb says, is a **sampling error**. The people who tried finance and *failed* don't live on Park Avenue — they dispersed silently back into ordinary life, generating no signal at all. Winners write memoirs, give interviews, and buy the apartments you walk past. Losers exit the sample. **We hear only the birds that sing** — and then we mistake the singing for the whole forest. Judged against her true reference class — all lawyers, all professionals — Janet's husband is a massive success; judged against a sample *pre-filtered for extreme wealth*, he lags. Same man, same money. The entire difference is the denominator. And it compounds: the visible survivors are also the most *confident* — they lived a run of good outcomes, credited their own qualities, and now radiate the certainty that makes them irresistible role models. The filter manufactures not just skewed evidence, but charismatic spokespeople for it. Check your own feed, friend: it is a Park Avenue of its own.

## Ten thousand coin-flippers

The old theorem says enough monkeys hammering typewriters will eventually produce the *Iliad*. Taleb's twist is the practical question: having found the monkey that typed it — *would you bet on that monkey typing the Odyssey next?*

Obviously not. Yet watch how investors treat fund managers.

::: guess
Start 10,000 fund managers whose yearly results are a pure coin flip — half win, half lose, losers leave the business. After five years, how many managers boast five consecutive winning years — from a process with ZERO skill in it?
---
About **313.** Halve 10,000 five times: 5,000... 2,500... 1,250... 625... ~313 five-year streaks, mathematically guaranteed, from dart-throwing. Every one of those three hundred will be profiled, will command fees, and will *sincerely* attribute the streak to discipline and insight — because from inside a streak, the randomness is invisible. Two icy consequences. First: **the number of impressive track records depends only on how many players started the game** — not on anyone's ability; the bigger the industry, the more godlike its luckiest survivor must look. Second: chance doesn't even need a fair coin — a cohort with *negative* average skill after fees still produces a crop of stars, so long as there's volume and a filter deleting losers. The prediction that follows is regression to the mean: the star you noticed *because* he was extreme should be expected to sink back toward ordinary — which is exactly the sad career of hot funds that attract the most money at their peak. When a record is shown to you, remember *why* it reached you: because it was extreme. And extremity selected from noise does not persist.
:::

## The millionaire next door's double error

Now the bestseller that taught a generation: *The Millionaire Next Door* studied wealthy Americans and found what they shared — frugality, cheap watches, dull businesses, patient buy-and-hold investing. Copy the habits, the book whispers, and become rich.

Two statistical sins, friend. First, it's an **ex-post sample**: Stanley selected people *because* they ended up wealthy and looked backward. He never counted the equally frugal, equally disciplined savers whose chosen assets collapsed — those identical twins of his millionaires vanished from the dataset, making thrift-plus-holding look like a recipe when it was one ticket in a lottery. Second, **era luck**: his millionaires accumulated through one of the greatest asset inflations in history. Run the same playbook through a long bear market — or run the survey in 1982 instead of the roaring late nineties — and the "patient accumulator" looks like a stubborn fool. The era did most of the work; the book billed the habits.

And Taleb's deepest cut: some survivor traits aren't even neutral — they are *causes of ruin in the alternative histories*. Concentrating your wealth in illiquid businesses and grimly riding out every downturn is precisely what destroys people when the dice fall differently. A trait common among visible winners tells you *nothing* until you know how common it is among the invisible losers. No denominator, no verdict.

## The mysterious letter

Here is survivorship bias weaponized — a classic con you should carry for life.

::: guess
A con artist mails 10,000 letters on January 2nd: half predict the market will rise that month, half predict a fall. What is he building — and why will it work perfectly on a few hundred people?
---
Each month he writes again — **only to those who received the correct call** — splitting his predictions anew. After several rounds, a few hundred strangers have personally witnessed an *unbroken string of perfect market calls*, and wire their savings to this evident prophet. Each victim saw a genuinely improbable personal history; the aggregate process *guaranteed* someone would see it. Your intuition fails here for a precise reason: you compute the odds of the specific coincidence you observed, after observing it — instead of the odds that *some* coincidence would happen to *someone*. The **birthday paradox** is the cleanest teacher: for someone to share *your* birthday you need 253 people for a coin-flip chance — but for *some pair* in the room to share one, just **23 people** suffice, because possible pairs multiply combinatorially. Any-match is cheap; specific-match is expensive; we chronically mistake the first for the second. That airport run-in with an old friend? The odds of *that person, there* — minuscule. The odds of *some* surprising encounter with *someone* from your whole network across a lifetime of airports — near certainty. You are not being sent signs, friend. You are drawing from an enormous urn and framing the hits.
:::

## Torturing the data until it confesses

Industrialize cheap coincidence and you get **data mining**: search enough patterns and some will fit your data by chance — and *only* your data. The bestseller *The Bible Code* found assassinations "predicted" in skip-letter sequences of the Torah; the debunkers replied by extracting equally stunning "prophecies" from *Moby-Dick*, because run enough skip-patterns over any long text and you can find anything. The Bible isn't encoded. The search space is enormous.

Finance runs the same ritual with better software. Feed history into a backtester, try thousands of rules — "buy after two down days," "sell Mondays in March" — and a handful *must* show spectacular past profits, for exactly the reason 313 coin-flippers show streaks. The winning rule has memorized the noise of one particular history, and dies on contact with the future. A researcher even showed that **butter production in Bangladesh** "predicted" the S&P 500 with stunning historical accuracy. The defenses are structural: demand a theory *before* the pattern; count how many tests were run (at 5% significance, a hundred tries hand you five "discoveries" free); insist on out-of-sample proof; and discount every finding in proportion to the size of the space searched to find it.

## Before you go

So: never judge a survivor without reconstructing the cohort; expect ~300 flawless five-year records from 10,000 dart-throwers; read every success formula against the invisible twins who failed with the same habits; price coincidence at its true cheapness; and when data confesses, ask how long it was tortured. One rule holds it all: **the information is never in the track record. It is in the denominator — and the person showing you the record has every incentive to hide it.**

But now, friend, a subtler poison. Suppose you *have* a genuinely good strategy — truly skilled, truly positive. Tomorrow Taleb shows why even then, checking your results too often will torture you into abandoning it: why a winning portfolio watched minute-by-minute delivers mostly *pain*, why the last straw breaks camels, and why an early accident — a keyboard layout, a software empire — can lock the whole world onto a path nobody chose.

## Key takeaways

- You hear only the birds that sing: reconstruct the full starting cohort before crediting any visible success.
- 10,000 coin-flippers must produce ~300 five-year streaks — impressive records are a function of cohort size, not skill.
- Success-formula books study survivors ex post; the same habits fill the invisible graveyard — no denominator, no verdict.
- Any-coincidence is cheap (23 people, 50% shared birthday); specific-coincidence is dear — don't mistake one for the other.
- Data mining finds "prophecies" in Moby-Dick and butter in Bangladesh: demand theory first, count the tests, insist on out-of-sample.
- What you noticed because it was extreme will regress: forecast reversion, not persistence — and audit your own filtered feeds.
