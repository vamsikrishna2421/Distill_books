# Optimal Stopping: The 37% Rule

## Key Ideas
- When you must evaluate options one at a time with no going back, the optimal strategy is to look at exactly 37% of them without committing, then leap at the first option better than everything seen so far.
- The 37% figure is 1/e, and it also equals your probability of ending up with the very best option — a 63% failure rate that is nonetheless the best any strategy can achieve.
- The rule bends with the situation: if your offers can be rejected half the time, start leaping after 25%; if you can go back to passed-over options with a 50% chance they're still available, look until 61%.
- When you have full information — an objective score for each option rather than mere rankings — a Threshold Rule replaces Look-Then-Leap and raises your success rate to 58%.
- Selling a house, hunting for parking, and quitting while ahead are all optimal stopping problems, each solved by a threshold set by the cost of continuing the search.
- Laboratory experiments show people consistently stop searching earlier than the math prescribes — a bias best explained by the unmodeled cost of the search itself.

## The Secretary Problem and Why 37% Is Magic

The canonical puzzle is the **secretary problem**: you interview applicants one by one in random order. After each interview you must hire or reject on the spot — no revisiting rejected candidates — and you can only judge each applicant *relative* to those already seen, not against an absolute scale. Your goal is to hire the single best applicant in the pool.

The dilemma is structural. Stop too early and you probably haven't seen the best; wait too long and you've likely already rejected them. Any sensible strategy therefore takes a **Look-Then-Leap** form: spend an initial phase gathering data without committing, establishing a standard, then commit to the first applicant who beats everyone from the look phase.

The mathematics pins down the crossover precisely: look at the first 37% of applicants (technically n/e, where e ≈ 2.718), then leap. Following this rule, your chance of landing the very best applicant is also 37% — the two numbers coincide because both equal **1/e**. Remarkably, this success rate holds no matter how large the pool. Picking the best of 100 applicants is a 1-in-100 shot by luck; the algorithm delivers better than 1-in-3. A 63% chance of failure sounds grim, but no strategy can do better, and rival intuitions (hire early, always hold out) do far worse as the pool grows.

The problem surfaced publicly in Martin Gardner's February 1960 *Scientific American* column as the "game of googol" and became a mathematical sensation; its origins are murky, with credit often traced to mathematician Merrill Flood in the 1950s. The rule also converts cleanly from counting candidates to counting time: if you've allotted a month to search, gather intel without committing for the first eleven days, then take the first option that beats all predecessors. Apartment hunting in a brutal market like San Francisco — where hesitating for a day loses the unit — is exactly this game, and the 37% rule tells you when calibration must end and commitment must begin.

## Rejection, Recall, and Kepler's Eleven Candidates

The classical problem's assumptions — your offer is always accepted, and passed-over options are gone forever — rarely hold in life, and the algorithm adapts to each relaxation.

**Rejection**: if every offer you make stands a 50/50 chance of being turned down, you should start making offers after seeing just **25%** of the pool. Because any proposal might fail, you must begin earlier and propose to every new best-yet option; your overall chance of landing the best falls to 25%. The pattern generalizes: a rejection probability of q shifts the leap point to q^(1/(1-q)) of the pool.

**Recall**: if you can go back to earlier options — say a rejected-then-revisited offer is accepted half the time — you can afford a longer look phase. The optimal strategy becomes: search without committing through **61%** of the pool, then take the first best-yet option; if you exhaust the pool empty-handed, go back to the best person you saw and hope. The safety net raises the success rate too.

The chapter's canonical anecdote is Johannes Kepler. After his first wife died of cholera in 1611, the astronomer methodically courted eleven women over two years, tormented by whether to settle or keep looking — he complained of a heart that "held itself back" while options remained. He ultimately returned to candidate number five, Susanna Reuttinger, who accepted; the marriage was by all accounts a happy one. Kepler lived the recall variant — and got lucky that going back worked.

The cautionary mirror image is operations researcher Michael Trick, who as a graduate student applied the 37% rule to his dating life, identified the first woman better than all before her, proposed — and was refused. The lesson: know which variant of the problem you're actually in before trusting its number.

## Full Information Changes the Game: The Threshold Rule

Everything above assumes you learn only *rankings* — is this option the best so far? — which is why a look phase is mandatory: you need data before any standard exists. But sometimes you get objective scores. Imagine hiring a typist whose words-per-minute test places her at an exact percentile of the whole population of typists. Now the first candidate already carries meaning; a 95th-percentile typist is excellent whether she's applicant one or applicant fifty.

With **full information**, the optimal strategy is no longer Look-Then-Leap but a **Threshold Rule**: accept an option immediately if it clears a percentile bar, and lower the bar as remaining options dwindle. With many candidates left, hold out for the top few percent; with only one left, take whatever comes. The logic is pure expected value: accept a candidate whenever the chance that a better one remains among those left is smaller than her own quality warrants.

Full information pays. The Threshold Rule lands the single best option **58%** of the time — comfortably beating the 37% ceiling of the no-information game, and again independent of pool size. The broader lesson: any objective yardstick, even a rough sense of the market's distribution, transforms the problem from "compare against what I've seen" to "compare against a standard," and you should hunt for such yardsticks before resigning yourself to the 37% regime.

## Selling, Parking, and Quitting While Ahead

Once search itself carries a cost, thresholds rather than look-phases govern a family of everyday problems.

**Selling a house** is optimal stopping with full information and an explicit waiting cost (mortgage payments, carrying costs) per offer. Suppose offers arrive uniformly between $400,000 and $500,000. The rule: keep waiting as long as the expected gain from one more offer exceeds the cost of soliciting it. If each further offer costs you $2,000 to wait for, accept nothing below roughly $480,000; if waiting costs $10,000 per offer, your bar drops to around $455,000. Strikingly, when the stream of potential buyers is effectively endless, the optimal threshold doesn't depend on how many offers you've already fielded — set it in advance from the waiting cost alone and never sell below it, no matter how the search wears on. The same math governs job offers and salary negotiation: the cost of continued searching, not fatigue or sunk time, should set your walk-away number.

**Parking** is optimal stopping where the key variable is **occupancy rate**. Driving toward a destination past a line of spaces, each spot poses take-it-or-gamble-onward. The optimal policy is a distance threshold that depends sharply on how full the street is: at 99% occupancy, grab the first open spot from nearly 70 spaces out; as occupancy falls, you can afford to cruise much closer before committing. Small changes in occupancy swing search times dramatically, which is why urban economist Donald Shoup argues for pricing meters to hold occupancy around 85% — near-full streets convert everyone's parking into a costly stopping problem and fill roads with cruising traffic.

**Quitting while ahead** is stopping in reverse. A burglar who succeeds with probability p on each job but loses everything when caught should retire after approximately p/(1−p) heists — a 90% success rate justifies about nine jobs, a 50% rate just one. And some games have no optimal stopping point at all: in "triple or nothing" gambles, the expected value of one more round always looks positive, so the math never tells you to stop — a warning that a problem with no good stopping rule is a problem to avoid entering.

## People Stop Too Early — and Why That's Almost Rational

How do humans actually perform on these problems? In experiments by Darryl Seale and Amnon Rapoport (1997), most subjects facing the classical secretary problem did use Look-Then-Leap strategies — but with look phases that were too short, leaping after about 31% of candidates instead of the optimal 37%. Across many studies, the signature finding repeats: **people tend to stop searching too early**, leaving the best options unseen.

The most compelling explanation reframes the "error." The textbook secretary problem assumes searching is free; real searching never is — interviewing, viewing apartments, and first dates all burn time and effort. Seale and Rapoport showed that once you charge even a small cost per candidate inspected, the *optimal* strategy shifts to earlier stopping that closely matches what people actually do. Our impatience may be a rational response to an **endogenous time cost** the classical model ignores, not a cognitive defect.

The chapter's practical synthesis: life rarely hands you the exact assumptions of any one variant, but the family of results gives you a decision skeleton. Know whether you're in a no-information or full-information world, whether rejection and recall are possible, and what continuing to search truly costs. Then set your leap point or threshold *in advance* — the mathematics exists precisely because in-the-moment judgment ("is this good enough, or is better coming?") is where humans flounder. When to stop looking isn't a matter of temperament; for a surprising range of problems, it has a provably best answer.

## In Practice
- Facing a sequential, no-going-back choice (apartments, hires, dating), fix your pool or time budget now, spend the first 37% only calibrating, then commit to the first option that beats everything from the look phase.
- If your offers can be rejected, move your leap point up to 25%; if you can realistically revisit past options, extend your look phase to 61% before committing.
- Before defaulting to the 37% rule, hunt for objective benchmarks (market data, salary ranges, test scores) — with full information, switch to a percentile threshold you lower as options run out, and enjoy 58% odds instead of 37%.
- Selling something or weighing job offers? Compute what one more round of waiting actually costs you, set the acceptance threshold that cost implies, and refuse to lower it just because the search feels long.
- When parking, read the occupancy: on a nearly full street take the first spot far out; only cruise close when you can see plenty of vacancies.
- Audit your instinct to grab an early option: unless search itself is genuinely costly, you are probably stopping too soon — the data says most people leap around 31% when 37% is optimal.
