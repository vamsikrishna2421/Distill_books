# Scheduling: What to Do, in What Order

## Key Ideas
- Before you can schedule well you must choose a metric; different goals (fewest late tasks, least total waiting, smallest worst-case lateness) demand entirely different orderings, and no single ordering optimizes them all.
- Three optimal single-machine algorithms cover most goals: **Earliest Due Date** minimizes maximum lateness, **Moore's algorithm** minimizes the number of late tasks, and **Shortest Processing Time** minimizes total completion time — with a weighted variant when tasks differ in importance.
- Add precedence constraints (some tasks must finish before others can start) and most scheduling problems become NP-hard; the vast majority of studied scheduling variants have no efficient solution.
- Strict priority systems can suffer **priority inversion**, where a low-priority task blocks a high-priority one — the bug that nearly killed the 1997 Mars Pathfinder mission until engineers uploaded a **priority inheritance** fix.
- Every task switch has a real cost; too many switches produce **thrashing**, where all time goes to switching and none to work. The remedies are interrupt coalescing (batching interruptions) and refusing to work in slices smaller than a minimum quantum.

## First Decide What "Best" Means

Scheduling theory began with concrete logistics. In 1954, Selmer Johnson analyzed book binding — jobs that pass through two machines in sequence, like laundry through a washer and dryer — and showed the optimal order: start with the item with the shortest wash time and end with the one with the shortest dry time, so both machines idle as little as possible.

But most personal scheduling is a **single-machine problem**: you are the machine, only one task runs at a time, and every ordering takes the same total time to finish everything. That yields the chapter's central insight: the ordering only matters relative to a chosen **metric**. Until you decide what you're optimizing — and you must decide, because the goals conflict — asking for the "best" schedule is meaningless.

If your metric is minimizing **maximum lateness** (how badly you blow your worst deadline), the optimal strategy is **Earliest Due Date (EDD)**: simply do tasks in deadline order, ignoring how long each takes. It's the right way to eat a fridge full of perishables if you care about how spoiled the worst item gets: eat in order of expiration date.

If instead you want to minimize the *number* of late tasks (or spoiled groceries), use **Moore's algorithm** (Moore–Hodgson). Start with the EDD ordering; the moment you foresee a task finishing late, remove the *biggest* (most time-consuming) task scheduled so far and set it aside. Repeat as needed, doing the discarded tasks last (or never — compost the watermelon). This minimizes the count of misses, though the tasks it abandons can end up spectacularly late. It's a defensible policy when a miss is a miss regardless of magnitude — but ruthless, since it explicitly sacrifices some tasks entirely.

The metric decision is not a technicality; it's the moral core of scheduling. Choosing EDD versus Moore's is choosing between "never let anything slip far" and "let a few things fail completely so most things succeed."

## Shortest First, Weighted by Importance

When deadlines aren't the issue but total burden is, the optimal metric is often **sum of completion times** — roughly, how long tasks (and the people waiting on them) linger. The optimal algorithm is **Shortest Processing Time (SPT)**: always do the quickest task first. Each completed task stops "waiting," so clearing small ones fast shrinks the outstanding pile quickest. This is the kernel of truth in productivity advice like Getting Things Done's do-it-now rule for anything under two minutes: it minimizes the number of things hanging over you.

But tasks aren't equally important. The fix is **weighted shortest processing time**: divide each task's importance (weight) by its duration and do tasks in descending order of that ratio — a kind of importance-per-hour density. A task twice as important justifies waiting only if it takes less than twice as long. The same math governs debt repayment: the "snowball" method (pay smallest balances first) is pure SPT and feels motivating, but the financially optimal policy weights by interest rate — pay down the highest-rate debt first, regardless of size.

Weighted SPT also explains **procrastination** in an unflattering new light: the procrastinator is often executing SPT faithfully but with the wrong weights, treating every item as equally important and therefore busily clearing trivial quick tasks while the consequential one waits. The bug isn't laziness; it's optimizing task *count* instead of importance. Christian and Griffiths note the pathology can be extreme — like a mania for doing easy things — and the cure is not more effort but better weights: staying "on the horse," working on the densest task available.

When tasks arrive unpredictably over time, the same algorithms survive if you allow **preemption** — pausing work when something new lands. Preemptive EDD and preemptive weighted SPT remain optimal (or near-optimal in expectation, using weighted *shortest expected* processing time under uncertainty): switch only if the newcomer beats the current task on the metric; otherwise, keep going. Uncertainty thus partly vindicates staying the course.

## Precedence Constraints and the Wall of Intractability

Real to-do lists have dependencies: you can't submit the report before the analysis is done. Add such **precedence constraints** and scheduling's clean optimality results start collapsing. Minimizing maximum lateness stays tractable — Eugene Lawler showed in 1968 that a clever variant of EDD still works — but when Lawler examined minimizing the *sum* of completion times with precedence constraints, he proved it **NP-hard**: no efficient algorithm exists (assuming P ≠ NP), a theme chapter 8 develops in full.

Small changes in the problem's fine print flip it between easy and impossible. Allow preemption and one variant becomes solvable; forbid it, or let tasks have release times, and it may become intractable again. Scheduling researchers have mapped this landscape exhaustively: of the thousands of catalogued scheduling problem variants, only a small minority — well under ten percent — are known to be efficiently solvable, while the overwhelming majority are provably intractable, with a residue still unresolved. Scheduling as a whole is not a solved problem; it's mostly a wall of hard ones.

The practical lesson isn't despair but humility and design: keep your own scheduling problem in the tractable zone. Fewer interdependencies, willingness to preempt, and a single clear metric keep you where optimal algorithms exist. Pile on constraints and you guarantee that neither you nor any computer can schedule perfectly — at which point good-enough heuristics (chapter 8's relaxations) are the rational recourse.

## Priority Inversion and the Machine That Saved Itself on Mars

Priorities alone can misfire badly. In July 1997, NASA's Mars Pathfinder — flawlessly landed and gathering data — began spontaneously rebooting, losing whole days of work. The cause was **priority inversion**. A low-priority meteorological task would acquire a lock on the spacecraft's shared information bus, then get preempted by unrelated medium-priority work (a long-running communications task). The high-priority bus-management task couldn't proceed — the resource it needed was held by the blocked low-priority task — so the system's highest priority effectively waited on its lowest, until a watchdog timer concluded something was fatally wrong and reset the machine.

The fix, which JPL engineers transmitted across 100+ million miles to patch the running spacecraft, was **priority inheritance**: when a high-priority task is blocked by a low-priority one, the low-priority task temporarily *inherits* the high priority, gets rushed through, releases the resource, and the system unblocks. The human translation is counterintuitive but sound: if a trivial task is blocking your most important work, that trivial task *is* your most important work. Do the annoying errand at the priority of the project it's holding hostage, rather than letting "unimportant" chores silently strangle important ones.

Pathfinder also illustrates why blindly working "top priority first" isn't a complete policy. Priorities interact with shared resources and dependencies; a scheduler needs rules for those interactions, not just a ranked list.

## Context Switching, Thrashing, and Guarding the Quantum

Every switch between tasks costs something: a computer must swap memory and state; a human must reload context, and studies of interrupted work find it can take substantial time to resume full concentration. Switching is pure **overhead** — metawork that produces nothing — yet responsiveness demands some of it. Schedulers therefore face a fundamental trade-off between **responsiveness** (reacting quickly) and **throughput** (getting the most done): more switching means snappier reactions and less total work.

At the pathological extreme lies **thrashing**, named in Peter Denning's analysis of memory paging: a system so overcommitted that it spends essentially all its time swapping context and none doing work, while feeling maximally "busy." Humans thrash too — the day of nonstop email, Slack, and meeting fragments that ends with nothing accomplished. Computer science's counterintuitive advice: when thrashing, the way out is to do *less* — drop tasks, refuse work, even pick items in arbitrary order rather than paying the metawork cost of re-prioritizing everything.

Operating systems avoid thrashing with two techniques that translate directly to life. First, a **minimum quantum** (minimum slice of work): Linux's scheduler enforces a floor on how briefly a task can run, ensuring each timeslice is long relative to the switch cost. The human version is timeboxing — protecting blocks long enough to amortize the cost of getting into a task, and declining any "quick" interruption that would shatter one.

Second, **interrupt coalescing**: rather than servicing every interrupt instantly, hardware waits and handles them in batches. Humans coalesce by checking email at fixed intervals instead of on arrival, holding office hours so questions arrive together, and letting mail and messages pool for scheduled processing. Donald Knuth is the exemplar: he abandoned email in 1990 and processes correspondence in concentrated batches, protecting the long uninterrupted stretches his work requires. Choose your interval by the maximum response delay you can genuinely afford — then don't look until it elapses.

## In Practice
- Pick one explicit metric for this week's tasks — fewest missed deadlines, least total lingering work, or importance-weighted completion — and order your list by the matching algorithm instead of by instinct.
- When deadlines dominate, sort by due date (EDD); if you clearly can't make them all, deliberately drop the single biggest offending task Moore-style rather than letting everything slip a little.
- For everything else, compute rough importance-per-hour and start with the densest task; check debts the same way and pay the highest interest rate first, not the smallest balance.
- When a trivial chore blocks an important project, apply priority inheritance: treat that chore as inheriting the project's priority and clear it immediately.
- Set a minimum work quantum — e.g., 90-minute blocks you refuse to fragment — and decline or defer anything that would slice one below the cost of switching.
- Coalesce your interrupts: turn off push notifications, batch email into two or three fixed daily checks, and set the interval by the longest response delay you can honestly tolerate.
