# Networking and Game Theory: Living Among Other Optimizers

## Key Ideas
- The internet works without any central controller by chopping messages into packets, confirming each one with acknowledgments, and treating silence itself as information.
- **Exponential backoff** — retrying forever but doubling the wait after every failure — is the machine solution to unreliable partners, and it doubles as a workable model of human forgiveness.
- TCP's **Additive Increase, Multiplicative Decrease** rule shows that the optimal way to use an unknowable, shifting capacity is a permanent sawtooth: push until you fail, cut back sharply, push again.
- **Bufferbloat** proves that always saying yes destroys responsiveness; queues are only healthy when they are bursts being absorbed, not a permanent backlog.
- Nash equilibria exist for every game but are generally intractable to compute, and even when reached they can be terrible for everyone — the **price of anarchy** measures how bad.
- When a game has a bad equilibrium, the escape is not better strategy but **mechanism design**: change the rules — as the Vickrey auction does — so that honesty and cooperation become the winning moves.

## Packet Switching: Cooperation Without Certainty

The phone network used **circuit switching**: a dedicated line held open between two parties. Paul Baran and Donald Davies proposed the alternative that became the internet — **packet switching** — in which messages are diced into small packets that each find their own route and are reassembled at the destination. There is no dedicated connection at all; a "connection" is a consensual fiction maintained by the two endpoints. The very first ARPANET transmission, in Leonard Kleinrock's UCLA lab in October 1969, crashed after two letters — "LO" — a fittingly humble start.

How do you build reliability on an unreliable channel? With the **acknowledgment (ACK)**: every packet received is confirmed back to the sender, and anything unconfirmed is retransmitted. Even opening a TCP connection is a **triple handshake** — request, acknowledgment, acknowledgment of the acknowledgment.

But acknowledgment has a deep limit, formalized in the **Byzantine generals problem**: two armies coordinating an attack through messengers who may be captured can never reach certainty, because the last message sent is always unconfirmed. Perfect assurance over an unreliable channel is provably impossible. Networks — and people — proceed anyway, replacing certainty with good-enough protocols.

The other elegant trick is making silence speak. Since packet loss signals congestion, a missing ACK is itself a message: the network is full, slow down. TCP extracts information from what doesn't arrive — a reminder that in any communication system, non-response is data, and the meaning of silence depends on how long you'd expect a reply to take.

## Exponential Backoff: How Machines — and People — Forgive

When two transmitters talk over each other, both messages are destroyed, and if both retry immediately they collide forever. The ALOHAnet, built in 1971 to link the Hawaiian islands by radio, solved this with randomness plus escalation: after a failure, wait a random delay; after each further failure, double the maximum delay. This is **exponential backoff**, and it now underlies Ethernet, Wi-Fi, and virtually every "try again later" on the internet — from a phone reconnecting to a dropped network to servers throttling repeated failed login attempts.

The genius is in what backoff avoids. Retrying at a fixed rate either hammers a struggling system or wastes capacity being overly timid; giving up after N strikes throws away a connection that might recover. Doubling the wait keeps total retry cost bounded no matter how long the outage lasts, while guaranteeing you'll eventually reconnect if the other side ever comes back.

Backoff has a striking property: it never gives up entirely, yet it can't be exploited indefinitely. Its human translation is a policy for flaky friends and broken trust — infinite mercy with exponentially decaying expectation. You never write someone off, but after each letdown you invest less and wait longer before trying again. It's a middle path between naive endless chances and brittle one-strike rules.

The book pairs this with Judge Steven Alm's **HOPE program** in Hawaii, which replaced probation's typical pattern — many ignored violations followed by a draconian penalty — with immediate, modest, utterly predictable sanctions for every violation. Violations plummeted. Both designs share a principle: the timing and reliability of consequences matter more than their severity.

## AIMD: The Sawtooth as a Way of Life

How fast should you send when you can't know the network's capacity — which is changing anyway? TCP's answer is **flow control** via **AIMD (Additive Increase, Multiplicative Decrease)**. A connection starts with **slow start** (doubling its rate each round trip), then increases linearly — one more packet per successful round trip — and the instant a packet is dropped, cuts its rate in half. The result is the **TCP sawtooth**: an endless cycle of probing upward and backing off, because the only way to find the ceiling is to bump into it.

Nature converged on the same design. Biologist Deborah Gordon and computer scientist Balaji Prabhakar found that harvester ant colonies regulate how many foragers leave the nest by the rate of returning ants — feedback identical in structure to TCP's, earning the nickname the **"anternet."**

The human application is a rebuttal to the **Peter Principle** (people rise to their level of incompetence and stay there). An AIMD career or organization would treat occasional failure as the expected cost of calibration: push people upward until strain shows, pull back promptly and without stigma, and resume. Everyone oscillates near their true capacity instead of sitting frozen above it.

The same logic condemns overcommitment. Jim Gettys diagnosed **bufferbloat** in 2010: oversized memory buffers in home routers never dropped packets, so TCP never got the "slow down" signal, queues stayed perpetually full, and latency exploded. Buffers are good only for absorbing bursts; a buffer that's always full delivers everything — late. A crammed inbox and calendar are bufferbloat in a life: maximum throughput, ruined responsiveness. Sometimes the kindest, most honest signal is **tail drop** — an explicit no, or letting a request visibly bounce, rather than silently queuing it for weeks.

## Nash Equilibria and the Price of Anarchy

Once other optimizers enter the picture, the question shifts from "what's the best strategy?" to "what happens when everyone plays theirs?" A **Nash equilibrium** is a set of strategies where no player can gain by unilaterally changing course. John Nash proved in 1950 that every game has at least one (allowing randomized strategies).

But existence isn't usefulness. Constantinos Daskalakis, Paul Goldberg, and Christos Papadimitriou proved that actually finding Nash equilibria is intractable in the general case (PPAD-complete) — which corrodes the concept's predictive power for real markets. As Kamal Jain put it, "If your laptop cannot find it, neither can the market."

Worse, equilibria can be stable and awful simultaneously. In the **prisoner's dilemma**, defection is each player's **dominant strategy** — best regardless of what the other does — yet mutual defection leaves both worse off than mutual cooperation. Scaled up, this becomes Garrett Hardin's **tragedy of the commons**: shared resources (pastures, climate, vacation norms) get destroyed by individually rational choices. Unlimited-vacation policies illustrate it perfectly: each employee rationally takes slightly less than their peers to look dedicated, and the equilibrium ratchets toward no vacation at all.

The gap between the equilibrium and the coordinated optimum is the **price of anarchy** (Koutsoupias and Papadimitriou). Sometimes it's mercifully small: Tim Roughgarden showed that for selfish drivers on a road network with linear congestion, traffic is at worst 4/3 of optimal — anarchy costs only 33%, so grand centralized traffic schemes have limited upside (autonomous cars mostly promise fewer accidents and closer following, not the elimination of congestion). But equilibria can also be perverse: **Braess's paradox** shows that adding a road can make everyone's commute *slower* — and closing one can speed it up.

## Mechanism Design: Don't Play Better, Change the Game

When a game's equilibrium is bad, advice to individual players is useless — deviating unilaterally just makes you the sucker. **Mechanism design**, game theory run in reverse, asks instead: what rules would make the outcome we want the equilibrium? A credible outside authority can transform a game: the mob boss who punishes defectors makes silence rational; a government that mandates shop closing hours or minimum vacation frees everyone from a race no one wanted to run. (Evernote tried paying employees $1,000 to take vacation — a nudge; a mandate changes the equilibrium.)

Evolution may have built mechanisms into us. Economist Robert Frank argues that emotions are involuntary commitment devices: anger makes retaliation credible even when it's costly, and love solves the couple's endless optimal-stopping problem by making defection unthinkable. Being somewhat irrational — visibly, reliably so — can be strategically superior to being a transparent calculator.

Auctions show mechanism design at its sharpest. Open outcry auctions leak information and fuel **information cascades**, where rational agents piggyback on others' bids until prices detach from any private valuation — herding without anyone being foolish. Sealed-bid auctions avoid cascades but force endless strategic **bid shading**, guessing what rivals will do. William Vickrey's **second-price auction** fixes both: sealed bids, highest bidder wins, but pays the *second*-highest bid. Under those rules, bidding your true valuation is the dominant strategy — lying can only hurt you. And Roger Myerson's **revelation principle** generalizes this: any game requiring strategic misrepresentation can be redesigned into an equivalent game where honesty is optimal. Good rules don't demand virtue; they make virtue the winning strategy.

## Computational Kindness

The book closes with an ethic the authors call **computational kindness**: since computation — searching, weighing, deciding — is genuinely costly, imposing it on others is a real harm, and absorbing it for them is a real gift. "Whenever works for you!" sounds polite but hands your friend the entire scheduling search problem; proposing "Tuesday at noon, or Wednesday morning?" is kinder, because verifying an option is vastly easier than generating one. The same holds for feigned indifference: saying "I'm happy with any table" forces the host to model your preferences; stating one lets them simply check it. Politely constraining choice — yours and others' — is a courtesy, not an imposition.

The principle extends to design. A parking garage that displays open spaces per level, or a bus stop with a countdown clock, spares thousands of people a daily inference problem — each converts an anxious prediction task into a trivial act of reading. Cities and interfaces can be computationally kind or cruel, and the difference compounds across every person who uses them.

Kindness also runs inward. Many of life's problems — hiring, scheduling, routing, choosing — are provably intractable, so the optimal *procedure* often succeeds only a minority of the time (the 37% rule fails 63% of it). The rational standard is therefore process, not outcome: if you used the best strategy available, a bad result isn't a mistake. Relax constraints, use approximations, accept randomness, stop on time — and extend to yourself and others the understanding that everyone is running heuristics against problems too hard for anyone to solve exactly.

## In Practice
- Adopt exponential backoff with unreliable people and systems: never blacklist permanently, but double the interval — and shrink the stakes — after each failure.
- Calibrate workloads AIMD-style this week: add one commitment at a time, and when you drop something, cut your load sharply rather than trimming 5%.
- Audit your buffers: if your inbox or task queue is *always* full, it isn't absorbing bursts — start declining or explicitly bouncing requests instead of silently queuing them.
- When you're stuck in a lousy equilibrium (always-on email, overwork one-upmanship), stop optimizing your play and change the mechanism: set team-level rules, mandates, or defaults.
- Structure any bidding or negotiation you run so honesty is the best policy — for example, use second-price (Vickrey) rules instead of formats that reward strategic misstatement.
- Practice computational kindness: propose specific options instead of "whatever works," state your preferences instead of feigning indifference, and judge your decisions (and others') by process, not outcome.
