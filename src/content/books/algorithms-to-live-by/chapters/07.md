# Overfitting: When to Think Less

## Key Ideas
- **Overfitting** happens when a model — or a mind — matches its data so closely that it starts memorizing noise instead of learning signal, making it *worse* at predicting the future than a simpler model would be.
- More thinking, more factors, and more data-fitting are not automatically better: past a certain point, added complexity degrades real-world performance.
- **Cross-validation** — holding back some data, or testing against a second, independent measure — is how you detect that a model or metric has been overfit or gamed.
- **Regularization** prevents overfitting by penalizing complexity; the **Lasso** technique pushes marginal factors' weights to exactly zero, and simple heuristics, energy budgets, and single-page pro/con lists do the same job in everyday life.
- Research by Robyn Dawes and Gerd Gigerenzer shows that equal-weight or single-factor rules routinely match or beat sophisticated statistical models when data is limited — simplicity is not a concession but a strategy.
- **Early stopping** turns deliberation time itself into a regularizer: when evidence is noisy or scarce, the rational move is to reason briefly from the biggest factors and stop — to think less.

## Why the Best-Fitting Model Makes the Worst Predictions

Suppose you plot a handful of data points — say, people's life satisfaction against years of marriage — and fit curves to them. A one-factor line captures the broad trend but misses details. A two-factor curve fits better. A nine-factor polynomial passes through every single point perfectly — and then swings wildly between and beyond them, predicting absurdities like satisfaction plunging to impossible lows a few years out.

That nine-factor model has the lowest error on the data you have and the highest error on any data you'll see next. This is overfitting: the flexible model doesn't just learn the underlying relationship, it learns the measurement noise, the sampling flukes, the quirks of this particular dataset. Since noise doesn't repeat, a model that has memorized it generalizes badly.

The deeper lesson is that fit-to-data is a treacherous target. Every real dataset is signal plus noise, so chasing a perfect fit is, in Christian and Griffiths's framing, a kind of idolatry of data — treating the measurements as the thing itself rather than an imperfect proxy for it. Because we can only ever optimize against proxies, doing that optimization *too well* is a failure mode, not an achievement.

This inverts a common intuition. We assume errors of judgment come from thinking too little — being too quick, too crude, too simple. Overfitting shows the opposite error is just as real: you can consider too many factors, weigh them too finely, and tune yourself too precisely to evidence that won't recur. In statistics as in life, the question is never just "does this explain the past?" but "will it predict the future?" — and those two goals genuinely conflict.

## Overfit Bodies, Overfit Organizations

Evolution overfits too. Human taste buds are a proxy model for nutrition: sugar and fat were reliable signals of scarce, valuable calories in the ancestral environment, so we evolved to crave them. The modern food industry then gamed the proxy — refined sugar, frying, and artificial sweeteners deliver the taste signal decoupled from the nutrition it once indicated. Our palate is a model overfit to a world that no longer exists, which is why following it now leads us astray.

Organizations overfit whenever they optimize a metric instead of the goal the metric was meant to track. Reward police forces for arrest counts or response times and you get more arrests and faster arrivals — not necessarily less crime. Judge schools by standardized test scores and teachers teach to the test, raising scores without raising learning. Evaluate employees by hours logged and you buy presence, not productivity. Any measurable proxy, optimized hard enough, comes apart from the thing it proxies.

Training regimes show the same failure vividly. FBI agents drilled to fire two shots and reholster were found pausing to reholster in real firefights; police officers trained on firing ranges where they collected their spent brass were found stopping mid-gunfight to pocket their casings. They had overfit to the incidental details of practice. Competitive fencing and stylized martial-arts sparring likewise drifted into techniques that win points under scoring rules but would fail in genuine combat — mastery of the proxy, not the target.

The pattern is identical to the nine-factor polynomial: the more perfectly you adapt to the exact structure of your training environment, the more fragile you become when reality differs from it even slightly.

## Cross-Validation: How to Catch Yourself Overfitting

You can't detect overfitting by looking at fit — the overfit model always looks best on the data it was trained on. **Cross-validation** solves this by asking how well a model performs on data it *hasn't* seen. Hold back a random slice of your points, fit candidate models on the rest, then test each on the held-out slice. The simple model that fit the training data worse will typically predict the held-out data better, and the gap between training fit and holdout performance directly measures how badly a model has overfit.

The second form of cross-validation tests against a different *kind* of data entirely — a second proxy for the same underlying goal. If a school's rising test scores reflect real learning, students should also do well on a different, unannounced test they weren't coached for; if scores rise while the alternate measure stays flat, the system has overfit the metric. The same logic recommends occasional spot checks anywhere a proxy is being optimized: random audits alongside official reports, live evaluations alongside benchmark scores, a few interview questions no one can rehearse.

Cross-validation also reframes how to react when a cherished measure starts being gamed. The instinct is to abandon the metric; the algorithmic answer is subtler. Proxies are unavoidable — you can't optimize "true learning" or "public safety" directly — so the fix is not zero metrics but cheap, rotating secondary checks that reveal when the primary one has decoupled from reality. Detection, in other words, is a solved problem. The harder question is prevention, which is where regularization comes in.

## Regularization: Penalize Complexity Before It Costs You

If overfitting comes from excess complexity, prevent it by making complexity expensive. **Regularization** modifies the objective: instead of rewarding only fit to data, you charge a penalty that grows with the model's complexity, forcing every factor to justify its inclusion by improving fit more than it costs.

The most celebrated version is the **Lasso** (Robert Tibshirani's technique), which penalizes the total absolute weight a model places on all its factors. The Lasso's distinctive property is that it doesn't merely shrink minor factors — it drives their weights to exactly zero, deleting them. What survives is a short list of the variables that genuinely matter, which is why the Lasso is a workhorse of modern machine learning: it produces models that are simultaneously more accurate on new data and simple enough to interpret.

Nature enforces its own regularization. Metabolism charges a caloric price for every neuron and every extra heartbeat of deliberation, so organisms can't afford arbitrarily elaborate machinery; only complexity that pays for itself persists. Markets do something similar to firms: overhead penalizes baroque org charts and processes. Anywhere maintaining complexity carries a cost, that cost quietly biases the system toward simple, robust solutions.

The human version is imposing constraints on your own decision process. Charles Darwin, deciding whether to marry, wrote out a pro/con list — children and companionship versus lost time, expense, and "less money for books." Crucially, he confined the whole deliberation to a single page, ruling on the spot: "Marry—Marry—Marry Q.E.D." The one-page limit was a Lasso for the mind — a hard budget that forced only the weightiest considerations onto the ledger and prevented endless factor-hunting. He proposed to Emma Wedgwood soon after, and the marriage was a happy one.

## Simple Rules Beat Clever Models: Dawes, Gigerenzer, and the 1/N Portfolio

Regularization taken to its limit says: don't just shrink the weights — make them all equal, or use only one factor. Remarkably, this often wins.

Psychologist Robyn Dawes showed that **improper linear models** — formulas that skip fitting altogether and simply weight every relevant factor equally — predict about as well as fully optimized regression models across domains. His famous example: marital stability is well predicted by rate of lovemaking minus rate of arguments, with no fitted coefficients at all. When data is noisy, the "optimal" weights a regression learns are largely artifacts of that noise, so equal weights lose nothing real and gain robustness.

Gerd Gigerenzer's research group pushed further, showing that **fast and frugal heuristics** — rules like *Take the Best*, which decides using only the single most valid cue and ignores everything else — can outpredict multiple regression on real datasets. The finding reframes heuristics entirely: they aren't lamentable shortcuts of a limited brain but natural regularization, precisely tuned to a world where data is scarce and noisy. Ignoring information can be the statistically correct thing to do.

Finance supplies the sharpest anecdote. Harry Markowitz won the Nobel Prize for mean-variance portfolio optimization — the mathematics of ideal investment allocation. Asked how he invested his own retirement savings, he admitted he split contributions 50/50 between stocks and bonds. This wasn't hypocrisy but implicit wisdom: subsequent research found that the naive equal-split **1/N portfolio** frequently outperforms fitted optimal portfolios out of sample, because estimating the "optimal" weights from limited historical data overfits historical noise. Uncertainty about the future is exactly the condition under which erring on the side of simplicity is optimal — and the greater the uncertainty and the scarcer the data, the simpler you should go.

## Early Stopping: The Algorithmic Case for Thinking Less

Machine-learning systems trained iteratively — neural networks descending an error gradient — exhibit a telltale pattern: early in training they learn the broad, real structure of the data; only later do they begin absorbing its noise. Prediction accuracy on fresh data rises, peaks, and then *falls* as training continues. The remedy, **early stopping**, is to quit while you're ahead: halt training at the peak rather than running to full convergence. Stopping early is regularization implemented in time.

Human deliberation follows the same curve. The factors that come to mind first are usually the most important ones; extended rumination dredges up progressively more marginal considerations and weights them ever more finely — fitting the noise in your own evidence. This is one lens on why chronic overthinkers and perfectionists can make worse decisions than people who decide briskly: they aren't lazier thinkers' opposites, they're overfitters.

The chapter's closing prescription is conditional, not blanket. When your data is plentiful and clean — a well-understood domain, reliable feedback, stable conditions — think long and model finely; complexity earns its keep. But when evidence is scarce, noisy, or drawn from a world that may differ from the one you'll act in — career choices, relationships, forecasts years out — the rational strategy is to weigh the few factors you're confident about, resist the urge to keep elaborating, and stop early. Ben Franklin's "moral algebra" of listing and canceling pros and cons, Darwin's single page, the entrepreneur's rough plan over the perfect one: all are early stopping in action.

Thinking less, in the right circumstances, isn't a failure of rationality. It is rationality — the same mathematics that keeps a learning algorithm honest keeps a decision-maker sane.

## In Practice
- Before a big decision this week, write your pro/con list on a single page, cap it at the five factors that matter most, and rule when the page is full — no second page.
- Cross-validate one metric you're currently optimizing (at work or personally): pick an independent second measure of the same goal and check whether it's moving too.
- For any prediction built on thin or noisy data, replace your fine-tuned weighting with equal weights or the single best cue, Dawes- and Gigerenzer-style.
- Apply the Lasso to a plan or product: charge every feature, clause, or step an imaginary cost, and delete anything that doesn't visibly pay for itself.
- Set a deliberation deadline proportional to the quality of your evidence — abundant, reliable data earns long analysis; scarce, noisy data means decide from first factors and stop.
- Audit one habit or routine for overfitting to a past environment (the brass-casings test): ask whether you're optimizing for conditions that no longer hold.
