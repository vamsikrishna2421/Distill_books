# Bayes's Rule: Predicting from Almost Nothing

## Key Ideas
- Bayes's Rule turns a single observation into a rational prediction by combining what you see with a **prior** — your pre-existing belief about how the world is distributed.
- Laplace's Law gives the optimal estimate from tiny samples: after w successes in n attempts, expect a success rate of (w+1)/(n+2), which never claims certainty from limited evidence.
- Gott's Copernican Principle — assume you're not observing something at a special moment — predicts an object's total lifespan as double its current age, and it is exactly Bayes's Rule with an uninformative prior.
- Real-world quantities fall into three families, each with its own optimal rule: power-law distributions call for multiplying what you've seen, normal distributions call for predicting the average, and Erlang distributions call for adding a constant.
- Griffiths and Tenenbaum showed that ordinary people's single-datum predictions almost perfectly match the Bayesian optimum, meaning humans carry remarkably accurate priors absorbed from everyday experience.
- Because priors come from what you're exposed to, skewed inputs — like news media that report only rare events — corrupt your predictions; protecting your priors matters as much as reasoning well.

## One Data Point Is Enough — If You Have a Prior

How long will a movie's box-office run last? How long will your friend's new relationship survive? Often you must predict from a single observation. The mathematics for doing this began with **Thomas Bayes**, an eighteenth-century English Presbyterian minister whose essay on "the doctrine of chances" was published posthumously in 1763 by his friend Richard Price.

Bayes's insight was to reason *forward* from hypotheticals to reason *backward* from evidence. Imagine buying a ticket in a raffle you know nothing about, and winning. What fraction of tickets are winners? You can't know for sure — but you can imagine every possible raffle, ask how likely each one was to produce your result, and weight accordingly. This inverted reasoning — from observed effects back to hidden causes — is the foundation of all inference under uncertainty.

Pierre-Simon **Laplace** independently developed and completed the mathematics, producing what we now call **Bayes's Rule**: the probability of a hypothesis given the evidence is proportional to the probability of the evidence given the hypothesis, multiplied by the hypothesis's **prior probability** — how plausible it was before you looked. Priors are not cheating; they are required. Without an assumption about how raffles in general behave, a single winning ticket tells you nothing.

Laplace also derived a beautifully simple tool for small samples, **Laplace's Law**: if you've made n attempts and seen w successes, estimate the probability of success as **(w+1)/(n+2)**. Win the raffle on your first and only try, and the estimate is 2/3 — not the naive 100%. The formula effectively adds one imaginary success and one imaginary failure, keeping you from absurd overconfidence while still learning from every observation. Laplace applied it even to the sun: given all recorded human history of sunrises, the odds of the sun rising tomorrow are overwhelming but, formally, never quite certain. Laplace's Law works with any number of data points, including one, and it captures the essence of Bayesian humility: evidence updates belief, it doesn't replace it.

## The Copernican Principle: Doubling the Berlin Wall

In 1969, physicist **J. Richard Gott III** visited the Berlin Wall, then eight years old, and asked himself how long it would stand. His reasoning: unless there's evidence otherwise, assume your arrival is not a special moment in the wall's life — you're equally likely to show up at any point in its existence. On average, then, you arrive at the halfway mark. This **Copernican Principle** (nothing privileged about your vantage point, just as Copernicus dethroned Earth's) yields a startlingly simple prediction: expect the thing to last *as long again as it has already existed* — total lifespan, double the current age.

The Wall was eight years old, so Gott's best guess was roughly eight more years. It fell in 1989, twenty years later — comfortably inside his 50%-confidence window, which spans one-third to three times the observed age. Gott later published the method in *Nature* in 1993, applying it to everything from Broadway shows to the survival of the human species.

The Copernican Principle looks like magic, but it is simply Bayes's Rule run with an **uninformative prior** — the prior you use when you genuinely know nothing about the scale of what you're observing, so that all magnitudes are on equal footing. That explains both its power and its failures. Meet a 90-year-old man and the doubling rule predicts he'll live to 180; meet a 6-year-old and it predicts death at 12. Both are absurd, because you *do* know something about human lifespans: you have a rich, well-shaped prior. The Copernican estimate is what optimal prediction degrades to when prior knowledge is stripped away. When you have a real prior, Bayes's Rule tells you to use it — and the right rule depends entirely on the prior's shape.

## Three Distributions, Three Rules of Thumb

Most quantities you'll ever predict come from one of three families, and each family has its own optimal prediction rule.

**Power-law distributions** cover quantities that range across many scales with no typical value: town populations, personal wealth, movie box-office grosses. Most values are small, but a few are enormous — a film can gross half a billion, and average income says little about any individual. For these, Bayes prescribes the **Multiplicative Rule**: multiply the quantity observed so far by a constant factor characteristic of the distribution. For an uninformative power-law prior the factor is 2 — which is exactly Gott's Copernican doubling. For movie grosses the empirical factor is about 1.4: a film that has earned $6 million so far will probably end near $8.4 million. The rule is scale-free — the longer something power-law-distributed has lasted, the longer you should expect it to keep lasting.

**Normal (Gaussian) distributions** cluster around a typical value: human lifespans (averaging around 76), heights, crop yields. Here the optimal strategy is the **Average Rule**: predict the distribution's mean until the observation exceeds it, then predict just slightly more. A 6-year-old will live to about 77; a 90-year-old to about 94, not 180. Normal distributions have a sharp edge — surviving past the average buys you only a little extra expectation, because the tail falls off fast.

**Erlang distributions** describe intervals between independent events: how long a politician stays in office, runs of luck at a casino, waiting for radioactive decay. These are **memoryless** — how long something has already gone on tells you nothing new. The optimal strategy is the **Additive Rule**: always predict the thing will continue a constant amount longer, regardless of history. A senator should always expect a few more years in office, whether freshly elected or a decades-long incumbent; a gambler's streak is always, on expectation, the same fixed span from ending.

The three rules give opposite intuitions. Under a power law, the longer something has endured, the longer it will endure ("the rich get richer"). Under a normal distribution, the longer it has endured, the *sooner* it should end. Under an Erlang distribution, its age is simply irrelevant. Knowing which world you're in is the whole game.

## Humans Are Nearly Optimal Bayesians — With Good Data

Do people actually predict this way? **Tom Griffiths** (this book's co-author) and **Josh Tenenbaum** tested it directly. They gave ordinary people single-datum prediction problems: if a movie has grossed $10 million so far, what will it earn in total? If a man is 39, how long will he live? If a poem is 24 lines in so far — how long is the poem? If a congressman has served 11 years, how long is his total tenure?

The results, published in *Psychological Science* in 2006, were striking. People's median answers tracked the Bayes-optimal predictions almost exactly — including the switches between rules. For power-law quantities like movie grosses, people implicitly multiplied. For normally distributed quantities like lifespans, they predicted the average. For Erlang-like quantities, they added a constant. Nobody did arithmetic; the correct priors, absorbed from a lifetime of exposure to movies, lifespans, and elections, were simply *in* them, and their intuitions applied them nearly optimally.

The one revealing miss involved Egyptian pharaohs. Asked how long a pharaoh who had already reigned 11 years would rule in total, people reasoned with the right *form* of rule but overshot the truth — because they assumed ancient reigns resembled modern tenures, when in fact pharaohs often took the throne young and died young by modern standards. The inference machinery was flawless; the prior data fed into it was wrong. That distinction — good reasoning, bad inputs — turns out to be the chapter's most practical warning.

## Protect Your Priors: Media Skew and the Marshmallow Test

If human prediction is only as good as the priors behind it, then what you expose yourself to becomes an epistemic issue. Your brain builds priors from the frequency of what it encounters — and modern media systematically distort that frequency. News reports events *because* they are rare and dramatic: plane crashes over car crashes, murders over routine deaths. Consume enough of it and your internal statistics drift away from reality; studies of risk perception show people overestimate exactly the vivid causes of death that get coverage. The best remedy is deliberately unfiltered contact with representative reality, because everything you take in becomes training data for your future predictions.

The same logic reframes one of psychology's most famous findings. In **Walter Mischel's** 1960s **marshmallow test**, children who resisted eating one marshmallow to earn a second later showed better life outcomes, and the standard interpretation credited willpower. But waiting is only rational if you *believe* the second marshmallow will actually arrive — a Bayesian judgment about adult reliability. In 2012, **Celeste Kidd** at the University of Rochester tested this: before the marshmallow task, an experimenter either kept or broke a promise to bring better art supplies. Children who had just seen the adult prove unreliable waited only about three minutes on average; children with the reliable adult waited around twelve, most lasting the full test. A child who grabs the marshmallow may not lack self-control — she may have accurate priors about a world where promised rewards don't come. Failing the test can be optimal inference from hard experience, which suggests intervening on children's environments, not just their willpower.

## In Practice
- When predicting from one data point, first ask which family the quantity belongs to: many-scaled with rare giants (power-law), clustered around a typical value (normal), or memoryless waiting time (Erlang).
- Apply the matching rule: multiply what you've seen so far (power-law), predict the average — or slightly beyond it if already past (normal), or add a constant amount more regardless of history (Erlang).
- Use Laplace's Law for small-sample success rates: (successes + 1)/(attempts + 2), so one win in one try means an estimated 2/3, not certainty.
- When you truly know nothing about something's scale, use the Copernican default: guess it will last as long again as it already has.
- Audit your information diet this week — cut input streams that overrepresent rare, dramatic events, and seek representative, unfiltered samples of whatever you need to predict well.
- Before judging someone's "irrational" impatience or pessimism, ask what priors their experience would rationally produce — and whether the fix is changing their environment rather than their reasoning.
