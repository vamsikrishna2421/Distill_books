# Explore/Exploit: New Favorite or Old Reliable

## Key Ideas
- Repeated choices — restaurants, songs, friends, drugs in a clinical trial — are **multi-armed bandit** problems: you must balance exploring new options (gathering information) against exploiting known winners (using information you already have).
- The single most important variable is the time interval: exploration is most valuable early, when you have time to enjoy what you discover, and exploitation is most valuable late, when there is little future left to inform.
- The **Gittins index** solves the bandit problem exactly when future payoffs are geometrically discounted, and it proves that an untested option can be rationally worth more than a known good one — exploration carries a quantifiable bonus.
- **Upper Confidence Bound** algorithms replace the Gittins index in messier settings: always pick the option whose plausible best case is highest, embodying "optimism in the face of uncertainty," and your regret grows only logarithmically — you make fewer new mistakes every year.
- Adaptive trial designs like Zelen's play-the-winner show that standard 50/50 randomization has a human cost; the ECMO trials made that cost a matter of infant lives.
- Laura Carstensen's research reframes old age: shrinking social circles are not decline but rational late-interval exploitation of life's best-known rewards.

## Every Choice Is Also a Data Point

Do you return to your favorite restaurant or try the new one? Computer science calls this the explore/exploit trade-off, and it is not a metaphor for the tension between novelty and safety — it is the same problem. **Exploration** means gathering information about your options; **exploitation** means using the information you have to get the best known payoff. Crucially, "exploit" carries no negative charge here: savoring a beloved place with old friends is exploitation in its finest form.

The canonical formulation is the **multi-armed bandit problem**, named for the "one-armed bandit" slot machine. You face a casino row of machines, each paying off at a different, unknown rate. Every pull is simultaneously a bet and an experiment: it earns (or loses) money *and* teaches you about that machine. The best strategy must weigh both values at once.

The dilemma is genuinely hard because every data point is also a payoff. If a machine pays 9 times out of 15 and another paid once in two pulls, which do you choose? The expected values (using Laplace's rule from Chapter 6) favor the first, but the second is far more uncertain — and uncertainty means potential. Mathematicians found the general problem so intractable that during World War II, in Peter Whittle's telling, it "so sapped the energies and minds of Allied analysts" that they joked about dropping it on Germany as intellectual sabotage.

An early practical answer came from mathematician Herbert Robbins in 1952: **Win-Stay, Lose-Shift**. Keep pulling an arm as long as it pays; switch when it doesn't. This provably beats chance, but it's flawed — abandoning a machine after one loss is rash when its track record is long, and the rule ignores how much time you have left. That last omission turns out to be the heart of the matter.

## The Interval Makes the Strategy

How you should balance exploring and exploiting depends almost entirely on one thing: how long you'll be choosing. A new arrival in a city should try every promising restaurant; someone moving away next week should book their favorites. Information is only valuable if you have future opportunities to use it. So the rational pattern over any interval is to explore early and exploit late — gather information when it has a long shelf life, cash it in as the horizon closes.

This lens makes sense of Hollywood. Sequels are pure exploitation: a proven franchise milked rather than a new bet placed. Of the top-ten grossing films, two were sequels in 1981, three in 1991, five in 2001, and eight in 2011. That escalation tracks the industry's shrinking horizon — with ticket sales declining for years, studios behaved like gamblers who believe the casino is about to close: stop experimenting, squeeze the known winners. It is rational behavior, and also a signal of expected decline.

The interval also explains why children and the elderly look "irrational" to working-age adults. Developmental psychologist Alison Gopnik argues that childhood — a long, protected stretch of flitting attention and constant novelty-seeking — is exactly what an algorithm at the start of a very long interval should do: explore relentlessly, because every discovery has decades of use ahead. Toddlers aren't bad at focusing; they're superb at exploring.

The upshot for adults: don't judge each choice in isolation. A disappointing new restaurant isn't evidence you should have gone with the favorite — exploration pays off in the aggregate and over time. Early in any new interval (a job, a city, a hobby), over-weight novelty deliberately; late in an interval, drop the guilt about ordering the usual.

## The Gittins Index: A Price on the Unknown

In the 1970s, John Gittins — working on a Unilever problem about allocating research among drug projects — found what decades of mathematicians had missed: an exact, tractable solution to the bandit problem, given one assumption. Assume **geometric discounting**: each future payoff is worth some constant fraction (say 99%) of the one before, reflecting the chance the game ends or your interest wanes. Then every arm can be assigned a single number, the **Gittins index** (formally the "dynamic allocation index"): the guaranteed payoff rate you would accept to never pull that arm again. The optimal strategy becomes trivially simple — always pull the arm with the highest index.

The striking property is the **exploration bonus** built into the numbers. With payoffs discounted at 90% per pull, a completely untested machine has an index of 0.7029 — meaning it is worth more than a machine you *know* pays off 70% of the time. Uncertainty itself has value, because an unknown machine might turn out to be great, and if it doesn't you can simply walk away. Likewise, a machine that's paid once in two tries scores above one that's paid 9 in 18, despite identical observed rates: the smaller sample leaves more upside. The index even keeps failures forgivable — under high discounting, an arm can remain worth pulling after a string of losses.

The Gittins index vindicates trying new things, but its optimality is fragile. It assumes geometric discounting (many people don't discount that way) and no cost to switching arms; once switching is expensive — changing jobs, moving cities — the index is no longer optimal, and sticking with a decent known option gains appeal. Computing it is also painful in practice. That fragility motivated a different, more robust approach: instead of maximizing discounted payoff, minimize regret.

## Regret, Optimism, and the Upper Confidence Bound

Define **regret** as the gap between what you actually earned and what you'd have earned playing the best arm from the start. In 1985, Herbert Lai and Tze Leung Robbins proved fundamental results about it: assuming you're not omniscient, regret must grow with time under any strategy — but the best possible strategies make it grow only **logarithmically**. Logarithmic regret means you make as many mistakes in your first ten pulls as in your next ninety: each year of experience brings fewer new errors than the last, and your average payoff per pull keeps climbing toward the maximum.

Several algorithms achieve this optimal rate; the most influential are **Upper Confidence Bound (UCB)** algorithms. Instead of scoring each arm by its expected value, compute a confidence interval around each arm's payoff rate and rank arms by the *top* of that interval — the best they could plausibly be given the evidence. A machine pulled once tells you little, so its interval is wide and its upper bound high; a machine pulled a thousand times has a tight interval hugging its observed rate.

UCB thus formalizes a principle the authors call **optimism in the face of uncertainty**: give the unknown the benefit of the doubt, because options are only ever eliminated by evidence, never by pessimism. If the optimism proves unfounded, a few more pulls reveal it cheaply; if it proves right, you've found a new best arm. Like the Gittins index, UCB awards an exploration bonus to under-sampled options, but it needs no discounting assumption and is far easier to compute — which is why it now underlies much of machine learning, website optimization, and recommendation systems.

The human translation: in new social and professional situations, accept the invitation, take the meeting, try the option you can't yet evaluate. Over a long interval, the strategy that minimizes lifetime regret is precisely the one that keeps giving uncertain things a chance.

## A/B Tests and the Ethics of Experiments

The web turned the bandit problem into daily commercial practice. In **A/B testing**, a site shows different users different versions of a page and shifts traffic toward whatever performs better. Google ran its first A/B test in 2000 and later famously tested some forty-one shades of blue for a toolbar. Dan Siroker brought the method to Barack Obama's 2008 campaign, where testing splash-page media and button text ("Learn More" beat "Sign Up") lifted sign-up rates about 40% — worth roughly $60 million in donations. Every major site you use is, in effect, a casino of experiments in which you are the arm being pulled.

Medicine faces the same mathematics with lives at stake. Standard randomized clinical trials split patients 50/50 for the whole study — pure exploration, even after the data start favoring one treatment. In 1969, biostatistician Marvin Zelen proposed an adaptive alternative, **play-the-winner**: imagine drawing treatment assignments from a hat, and each time a treatment succeeds, add another ball for it. Successes tilt subsequent patients toward whatever is working, blending exploration with exploitation.

The stakes became vivid in the **ECMO** controversy. Robert Bartlett's team at Michigan developed extracorporeal membrane oxygenation for newborns with respiratory failure and tested it in the 1980s with a Zelen-style randomized play-the-winner design: one infant received conventional treatment and died; eleven consecutively received ECMO and all survived. Critics dismissed the lopsided design, so a Harvard-led team under James Ware ran a compromise trial: randomize until four deaths accumulated in either arm, then switch every patient to the better treatment. Four of ten conventionally treated infants died; all nine ECMO infants lived. Even then skeptics demanded a conventional UK trial in the 1990s — during which roughly twice as many infants died in the conventional arm before ECMO's superiority was accepted. Statistician Don Berry and others argue such deaths were the avoidable price of ignoring bandit algorithms: adaptive designs can deliver rigorous evidence while treating far more patients with the winning arm.

## Restless Worlds and the Rationality of Old Age

Stanford psychologist Laura Carstensen asked why older people's social networks shrink, and rejected the sad standard story. Her **socioemotional selectivity theory** says the narrowing is deliberate and rational: it's the exploit phase. With a shorter perceived horizon, investing in unknown new acquaintances has little expected payoff, so older adults prune their circles to the deepest, most reliable relationships — and report *higher* emotional well-being, not loneliness.

Her experiments confirm the interval, not age itself, drives the pattern. Asked to choose a companion for thirty minutes — a family member, the author of a book they'd read, or a new acquaintance — older people picked family and younger people the novel options. But tell older subjects to imagine a medical breakthrough granting twenty extra years, and their choices matched the young. Tell young people to imagine moving across the country soon, and they chose like the old. Young people facing genuinely short horizons — the study extended to contexts like political handovers and illness — likewise shift to exploitation. Old-age "narrowing" is the same algorithm every rational agent should run at the end of an interval: cash in your best-known rewards.

One caveat keeps exploration alive at every age: the world is a **restless bandit**. Real options' payoffs drift — restaurants change chefs, friends move, skills go stale — and once arms can change behind your back, the problem becomes formally intractable and continued periodic exploration stays necessary forever. And exploration's costs are real: whichever way you err, expect to feel it. The math's consolation is that over a long enough run, following these strategies means your regrets grow ever more slowly — and that trying the new thing, most of the time you're in doubt, is not recklessness but optimality.

## In Practice
- Anchor every explore/exploit decision to the interval: new to a city, job, or field, deliberately over-sample novelty; in your final months somewhere, book the favorites without guilt.
- Give under-tested options an explicit optimism bonus: judge a new restaurant, tool, or contact by its plausible upside, not its average, and eliminate it only with evidence.
- Judge exploration by the portfolio, not the pull: log new things you try each month and expect most to disappoint — the occasional new favorite pays for all of them.
- Before big switches, price the switching cost: when changing is expensive (jobs, homes), demand a larger edge before abandoning a known-good option.
- A/B test anything you publish repeatedly — emails, listings, landing pages — and shift traffic toward winners as data accumulates rather than splitting evenly forever.
- Reread declining industries and aging relatives through the interval lens: sequels and shrinking social circles are rational exploitation, and they tell you what horizon someone perceives.
