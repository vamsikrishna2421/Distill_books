# Preacher, Prosecutor, Politician — or Scientist?

## Key Ideas
- We spend years building knowledge and opinions, then defend them like prized possessions — but in a fast-changing world, the ability to *rethink* and unlearn matters more than raw intelligence.
- When we think and talk, we default to three modes that block rethinking: the **preacher** (protecting sacred beliefs), the **prosecutor** (attacking others' reasoning), and the **politician** (campaigning for approval). The alternative is **scientist mode**: treating opinions as hypotheses to test.
- In a randomized experiment by Arnaldo Camuffo's team, Italian startup founders trained to think like scientists earned dramatically more revenue and pivoted more than twice as often as untrained peers.
- Mental horsepower doesn't guarantee mental dexterity: smarter people can be *better* at rationalizing their existing views, and almost everyone believes bias afflicts others more than themselves.
- The **first-instinct fallacy** is a myth: eraser-mark studies show most answer changes on tests go from wrong to right. Hesitating to revise usually costs us, and the discomfort of doubt is a sign of learning, not weakness.
- The hardest things to abandon are the tools and identities that once made us successful — like the firefighters at Mann Gulch who died running with heavy equipment they should have dropped.

## Mann Gulch: The Fatal Refusal to Drop Our Tools

In August 1949, fifteen smokejumpers parachuted into Montana's Mann Gulch expecting a routine fire. The wind shifted, the blaze jumped the gulch, and suddenly the crew was racing uphill ahead of flames closing at roughly 700 feet per minute. Twelve men died — many within a few hundred yards of the ridge that meant safety.

Foreman **Wagner Dodge** survived through an act of rethinking under pressure: he stopped running, lit a match, and burned a patch of grass ahead of him — an **escape fire**. By lying down in the fresh ashes, he removed the fuel around him and let the wall of flame roar past. None of his crew joined him; the idea was too foreign, and there was no time to explain.

The subtler tragedy is that the others never dropped their axes, saws, and packs. Analyses later suggested some could have outrun the fire had they shed the weight. The same pattern killed firefighters again in 1994 on Storm King Mountain, where crew members died still wearing heavy packs and clutching tools. Carrying equipment is what firefighters *do* — abandoning it feels like abandoning who they are.

Adam Grant's point is that we all run with our tools. Our tools are assumptions, habits, and beliefs that once served us. The more they've defined our success and identity, the harder they are to release — even when they're the very things slowing us down. Rethinking isn't just an intellectual nicety; sometimes it's survival. And the skill most neglected in a world obsessed with learning is **unlearning**: knowing when to let a hard-won belief go.

## The Four Mindsets: Three That Block Rethinking, One That Enables It

Drawing on work by psychologist **Phil Tetlock**, Grant describes three professional mindsets we slip into when we think and talk — none of which involves changing our own minds:

- **Preacher mode**: our sacred beliefs feel threatened, so we deliver sermons to protect and promote our ideals. The goal is conversion, not truth.
- **Prosecutor mode**: we spot flaws in other people's reasoning and marshal arguments to convict them of being wrong. Winning the case replaces getting it right.
- **Politician mode**: we campaign for the approval of an audience — lobbying, flattering, telling constituencies what they want to hear. We may update what we *say* without updating what we *think*.

The risk isn't holding these roles as jobs; it's getting mentally trapped in them. In all three modes, being right is a foregone conclusion; the only question is how to defend, attack, or sell.

The fourth mode is **thinking like a scientist**. It doesn't require a lab coat — it's a mindset defined by searching for truth: favoring humility over pride, doubt over certainty, and curiosity over closure. In scientist mode, your opinions are hypotheses, your decisions are experiments, and disagreements are chances to learn rather than battles to win. You run experiments to test ideas, follow the evidence even when it stings, and treat being wrong as progress. As Grant puts it, "If knowledge is power, knowing what we don't know is wisdom."

Crucially, actual scientists aren't immune: when defending pet theories, credentialed researchers preach, prosecute, and politick like everyone else. Scientist mode is a discipline anyone can practice and any scientist can abandon.

## Proof It Pays: The Italian Startup Experiment

Does scientist mode produce better real-world results, or just nicer conversations? Economist **Arnaldo Camuffo** and colleagues ran a randomized controlled trial with more than a hundred founders of early-stage Italian startups spanning furniture, food, healthcare, and retail.

All founders received a four-month entrepreneurship curriculum. The treatment group got one addition: they were taught to think like scientists — to view their business strategy as a theory, customer interviews as hypothesis development, and product launches as experiments whose results should drive decisions to persist, adjust, or pivot.

The gap was striking. Over the following year, the control-group startups averaged under $300 in revenue; the scientific-thinking startups averaged over $12,000, and generated revenue more than twice as fast. They were also more than twice as likely to **pivot** to a different business model. Control-group founders tended to preach the virtues of their original idea, prosecute critics who doubted it, and politick by hyping it — staying anchored to their first plan because admitting fault felt like failure. The scientist-founders felt freer to change course, because a rejected hypothesis is just information.

Grant frames the two trajectories as opposing loops. The **overconfidence cycle** runs from pride to conviction, which invites **confirmation bias** (seeing what we expect to see) and **desirability bias** (seeing what we want to see), which delivers validation and swells pride further. The **rethinking cycle** runs from humility to doubt, doubt to curiosity, and curiosity to discovery — which produces new learning and keeps humility honest. The first loop feels good and calcifies; the second feels uncomfortable and compounds.

## BlackBerry: How a Genius Fails to Rethink

**Mike Lazaridis** was a genuine prodigy — an inventor obsessed with electronics from childhood who co-founded Research In Motion and created the BlackBerry, brilliantly reimagining the mobile device as a secure wireless email machine with a physical keyboard. By summer 2009, BlackBerry held nearly half the US smartphone market. By 2014, its share had collapsed to less than 1 percent.

The fall wasn't a failure of intelligence; it was a failure of rethinking. When the iPhone appeared, Lazaridis dismissed the ideas at its core. He couldn't accept that people would want a full computer in their pocket rather than a focused email device, that they'd tolerate typing on glass instead of his beloved keyboard, or that home users choosing apps would outweigh corporate IT departments choosing security. Even years into the decline, engineers' proposals — a browser-centric device, new messaging strategies for ordinary consumers — were rebuffed because they clashed with his original theory of what a smartphone was.

Lazaridis fell into the trap of treating his own past success as proof his assumptions were permanent truths. The very vision that built the company became the tool he wouldn't drop. Grant's warning: the better our ideas have worked before, the more conviction we invest in them, and the more we defend them in preacher and prosecutor mode when the world shifts.

The pattern generalizes. Intelligence can make the problem worse: research shows the smarter people are, the better they are at generating reasons why they're right — and studies of politically charged math problems find strong quantitative reasoners are *more* polarized, not less, using their skill to twist data toward their side. Nearly everyone also exhibits the **"I'm not biased" bias**: we're confident that bias distorts other people's judgment while ours stays clear, and more objective self-ratings often accompany more biased behavior.

## Cognitive Laziness and the First-Instinct Fallacy

Why is rethinking so rare? Partly effort: psychologists describe humans as **cognitive misers** — we prefer the ease of clinging to old views over the work of wrestling with new ones. Questioning ourselves also destabilizes: it makes the world less predictable and can feel like a threat to identity. So we engage in what researchers call **seizing and freezing**: we grab an answer that gives us closure and lock it in, favoring the comfort of conviction over the discomfort of doubt.

We even romanticize this laziness as intuition. Generations of students have been warned not to second-guess themselves on multiple-choice tests — go with your gut. This is the **first-instinct fallacy**. When psychologist **Justin Kruger** and colleagues counted eraser marks on more than 1,500 students' exams in Illinois, only about a quarter of answer changes moved from right to wrong; half moved from wrong to right. A review of 33 studies on answer changing found that in every single one, the majority of revisions improved scores. Changing answers doesn't hurt on average — *hesitating to reconsider* does.

The catch is that rethinking has to actually happen, not just erasing. Students who deliberately reconsider whether they're wrong, and instructors who teach that first answers deserve scrutiny, do better. The broader lesson extends past test-taking: we hold on to first impressions of people, stale career plans, and outdated mental models the same way we hold on to answer A. Grant suggests thinking of beliefs the way you think of possessions during spring cleaning — schedule regular checkups and ask which convictions you formed years ago that deserve re-examination now. (Later chapters build the toolkit: calibrating confidence, separating opinions from identity, and fighting well over ideas.)

Rethinking, in short, is a learnable skill set sitting on top of a mindset. The mindset is the scientist's; the enemy is not ignorance but the illusion that what we already know is enough.

## In Practice

- Catch yourself mid-sentence this week: when you're defending, attacking, or performing in a discussion, name the mode — preacher, prosecutor, or politician — and consciously switch to asking a testing question instead.
- Rewrite one strong opinion you hold as a hypothesis: state what evidence would change your mind, then go look for that evidence.
- Before a decision at work, design it as an experiment: define the prediction, the measure, and the point at which you'll pivot — like Camuffo's scientist-founders.
- On tests, forms, or estimates, revisit your first answers and ask "is there a reason to doubt this?" — revise when you find one, since most changes go wrong-to-right.
- List the "tools" tied to your identity — a skill, process, or belief that powered past success — and ask honestly whether any has become the pack you're running uphill with.
- Put a recurring "belief checkup" on your calendar: pick one assumption formed years ago and re-evaluate it against what you know today.
