# Adventures in Mental Time Travel

The last night with Annie Duke, friend — and it opens with a crime that is committed in your home, every single week, by a culprit you will never catch. Jerry Seinfeld confessed it best in a stand-up bit: **Night Jerry** stays up late having fun, because getting up exhausted is *Morning Jerry's* problem. Morning Jerry despises Night Jerry — but he's powerless. He can't retaliate... except by oversleeping, and robbing a still-later Jerry.

Laugh, friend — then notice you run the same con. The dessert tonight is billed to a stranger decades away. The angry email feels magnificent to the self who sends it; a different self wakes up to the replies. Tonight, the finale: **how to bring all your selves into the room where one of them is about to do something stupid.**

## The committee across time

![A person at a desk facing translucent past and future versions of themselves seated on either side, moonlight through a window](/story-art/thinking-in-bets/06-timetravel.jpg)

The mechanism is **temporal discounting**: we weight immediate rewards far more than future costs, even when the trade is objectively terrible. And Duke's fix is the book's most charming: **mental time travel** — deliberately summoning your past and future selves into the decision. When a choice is made only by the present self, it is made by *the self with the worst incentives*. Recruit Past You — who has watched these choices play out before — and Future You — who inherits the consequences — and the negotiation balances: an internal committee spanning your whole timeline.

The ready-made tool is Suzy Welch's **10/10/10**: before deciding, ask — *how will I feel about this in ten minutes? Ten months? Ten years?* The intervals matter less than the summons: future selves dragged into a moment that in-the-moment feeling would otherwise rule alone. It works backward too — *how did I feel about the last version of this choice, ten months later?* — calling your past selves as witnesses.

And when emotion floods anyway, poker has the word: **tilt**. After the brutal bad beat, feelings swamp the system, the deliberative mind goes offline, and the wild decisions pour out of the emotional center — the blowout argument, the rage-quit, the revenge email are all tilt by other names. The defense is a zoom: imagine watching your happiness like a nervous investor watching a live ticker — every downtick a catastrophe, losses stinging double. Now switch to the *one-year chart*: the flat tire in the rain vanishes into the trend. Learn your tilt signature — the heat in the face, the rising voice, the repeated grievance — and precommit: **no irreversible decisions while any sign is present.** And run Kahneman's most useful inversion: regret normally arrives *after* the decision, when it can only punish. **Move it in front** — "how will I feel tomorrow about what I'm about to do?" Anticipated regret, unlike the retrospective kind, can still change the choice.

## Contracts with the mast

You met Ulysses two books ago, friend — lashed to the mast, wax in the crew's ears. Duke names the general device: a **Ulysses contract** — a precommitment made by your rational present self that binds your irrational future one. Two flavors, both essential.

**Barrier-raising**: make the bad choice harder — the poker player who leaves her bankroll at home so tilt *can't* rebuy; no junk food in the house; the cut-up credit card. **Barrier-lowering**: make the good choice automatic — payroll savings that move before you see them; the workout partner already waiting; vegetables pre-chopped at eye level. And here is the subtle gift: even *weak* contracts pay, because every precommitment installs a **decision-interrupt** — a speed bump at exactly the point where autopilot would take over. You can still break the contract... but now you must *notice* you're breaking it, and the noticing is often enough to wake the deliberative mind.

::: guess
Duke's most portable Ulysses contract costs nothing and travels everywhere: the "decision swear jar." What goes in it — and what's the fine?
---
A watch-list of **words and thoughts that signal motivated reasoning** — you commit to catching yourself saying them: *"I'm 100% sure." "There's no way." "Always." "Never." "I knew it." "I told you so." "So unlucky"* (while banking wins as skill). *"They're idiots."* Plus the quieter tells: performing for an audience instead of truthseeking, and shutting out information because it threatens your story. And the fine, friend, isn't money — **each catch charges you a moment of honest self-examination**: stop, and vet the belief underneath the phrase. It's the perfect miniature of the whole book: the bias can't be deleted, but it can be *flagged* — and the flag summons the audit. Write your five phrases this week. You'll be shocked how often the jar rattles.
:::

## Scouting the future

Poker players can't know the next card — so they master **reconnaissance on the future**: before committing chips, map the possible outcomes and price each one. Transferred to life, it's **scenario planning**: list the realistic futures, attach rough probabilities, exactly as an oddsmaker prices a race. Three payoffs: better bets (you can't price what you haven't enumerated); *preparedness* — imagined futures can't blindside you; and better teams — agree on scenarios and odds up front, and later disagreements become disciplined arguments about estimates rather than after-the-fact blame.

Then work **backward**. In **backcasting**, you time-travel to the *successful* future — write the headline: "We hit the goal" — and reconstruct the story of how it happened: the intermediate steps, the resources, the decision points, the lucky breaks that had to fall your way. If the success story turns out to require three long-shots in a row — better to learn it now, while the plan can still change. But backcasting interrogates only the happy branch, friend, and inherits your optimism. It needs its pessimistic twin.

::: guess
Gary Klein's "premortem" gathers the team BEFORE a decision and assigns a strange writing exercise. What is it — and why does the exact verb tense matter so much?
---
The team time-travels to a future where the project **has already failed** — and each member writes the story of *why*. Not "what could go wrong?" but **"it went wrong — what happened?"** The tense is the active ingredient: researchers found that **prospective hindsight** — imagining an event as already having occurred — improves people's ability to identify reasons for future outcomes by about **30%**. Treating failure as accomplished fact unlocks explanations the "maybe" framing hides. And the premortem does social work too: in most rooms, voicing doubts reads as disloyalty — the premortem makes pessimism *the assignment*, rewarding whoever finds the fatal flaw. It is last night's devil's advocate, institutionalized in miniature. Paired with backcasting, the map is complete: routes to success and routes to failure, probabilities attached. Some failures you can now prevent; some you can only brace for; and some plans, seen whole, deserve abandonment — the cheapest lesson ever sold, at premortem prices.
:::

And the map pays one final dividend, *after* the outcome lands. Hindsight bias works by collapse — once one future becomes fact, the alternatives evaporate and the result feels inevitable. But if you mapped the tree in advance, **you hold a record proving the outcome was one branch among many** — your own pre-decision numbers say the 20% disaster was always on the map. The mapped future protects you twice: before the bet, by improving it — and after, by letting you field the outcome honestly instead of resulting on yourself.

## The whole book in one breath

Here is *Thinking in Bets*, friend, as I'd whisper it at the door:

*Life is poker, not chess — luck stands between every decision and its result, so judge the process, never the single draw. Every choice is a bet against the other you, priced by your beliefs — so vet the beliefs: ask "wanna bet?", hold them at percentages, and let updating feel like winning. Field your outcomes honestly — luck its due on wins, skill its due on losses — and let the honest sort compound like a house edge. Don't fight your biases alone: charter a pod, ban the bad-beat stories, share the ungroomed data, judge messages not messengers, stop the story before the ending. Pay for dissent, because it never volunteers. And when the moment burns hot — summon the committee across time: ten minutes, ten months, ten years; lash the mast; rattle the swear jar; write the failure story before you bet. You can't be right every time, friend. You can only be a little better calibrated than yesterday — and over enough hands, that is everything.*

## Key takeaways

- Night Jerry robs Morning Jerry: temporal discounting hands decisions to the self with the worst incentives — summon the committee across time.
- 10/10/10 and previewed regret: drag ten-minute, ten-month, ten-year You into the moment, and move regret in front of the choice.
- Know your tilt signature and zoom to the one-year chart — no irreversible decisions while any warning sign is present.
- Ulysses contracts raise barriers to bad and lower barriers to good — and even weak ones install the decision-interrupt that wakes deliberation.
- Keep a decision swear jar: catch "I'm sure," "I knew it," "so unlucky," "they're idiots" — each catch buys a moment of vetting.
- Backcast from success, premortem from failure (past tense = +30% insight), and keep the map — it defeats hindsight bias when the outcome lands.
