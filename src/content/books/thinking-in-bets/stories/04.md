# The Buddy System: Building a Truthseeking Group

Fourth night, friend — and it begins with a confession about last night's homework. Fielding your outcomes honestly, betting on your own excuses, relocating your pride... did you feel how *hard* it is alone? There's a reason. The attorney in your head has argued your case for decades, knows every soft spot, and never sleeps.

So tonight Duke stops asking you to fight alone — and reveals where the world's best players actually get their edge. Not from solitary discipline. From a *pod*.

## The lawyer in your head

![Three poker players in a quiet corner booth after hours, cards spread between them, leaning in over one hand replayed](/story-art/thinking-in-bets/04-pod.jpg)

Here's the finding that reframes everything: cognitive scientists Mercier and Sperber argue that human reason never evolved as a private truth-finding instrument at all. It evolved *socially* — to build persuasive arguments and to evaluate other people's. Seen this way, confirmation bias isn't a design flaw, friend. **It's the engine of advocacy.** An individual reasoning alone is a lawyer building one side's case — which is why knowing about your biases doesn't cure them, and why smarter people just build better briefs.

But the same architecture becomes a *strength* in a group. Mercier and Sperber found that while we're lazy and biased generating our own arguments, we are demanding and fairly objective evaluating *someone else's*. Put a few motivated advocates in a room with a norm of mutual evaluation, and the biases start **canceling instead of compounding** — my blind spots are invisible to me but obvious to you, and vice versa. Duke lived it: her brother Howard's circle of world-class players never handed her sympathy after losses; they interrogated her decisions — and did for her what she could never do for herself. The practical conclusion is wonderfully modest: you don't need to become unbiased — an impossible project. You need **two or three people whose job is to catch what you can't.**

## Not all groups make you smarter

But wait — most people already have groups. Friends, colleagues, group chats. Why aren't we all calibrated geniuses?

The opposite: **group polarization.** Like-minded groups exit discussion holding *more extreme* versions of the views they walked in with — echo chambers aren't neutral, they are *bias-multiplication machines*. And the default social contract in most groups — poker rooms and offices alike — is exactly wrong for truth: *"I'll agree with your version if you agree with mine."* Mutual validation societies, where everyone confirms everyone's terrible luck and nobody's play improves. That's why truthseeking must be **opted into explicitly**. Duke's cautionary tale is a talk-show moment: David Letterman, hearing actress Lauren Graham describe the endless drama in her life, skipped sympathy and offered a hypothesis — maybe *she* was the common element, choosing "crazy" friends. It landed terribly — not because the idea was worthless, but because Graham *hadn't signed up for truthseeking*; she'd come for a sympathetic chat. The lesson cuts both ways, friend: never impose accuracy-first feedback on people who didn't agree to it — and inside your pod, make the agreement *explicit*: a charter stating the goal is accuracy, dissent is welcome, and naming each other's motivated reasoning is help, not attack. One more requirement: **diversity of viewpoint** — a pod sharing all your priors can run the charter perfectly and still polarize, because no one in the room can generate the disconfirming argument.
:::

## Seidel's rule: no bad-beat stories

Erik Seidel — decades at the very top of world poker — enforces one filter in his circle, and it sounds almost rude: **he refuses to listen to bad-beat stories.** The unlucky river card, the miracle draw that stole your pot — he simply won't hear it.

Why? Because complaints about luck are *conversationally satisfying and informationally empty*. Luck is the part you couldn't control; there is nothing to learn from re-narrating it — and every retelling *trains* the self-serving sort from last night, rehearsing losses into the luck bucket. Seidel's circle reserves its attention for a different exchange entirely: *"I faced this spot, here were my considerations — what would you have done, and why?"* Strategy questions have answers that change future behavior. Luck stories don't.

And here the group plugs into the habit machinery, friend: **we repeat what gets rewarded.** If your social reward is pity, you'll produce grievances. If approval flows to well-reasoned analysis and to admitting an error the group helped you find, you'll produce *those* — until members genuinely start *wanting* to find their own mistakes, because mistake-finding is the currency of status inside the pod. The filter also keeps the group honest about what it is: a pod that tolerates venting becomes a support group. Support groups have their place — but keep at least one group that is unambiguously about *getting better*.

## CUDOS: the operating manual

For the pod's actual rules, Duke borrows from an unexpected source: sociologist Robert Merton, who in 1942 codified the ethos that makes *science itself* self-correcting. Four norms — **CUDOS** — and each maps straight onto your pod.

**Communism** — of data, not politics: within the group, information is common property. No groomed stories; share *all* the details, including the incriminating ones, and let members ask the enforcement question: *"what are you leaving out?"* A doctor can't diagnose from a censored chart. **Universalism**: judge the message, never the messenger — Duke admits she long dismissed observations from players she'd labeled bad, and lost a lesson every time. Evaluate each idea as if you didn't know its source; don't wave through the high-status and don't wall out the low. **Disinterestedness**: keep conflicts of interest out — and the most insidious conflict is *knowing how things turned out*.

::: guess
Duke discovered a specific way of presenting a decision to her group that dramatically improved the quality of their feedback. What was the trick?
---
**Stop the story before the ending.** "I got my chips in with a pair against a flush draw — thoughts?" — and let the group judge the decision on *only the information available at the time*. She calls it outcome-blind analysis, and it works because the moment listeners hear the result, they reason *backward* from it — hindsight bias and resulting contaminate every judgment downstream. Tell hand histories without endings and the feedback quality jumps instantly. Use it everywhere, friend: present the project decision, the hiring call, the investment thesis to your reviewers *without revealing how it went* — collect their reads first, then unveil. And the fourth norm ties the room together: **Organized Skepticism** — doubt as a communal duty, not an insult; engagement by question ("what would make this wrong?") rather than verdict. In a CUDOS pod, dissent isn't disloyalty. It's the membership fee.
:::

## The imagined cross-examination

The final payoff is the strangest: a good pod improves your thinking **even when it isn't there**. Accountability — knowing you'll have to explain your reasoning to people who judge *process*, not results — recruits your future embarrassment as a present-tense decision aid.

Duke felt it at the table: mid-tilt, steaming after a loss, tempted by a reckless call... and she'd hear the question her group would ask — *"why did you do that?"* — and fold. The anticipated conversation worked as a real-time filter, pre-loading the deliberative mind into moments the reflexive mind would otherwise own. **You are effectively never deciding alone; the imagined audience is always present.** It reshapes the after, too: knowing the pod demands the full, ungroomed account — heard outcome-blind — you start recording decisions honestly *as you make them*, because you can't retrofit a flattering story you'll be cross-examined on. Duke seals it with John Stuart Mill's line: *he who knows only his own side of the case knows little of that.* A good pod guarantees you never know only your own side. The entire technology, friend: **three people, a written charter, CUDOS norms, and a ban on bad-beat stories.** It compounds every week you run it. Recruit yours this week.

## Before you go

So: reason is a lawyer, not a scientist — and only opposing counsel keeps it honest; like-minded comfort polarizes; Seidel bans the stories that teach nothing; CUDOS supplies the constitution; and the pod's cross-examination guards you even in solitude.

But two hard problems remain, friend. First: most of the people in your life *never signed the charter* — the spouse, the boss, the friend who just wants sympathy — so how do you carry truthseeking into rooms that didn't opt in? And second, the deepest enemy of all: the version of you that exists *inside the moment* — tilted, tempted, hungry, certain — who outvotes every careful plan. ## Key takeaways

- Reason evolved for advocacy, not truth: alone you're a lawyer; in a mutual-evaluation pod, biases cancel instead of compound.
- Like-minded groups polarize (Sunstein) — truthseeking must be opted into explicitly, with a charter and viewpoint diversity.
- Seidel's rule: ban bad-beat stories — reward strategy questions and error-finding, and status starts flowing to accuracy.
- CUDOS: share ungroomed data, judge messages not messengers, present decisions outcome-blind, and make skepticism a duty.
- Stop the story before the ending: outcome-blind review is the single biggest upgrade to feedback quality.
- Accountability travels: the imagined cross-examination filters your decisions even when you're alone.
