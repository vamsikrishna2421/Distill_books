# Dissent to Win: Engineering Productive Disagreement

Fifth night, friend — and it opens in an unexpected place for a poker book: the Vatican, in the year 1587, where the Pope has just created the strangest job in Christendom.

Before anyone could be declared a saint, one official was now *required* to argue **against** the sainthood — to probe the miracles, attack the evidence, and represent the case that the whole gathered assembly did not want to hear. His formal title: **advocatus diaboli**. The devil's advocate. Not a heckler, not a cynic — a *appointed officer of doubt*.

Why would the Church pay a man to argue against its own saints? Because it had learned what tonight's chapter teaches: **dissent is so valuable, and so unnatural, that it will never volunteer. It must be manufactured.**

## The audience in your head

![A lone cardinal in red robes standing to speak against a long candlelit table of seated clergy in a Renaissance hall](/story-art/thinking-in-bets/05-advocate.jpg)

Start with a finding that should change how you prepare every argument. Researchers Tetlock and Lerner distinguish two styles of thought: **confirmatory** — one-sided lawyering for a conclusion you already hold — and **exploratory** — genuinely weighing alternatives before settling. Confirmatory is the default. Exploratory must be *induced*. And the inducer is **accountability**: expecting to justify your reasoning to others.

But here's the twist that matters: accountability can push you *either way*, depending on the audience. Know your audience's views in advance, and accountability makes you **worse** — you tailor, you bolster, you slide deeper into confirmation. Exploratory thought emerges only when you know you'll answer to an audience whose views are **unknown**, who care about accuracy, *before* you've formed your opinion. That is precisely why the truthseeking pod works its magic, friend — it makes you perpetually accountable to accuracy-minded listeners whose reactions you can't script. And it's why the pod's load-bearing wall is *disagreement*: Mill again — he who knows only his own side of the case knows little of that. Your own mind will never supply the strongest opposing argument. **Someone has to be assigned.**

## Manufacturing the tenth man

What happened when the Church *stopped* paying its officer of doubt? In 1983, Pope John Paul II effectively abolished the devil's advocate role.

::: guess
With the assigned dissenter removed after four centuries, what happened to the canonization rate?
---
He canonized roughly **500 saints — more than his predecessors of the preceding five centuries combined.** Remove the assigned dissenter, and the approval machine runs unopposed — exactly as the model predicts. And the institutions that cannot afford blind spots all learned the same lesson, usually the hard way. The CIA runs **red teams** — analysts explicitly tasked with attacking the prevailing analysis. The State Department has protected a formal dissent channel since 1971. And after Israel was blindsided by the 1973 Yom Kippur attack — despite ample warnings that a *unanimous* intelligence consensus had explained away — its military intelligence adopted the **tenth-man doctrine**: *if everyone in the room agrees, someone is obligated to build the case that they're all wrong.* The design does two things at once, friend: it guarantees the strongest counterargument gets voiced — and it **decouples disagreement from disloyalty**. The red-teamer isn't being difficult; she's doing her job. Rotate the role in your own pod — today you attack my read, tomorrow I attack yours — and challenging each other stops being aggression and becomes the *norm*.
:::

## Every story is missing its other side

Kurosawa's film *Rashomon* tells one violent encounter through four witnesses — four contradictory versions, no authoritative truth. Duke borrows the name for a law of everyday life: **every account of a disputed outcome that reaches you is one self-interested perspective**, pre-shaped by the teller's biases before it left their mouth.

The poker player narrating a lost hand cannot tell you the opponent's reasoning — only guess at it, in the direction that flatters. Your colleague's story of the collapsed deal, your friend's account of the argument with their spouse — you are not hearing what happened, friend. **You are hearing a closing argument.** The pod's job is to reconstruct the missing sides: *What would the other person say happened? What might they have known that you didn't? What would a neutral observer report?* This isn't calling the teller a liar — it's recognizing that no single vantage point contains the whole truth. And it comes with an anti-enabling rule: reflexively agreeing that your friend's boss is a jerk *feels* supportive, but in a group chartered for accuracy, comfort-first responses squander the exchange. Inside the charter, skeptical questions are a gift — everyone signed up for them.

The problem is everyone who *didn't* sign up. Which is most of the world.

## Crossing the border

Duke is blunt: truthseeking is an **opt-in game**. Outside the pod — spouses, bosses, clients, friends — unsolicited belief-auditing lands as attack, triggers defense, and changes no minds. Remember Letterman ambushing his guest with an accurate-but-uninvited diagnosis? The insight was fine. *The contract was missing.* So Duke offers four border-crossing moves for carrying truth into rooms that never signed CUDOS.

::: guess
The first two moves both work by lowering the temperature BEFORE the disagreement arrives. One involves how you open; the other involves a single conjunction. What are they?
---
**Express uncertainty first** — open with "I could be wrong about this…" or "I'm not sure, but…" It isn't false modesty; it's *accurate* (night one's honest position), and it reframes the exchange from verdict to collaboration: people who would fight a correction will happily help solve a puzzle. And **lead with assent — "and," never "but."** Affirm the parts of their account you genuinely agree with, then attach your divergence with *and*: "I agree the client was unreasonable, **and** I wonder whether the proposal gave them an opening." Because "but," friend, functions as a *delete key* for everything before it — listeners hear only "you're wrong." Same content, different container: one gets processed, the other repelled. Both moves exploit how belief-updating really works — beliefs are identity-loaded, and challenges trigger the motivated-reasoning lawyers. Uncertainty and assent keep the listener's identity out of the line of fire just long enough for the substance to land. You're not diluting the truth. **You're improving its odds of delivery.**
:::

And the last two moves: **ask permission before dissecting** — "do you want to vent, or do you want my honest take?" — and respect the answer; even poker pros don't critique a hand unless asked. And **point at the future, not the past** — people defend their past selves fiercely, but the future isn't part of anyone's identity yet. Not "you shouldn't have sent that email" but *"what's the best way to handle the follow-up?"* Not "why did you miss the deadline?" but *"what would need to change to hit the next one?"* The diagnosis gets smuggled in anyway — to plan a better future, they must implicitly audit the past — but voluntarily, without a courtroom.

## Key takeaways

- Accountability to an unknown, accuracy-minded audience induces exploratory thought; a known, friendly audience deepens confirmation.
- Dissent never volunteers — assign it: devil's advocates, red teams, the tenth-man doctrine — and rotate the role to decouple it from disloyalty.
- Removing the assigned dissenter breaks the machine: 500 canonizations in one papacy after the devil's advocate was abolished.
- Every story you hear is a closing argument — reconstruct the other side before reacting, and don't reflexively validate.
- Cross the border gently: uncertainty first, "and" never "but," ask permission before dissecting.
- Aim conversations at the future — it's the only thing a decision can still change, and nobody's identity lives there yet.
