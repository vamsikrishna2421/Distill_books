# The Dart-Throwing Chimp and the Illusion of Knowledge

A new fire tonight, friend — and a new guide with a mischievous experiment. Philip Tetlock is the scientist who spent *twenty years* collecting predictions from hundreds of experts — tens of thousands of forecasts about wars, elections, economies — and then did the one thing nobody in the prediction business ever does.

**He kept score.**

What the scoreboard revealed became one of the most famous — and most misunderstood — jokes in social science. And it begins with a chimpanzee holding darts.

## The most famous chimp in science

![A chimpanzee mid-throw with a dart, aiming at a dartboard covered in newspaper headlines, in a wood-paneled study](/story-art/superforecasting/01-chimp.jpg)

First, notice something strange about the world. We are *all* forecasters — every job taken, house bought, product launched rests on predictions of what happens next. Governments spend billions on it. And yet forecasting is nearly the only skilled activity on earth where **performance is never measured**. A pundit can be spectacularly wrong for decades and keep a full calendar of television bookings, because *nobody tallies the record*. Tetlock's example is Tom Friedman of the New York Times — enormously influential, read in the White House — and nobody on earth, *including Friedman*, has any rigorous idea how accurate his predictions have been. That's not an attack, friend. That's the point: even for the most famous forecaster alive, **the data simply does not exist.**

So Tetlock built the scoreboard: the Expert Political Judgment study — hundreds of experts, two decades, tens of thousands of verifiable predictions, all scored against reality.

::: guess
The result became a legend, usually quoted as: "the average expert did about as well as a dart-throwing chimpanzee." What did the chimp joke actually hide — the two findings everyone missed?
---
First: the "chimp" is an *average* that conceals the real discovery — **the experts split into two groups**. One did no better than chance and got *worse* the further out they predicted. The other **beat chance meaningfully** — and what separated them was not credentials, ideology, or secret intelligence. It was *how they thought* — a style difference the next chapter will name. Second: **difficulty varies enormously with the horizon.** Experts predicting a year out did decently; accuracy decayed toward chance at three to five years; beyond that, no mind on earth holds much edge — chaos theory guarantees it, since tiny differences in starting conditions explode into divergent futures. Saying "experts can't predict anything" because they fail at five-year geopolitics is like calling doctors useless because they can't predict your lifespan to the month. The chimp was never a verdict on foresight, friend. It was a verdict on **unmeasured** foresight — proof that fame and confidence are worthless proxies for accuracy... and that once you actually keep score, you can find who is good, and then ask *why*. That question launched everything that follows.
:::

## When doctors were confident and useless

Want to know what forecasting looks like today? Look at medicine — two centuries ago. For millennia, physicians were prestigious, supremely confident... and largely useless. Bloodletting killed patients while doctors credited every survival to their skill. Validation was *eminence*: the great doctor knew his treatments worked because *he had seen them work* — the illusion of knowledge in its purest form.

The fix wasn't smarter doctors, friend. It was a **method**: the randomized controlled trial — compare treated patients against untreated controls, and let outcomes, not authority, decide. And even that obvious idea met fury. Archie Cochrane, the Scottish physician who championed rigorous trials, fought the establishment his whole career — and won one battle with a trick worthy of a poker player.

::: guess
Cochrane ran a trial comparing hospital-based cardiac care against recovery at home. When preliminary data seemed to favor hospitals, indignant cardiologists demanded the "unethical" trial be stopped immediately. What did Cochrane then reveal?
---
**He had swapped the labels.** *Home care* was winning; the hospitals were losing. The cardiologists' certainty was running exactly backwards — they were demanding the trial stop to protect a treatment the data showed was worse. The room went quiet. Medicine only became genuinely effective when it institutionalized *doubt* — when "does it work?" became an empirical question settled by measurement rather than eminence. And forecasting circa today, Tetlock says, is **pre-Cochrane medicine**: confident, prestigious, unmeasured practitioners, judged by story quality and fame — the eminence heuristic all over again. The remedy is identical: *evidence-based forecasting.* State predictions precisely enough to be scored. Score them. Let results, not reputations, decide whom to trust. Your homework starts tonight, friend: a forecasting log — five predictions, each with a specific outcome, a deadline, and a number. Then keep score on yourself.
:::

## The tip of your nose

But why are we so *sure* of judgments that turn out wrong? The machinery, once seen, explains half the confident nonsense in the world.

Kahneman's fast **System 1** delivers instant answers — pattern-matched, effortless, unbidden — while slow **System 2** mostly ratifies whatever arrives. Tetlock's name for how this feels from inside is the **tip-of-your-nose perspective**: you see reality only from your own vantage point, and what you see *feels like all there is to see*. System 1 never announces "insufficient data" — it always delivers an answer, with a feeling of certainty attached, built from whatever fragments were lying around.

Its core trick is **attribute substitution**: faced with a hard question, System 1 silently answers an *easier* one and passes it off as the original. Ask a pundit *"will Iran comply with the deal?"* and he actually answers *"do I trust Iran?"* — without ever noticing the swap. The substitution is invisible from inside; the answer arrives feeling like insight. Then **confirmation bias** locks it in: evidence that fits is embraced, evidence that doesn't is discounted, and the first impression becomes a fortress. Instant answers plus one-sided evidence-gathering equals *confident conclusions that were never tested against anything*.

None of this damns intuition, friend — in domains with fast, clear feedback (firefighting, chess), trained System 1 is genuine expertise. The failure mode is trusting the tip of your nose where feedback is *slow, ambiguous, or absent* — like geopolitics, markets, careers. There, conviction and correctness come apart... and only deliberate checking, disciplined by scorekeeping, can tell them apart. So when you feel instantly certain this week, run the diagnostic: *what question was I asked — and what easier question did I just answer instead?*

## Before you go

So: everyone forecasts and almost no one keeps score; the chimp indicted unmeasured expertise, not expertise; medicine escaped its confident dark age through measurement, and forecasting hasn't yet; and the certainty in your chest is a feeling System 1 attaches to answers — not evidence that they're true.

But the scoreboard left a beautiful mystery, friend. Those experts who *beat* chance — who were they? What made them different? ## Key takeaways

- Everyone forecasts; almost nobody keeps score — and without a scoreboard, fame and confidence are the only (worthless) proxies.
- The dart-throwing chimp indicted unmeasured foresight: experts split by thinking style, and accuracy decays with the time horizon.
- Forecasting today is pre-Cochrane medicine: confident, prestigious, untested — the fix is evidence-based forecasting.
- The tip-of-your-nose perspective always supplies an answer plus certainty — whether or not it supplies truth.
- Catch attribute substitution: name the hard question asked and the easy question you actually answered.
- Start the log: five precise predictions with numbers and deadlines — and score yourself when they land.
