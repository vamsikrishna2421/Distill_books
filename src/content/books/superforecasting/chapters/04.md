# Granularity: Percentages, Not Fate

## Key Ideas
- Most people judge the future with a **three-setting mental dial** — "gonna happen," "not gonna happen," and "maybe" — while superforecasters think in fine-grained percentages like 63% or 12%.
- Tetlock's rounding experiment showed that superforecasters' precision is not false precision: rounding their forecasts to the nearest 10%, or collapsing them to three settings, measurably worsened their Brier scores, so the extra digits carry real information.
- The **wrong-side-of-maybe fallacy** — declaring a 70% forecast "wrong" because the 30% outcome occurred — is how most consumers judge probability, and it punishes honest forecasters while rewarding vague ones.
- The bin Laden raid deliberations, where President Obama reportedly compressed estimates ranging from roughly 30% to 95% into "fifty-fifty, a coin flip," show that even elite consumers of forecasts default to the crude dial.
- Distinguishing **epistemic uncertainty** (facts that exist but are unknown) from **aleatory uncertainty** (irreducible randomness) tells a forecaster when digging for information can sharpen a probability and when "maybe" is the honest ceiling.
- Belief in fate is measurably corrosive: the more strongly forecasters agreed that events are "meant to be," the worse their accuracy — probabilistic thinking and destiny thinking are opposites.

## The Three-Setting Dial in Our Heads

When people talk about the future, they rarely use numbers. They say something will happen, won't happen, or might happen. Amos Tversky quipped that most people run on exactly three settings: "gonna happen, not gonna happen, and maybe." That crude dial is our evolutionary default. For our ancestors, fast certainty — that shadow is a predator, run — beat slow nuance; the tip-of-your-nose System 1 delivers confident verdicts, not confidence intervals.

But a three-setting dial throws away enormous amounts of information. There is a world of difference between a 20% chance of war and a 45% chance, yet both collapse into "maybe." Superforecasters replace the dial with a percentage scale and use it with unusual **granularity**: they distinguish 60% from 65%, and 3% from 8%, where ordinary forecasters snap to round numbers like 25%, 50%, and 75%.

A natural objection is that this precision is theater — that no one can really tell 63% from 65% about, say, a Nigerian election. This is testable, and Tetlock tested it. His team took years of tournament forecasts and rounded them: first to the nearest 0.05, then to the nearest 0.10, then all the way down to the three-setting dial (yes, no, maybe). If the fine distinctions were noise, rounding would cost nothing.

It cost plenty. For average forecasters, rounding to the nearest tenth barely changed accuracy — their granularity really was decoration. For superforecasters, every round of coarsening degraded Brier scores, and collapsing them to three settings hurt badly. Their decimal places are signal. Granularity is not a symptom of overconfidence; among the best forecasters, it is a measurable component of skill.

One caveat the chapter is careful about: this is not because superforecasters are closet quants. Many are numerate — some highly so, like mathematician Lionel Levine — but almost none use explicit equations or statistical models to forecast. The numbers express carefully weighed judgment; they do not replace it. What matters is the habit of translating vague hunches into numbers that can be compared, updated, and scored.

## The Wrong-Side-of-Maybe Fallacy

Why do smart people resist probabilistic forecasts? Partly because of how forecasts get judged. Suppose a meteorologist says there is a 70% chance of rain tomorrow and the day is sunny. Most people conclude she was wrong. She wasn't — a 70% forecast explicitly reserves a 30% chance of no rain. Judging a single probabilistic forecast by whether the outcome landed on the nearer side of 50% is the **wrong-side-of-maybe fallacy**.

The only fair test is the one from the scorekeeping chapter: gather many forecasts and check calibration — does rain actually fall on about 70% of the days she says 70%? For weather forecasters, who get fast, unambiguous feedback daily, the answer is yes; they are among the best-calibrated professionals anywhere. But for one-off geopolitical calls, the public, the press, and politicians apply the fallacy relentlessly.

That creates perverse incentives. A forecaster who says "there is a serious possibility" can never be caught on the wrong side of maybe, because "serious possibility" has no side. Vagueness is professionally safe and informationally worthless. A forecaster who commits to 70% is giving you more, and risking ridicule for it. Institutions that punish honest probabilities get elastic language instead — and then can't tell their good forecasters from their bad ones.

The fallacy also feeds a deeper objection: that probabilities of unique events are meaningless because history runs only once — we never observe the 30% of worlds where it rained. Tetlock's answer is practical, not philosophical: treat probability as a degree of belief, score it across many forecasts, and the meaningless-number objection dissolves. Forecasters who use the full percentage scale demonstrably outperform those who don't, which is all the meaning a decision-maker needs.

## Fifty-Fifty: When Consumers Flatten the Numbers

Forecasting fails at two ends — production and consumption — and the bin Laden decision shows the consumption end at the highest possible stakes. In 2011, President Obama's intelligence advisers gave varying probabilities that the tall man pacing the Abbottabad compound was Osama bin Laden. Estimates ran from roughly 30% up to 95%, with the CIA team leader near the top. After hearing the range, Obama — by journalist Mark Bowden's account — concluded that the situation was fifty-fifty, a coin flip.

Read literally, that is the three-setting dial swallowing hard-won analysis: a cluster of estimates averaging well above 50% got flattened into "maybe." If the president genuinely treated 30-to-95 as 50-50, he discarded exactly the information his analysts were paid to produce. Tetlock offers a more charitable reading — Obama may have meant "the debate won't settle this; residual uncertainty remains, so decide anyway" — and the decision itself worked out. But the ambiguity is the lesson: even at the apex of government, probability estimates get consumed by minds that default to yes/no/maybe.

The fix has to work on both sides of the table. Producers of forecasts must commit to granular numbers that can be scored. Consumers must learn that 70% is not "yes," that 30% is not "no," and that a 75% estimate which goes the other way may still have been the best possible call. An organization whose leaders round everything to the nearest coin flip will get coin-flip-quality analysis, because nothing finer survives the meeting.

## Two Kinds of Uncertainty

Not all "maybes" are alike, and superforecasters keep two species distinct. **Epistemic uncertainty** concerns facts that exist but are unknown: whether bin Laden was in the compound was a definite yes or no in reality; the uncertainty lived only in the analysts' heads, and more intelligence could shrink it. **Aleatory uncertainty** is irreducible randomness: the weather three weeks out isn't a hidden fact waiting to be found — the system itself is chaotic, and no amount of digging can pin it down.

The distinction is operational. Facing epistemic uncertainty, the right move is to work the problem — Fermi-ize it, hunt for diagnostic evidence, and let the probability march away from 50% as knowledge accumulates. Facing aleatory uncertainty, the skilled response is humility: keep the forecast near maybe, because pretending to precision about genuine randomness is its own error. Superforecasters are granular where granularity is earned and deliberately vague-in-numbers where the world is dice.

Knowing which kind of uncertainty you face also sets expectations for improvement. Epistemic questions reward effort and updating; aleatory questions punish overconfident swings in either direction. Mistaking one for the other produces the two classic failures — lazy fifty-fifties on knowable questions, and false precision on unknowable ones.

## Nothing Is Meant to Be

The deepest obstacle to probabilistic thinking isn't innumeracy — it's fate. Ask people about pivotal life events, such as meeting their spouse, and many say it was "meant to be." That framing feels meaningful, but it is the opposite of probability: it says the outcome was certain all along, so there was never a distribution of possibilities at all. The probabilistic thinker's version is colder and truer — the meeting was wildly improbable, but wildly improbable events happen constantly, and *something* improbable was bound to happen. Your own existence sits atop an absurd chain of contingencies; that makes it lucky, not destined.

Tetlock turned this worldview into a measurement. Forecasters completed a fate-and-destiny questionnaire, rating agreement with statements like "everything happens for a reason" and "events unfold according to a plan." Superforecasters rejected such statements far more strongly than regular forecasters, who in turn rejected them more than the general public. And within the tournament the correlation was direct: the higher a forecaster's fate score, the worse their accuracy. Belief in destiny wasn't a harmless private comfort — it predicted bad Brier scores.

The mechanism is straightforward. If events are fated, probability is beside the point, evidence-weighing is theater, and hindsight always confirms that what happened had to happen. If events are contingent, then percentages are the only honest language for the future, and every forecast is a claim that can be sharpened. Granularity, in the end, is a worldview: the world is not scripted, "maybe" comes in a hundred shades, and the forecaster's job is to say which shade — then be scored on it.

## In Practice

- Ban "will," "won't," and "maybe" from your forecasts this week; state every prediction as a specific percentage, even when it feels uncomfortably precise.
- When you catch yourself at 50%, ask whether that number reflects analysis or a dodged question — then do an hour of digging and see if the estimate moves.
- Before forecasting, label the uncertainty: if it's epistemic (a knowable fact), invest in research; if it's aleatory (true randomness), stay near maybe and resist false precision.
- Never grade a single probabilistic call as right or wrong by its outcome; log your probability estimates and judge calibration across many of them instead.
- When someone hands you a probability, consume it faithfully: treat 70% as 70%, not as "yes," and don't average a range of expert estimates down to a coin flip.
- Audit your language for fate-talk — "it was meant to be," "everything happens for a reason" — and restate those events as improbable outcomes that happened anyway.
