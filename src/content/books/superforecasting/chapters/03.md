# How Superforecasters Think: Fermi-ize and Zoom Out

## Key Ideas
- Superforecasters attack seemingly impossible questions by **Fermi-izing** them — decomposing one unanswerable question into several smaller ones that can be estimated or researched separately.
- They start with the **outside view** (the base rate for the general class of situations) before the **inside view** (the specifics of this case), because the outside view supplies a meaningful anchor that the inside view can then adjust.
- **Anchoring** makes first numbers sticky; starting from an arbitrary or vivid detail biases the whole forecast, while starting from a base rate biases it toward reality.
- The best forecasters cultivate a **dragonfly eye**: they actively hunt for perspectives that contradict their own and synthesize them into a single judgment, effectively becoming a one-person wisdom-of-crowds.
- Galton's ox experiment shows why aggregation works — individual judgments mix valid information with error, and combining many judgments keeps the information while the errors cancel.
- What separates superforecasters is not raw intelligence but **active open-mindedness**: treating beliefs as hypotheses to be tested rather than treasures to be defended.

## Fermi-izing: Break the Impossible Question into Estimable Pieces

Physicist Enrico Fermi was famous for making startlingly good estimates of quantities nobody could look up — like "How many piano tuners are there in Chicago?" His method was not intuition but decomposition. Break the unknowable question into smaller questions, each of which you can estimate, however roughly, and separate what you know from what you merely assume.

For the piano tuners: How many people live in Chicago (about 2.5 million at the time)? How many people per household, and what fraction of households own a piano that gets tuned regularly (guess around one in a hundred, plus institutional pianos)? How often is a piano tuned (roughly once a year)? How long does a tuning take, and how many tunings can one tuner do in a working year (perhaps a thousand)? Multiply and divide, and you land in the low sixties — remarkably close to the roughly eighty tuners actually listed in the Chicago phone book at one point.

The power of the technique is twofold. First, each sub-estimate can be wrong in either direction, so errors tend to partially cancel rather than compound. Second, and more important, decomposition **flushes hidden assumptions into the open** where they can be examined, researched, and corrected. The alternative — answering from the gut — is what Tetlock calls the tip-of-your-nose perspective (see Chapter 1): it feels like insight but is really just whatever your System 1 happened to serve up.

Superforecasters do this reflexively. Where an ordinary forecaster sees one giant question and freezes or guesses, a superforecaster sees a bundle of tractable sub-questions, some answerable with data, others requiring only rough bounds. Even crude bounds on each piece beat a single crude guess at the whole.

## The Arafat Question: Answer the Question That Was Actually Asked

In 2012, IARPA tournament forecasters faced a bizarre question: would Swiss or French scientists find elevated levels of polonium in the exhumed remains of Yasser Arafat? Most people instinctively substituted an easier, more emotional question — "Did Israel poison Arafat?" — and answered that instead. This is **attribute substitution** in action, and it leads forecasts astray, because the tournament question and the substituted question have different answers.

Superforecaster Bill Flack Fermi-ized it. First sub-question: can polonium even be detected in a body a decade after death? Polonium decays fast, so this required actual research — which showed detection remained possible. Second: would the testing proceed at all? Third — and this is the subtle part — how many distinct pathways lead to a "yes"? Israel could have poisoned him; someone else could have; or polonium could have been placed on the remains after death to frame Israel. Because multiple independent routes led to elevated polonium, the probability of "yes" was higher than any single poisoning theory alone would suggest. Flack forecast about 60% yes — and the Swiss team indeed reported elevated polonium.

The lesson: rigorously **unpack the question as posed**, not the question your emotions want to answer. List every path to each outcome, including paths that have nothing to do with your pet theory. A question about test results is not a question about guilt; a question about a leader leaving office is not a question about whether you like him. Precision about what is being asked is the unglamorous first step that most forecasters skip.

## Outside View First: Base Rates Before Details, Because Anchors Stick

Consider the Renzettis: a small family in a modest house, Frank a bookkeeper, Mary a part-time daycare worker, one child, saving for college. What's the chance they have a pet? Most people dive into the details — the tight budget, the small house. Superforecasters first ask a different question: what percentage of American households own a pet? That figure — around 62% — is the **base rate**, and starting there is what Daniel Kahneman calls taking the **outside view**: treating the case as an instance of a broader class before treating it as unique. Only then do they switch to the **inside view**, adjusting up or down for the Renzettis' particulars.

The order matters because of **anchoring**: the first number you generate exerts a gravitational pull on every subsequent adjustment. Kahneman and Amos Tversky showed that even obviously irrelevant numbers — a rigged wheel of fortune landing on 10 or 65 — dragged people's estimates of the percentage of African nations in the UN toward the arbitrary number. Since you will anchor on something, anchor on the most informative number available. Start with the inside view and you anchor on a vivid detail; start with the outside view and you anchor on reality's average.

Applied to geopolitics, "Will there be a lethal Sino-Japanese clash in the East China Sea this year?" should begin not with today's headlines but with history: how often have comparable standoffs between these countries turned lethal in a given year? That base rate becomes the anchor; current tensions, leaders, and deployments then adjust it. The outside view rarely gives the final answer — but it prevents the final answer from floating free of all historical experience. Skipping it is the single most common amateur error.

## The Dragonfly Eye: Aggregate Perspectives Like Galton's Crowd

In 1906, Francis Galton watched nearly 800 fairgoers at a Plymouth livestock fair guess the weight of an ox. The crowd's collective judgment came out at 1,197 pounds; the ox weighed 1,198. No individual did as well. The logic of this **wisdom of crowds**: every judgment blends genuine information with error, and when many independent judgments are combined, the errors — scattered in all directions — largely cancel, while the shared information accumulates.

Superforecasters internalize this logic. A dragonfly's compound eye contains thousands of lenses, each capturing a different angle, which the brain fuses into a single image. Likewise, superforecasters deliberately gather perspectives that clash with their own — other analysts' arguments, hostile commentators, alternative framings of the same data — and synthesize them, rather than seeking one congenial viewpoint and stopping. The synthesis of contradictory views is itself a perspective, and typically a better one than any input.

Remarkably, you can even crowd-source within your own skull. Psychologists Edward Vul and Harold Pashler found that averaging two estimates from the same person — the second made after a delay, or after being told to assume the first was wrong — beats either estimate alone: the **crowd within**. Techniques that force a second look, like asking "suppose my initial judgment is mistaken — why?", or flipping a question ("What's the chance this *doesn't* happen?"), reliably surface information the first pass missed.

The full superforecaster sequence, then: unpack the question into components; separate the knowable from the unknowable; take the outside view and anchor on the base rate; adopt the inside view and adjust for case specifics; seek out other views, especially contrary ones; and merge everything into a single, precisely calibrated probability.

## Open Minds Beat High IQs

Are superforecasters simply geniuses? The data say no. Tested on intelligence measures, regular forecasters in the Good Judgment Project scored around the 70th percentile of the population, and superforecasters around the 80th — clearly above average, but far from Einstein territory, and plenty of higher-IQ participants forecast worse. Beyond a decent threshold, additional raw brainpower buys little. What you have matters less than how you use it.

The trait that does discriminate is what psychologist Jonathan Baron calls **active open-mindedness (AOM)**: the disposition to treat beliefs as testable hypotheses rather than possessions to protect, to actively search for evidence against your own views, and to regard changing your mind as a win rather than a defeat. People high in AOM agree that contradictory evidence should be sought out and that attending to those who disagree makes you smarter. Superforecasters score distinctly high on it.

This reframes forecasting skill as a stance, not a gift. The dragonfly eye is AOM in action: someone protecting a belief filters out contrary lenses, while someone testing a hypothesis welcomes them. It also explains the fox-over-hedgehog result from Chapter 2 at the level of individual cognition — hedgehogs deploy their intelligence as lawyers defending One Big Idea; foxes deploy theirs as investigators integrating many small ones.

The encouraging implication: this style is learnable. Fermi-izing, base-rate anchoring, and perspective-hunting are procedures, not talents — deliberate System 2 checks on System 1's seductive first impressions. ## In Practice
- Take one hard question you face this week and Fermi-ize it: write down four or five sub-questions whose answers, multiplied together, yield an estimate — then research or bound each piece separately.
- Before analyzing any specific case, force yourself to ask: "How often does this kind of thing happen in situations of this kind?" Write the base rate down first and treat it as your anchor.
- Re-read the exact wording of whatever question you're answering and list every distinct pathway to each outcome — including pathways that contradict your favored story (the polonium-planted scenario, not just the poisoning one).
- After forming a judgment, assume it is wrong and spend five minutes articulating the strongest reason why; then average your revised view with your original to exploit the crowd within.
- Seek one source this week that you expect to disagree with your position, and extract at least one factor it sees that you missed — then adjust your estimate accordingly.
- Audit your reaction the next time evidence cuts against a belief you hold: if you feel defensive, label the belief a hypothesis under test and ask what evidence would change your mind.
