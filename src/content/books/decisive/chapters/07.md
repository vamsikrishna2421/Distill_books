# Prepare to Be Wrong: Bookend the Future

## Key Ideas
- We are systematically overconfident about the future: in autopsy studies, physicians who felt "completely certain" of a diagnosis were wrong about 40% of the time, and expertise makes predictions no more reliable — only more confident.
- Because the future is a range and not a point, good decision-makers **bookend** it: they prepare for both a bad outcome (lower bookend) and a great one (upper bookend) instead of betting on a single forecast.
- Gary Klein's **premortem** — "assume we failed; why?" — exploits **prospective hindsight**, which Mitchell, Russo, and Pennington showed boosts people's ability to generate correct reasons for outcomes by about 30%.
- A **preparade** stress-tests success: Softsoap's Robert Taylor bought his industry's entire pump supply because he asked, in advance, what runaway success would require and reveal.
- Since you can't foresee every specific problem, build **safety factors** into plans the way engineers do — elevator cables are built roughly eleven times stronger than needed, and veteran software managers pad every schedule estimate.
- For the problems you can foresee, use **realistic job previews**: John Wanous's research shows that honestly disclosing a job's downsides "vaccinates" people against disappointment and reduces turnover.

## Certainty Is a Feeling, Not a Forecast

The fourth villain of decision making is overconfidence about how the future will unfold. The most sobering evidence comes from medicine: when researchers compared physicians' diagnoses of ICU patients against the ground truth later revealed by autopsy, doctors who had declared themselves "completely certain" of their diagnosis were wrong roughly 40% of the time. If confidence tracked accuracy that poorly among highly trained experts staring at rich clinical data, our confidence about product launches, hires, and career moves deserves deep suspicion.

The problem isn't that we know nothing about the future — it's that we treat the future as a single point when it's actually a **range** of possible outcomes. We spotlight one scenario, usually the one we expect or want, and plan as though it were guaranteed. The fix is not to predict better. Decades of research suggest experts are dreadful at point predictions, though they're quite good at supplying base rates and boundaries (a strength covered in chapter 5). The fix is to stop needing the prediction to be right.

That means **bookending the future**: sketching a distinctly bad outcome on one end and a distinctly great outcome on the other, then preparing for both. The Heaths illustrate the mindset with investor Byron Penstock, who didn't ask "what will this stock be worth?" but instead estimated a plausible floor and ceiling, and only invested when the price sat near the floor — so a wrong guess cost little and a right one paid handsomely.

Bookending flips the emotional logic of planning. Instead of defending a forecast, you interrogate it from both sides: What if this goes much worse than we think? What if it goes much better? Each bookend has its own tool — the premortem for failure, the preparade for success — and each surfaces preparations that a single-point plan would never include. The goal isn't pessimism or optimism. It's readiness across the whole range.

## The Premortem: Assume Failure, Then Explain It

Psychologist Gary Klein developed the **premortem** as a corporate counterpart to the medical postmortem — except the patient hasn't died yet. Before committing to a plan, the team leader announces: it's one year later, and our project has failed spectacularly. Everyone then spends a few minutes independently writing down every plausible reason for the failure.

The tense matters. Asking "what might go wrong?" invites polite, vague hedging; declaring that the failure has already happened licenses people to be specific and candid. This works because of a documented quirk called **prospective hindsight**. In a 1989 study, Deborah Mitchell, Jay Russo, and Nancy Pennington found that people who imagined an event as if it had already occurred — explaining the past rather than forecasting the future — generated about 30% more correct reasons for the outcome than people merely predicting it. Certainty about the past, even a fictional past, unlocks concrete causal thinking that speculation about the future does not.

A premortem delivers three benefits. First, it surfaces threats while there's still time to defuse them cheaply — the team can strengthen the plan against the most damaging or most likely failure modes before launch. Second, it legitimizes dissent: the skeptic who would have been branded disloyal for "negativity" is now just playing the assigned game, which complements the engineered-disagreement techniques from chapter 4. Third, it calibrates commitment — if the imagined failure reasons look overwhelming and unfixable, that's a signal to rethink the decision itself, not just the execution.

The premortem is deliberately cheap: minutes of writing, one meeting. It doesn't require abandoning the plan or hiring consultants. It only requires admitting, before reality forces the admission, that the confident version of the future is one scenario among several — and that the failure scenario deserves as much detailed imagination as the success story on the pitch deck.

## The Preparade: What If We Succeed Wildly?

The upper bookend gets its own exercise, which the Heaths call a **preparade**: assume it's a year from now, the decision was a runaway triumph, and there's a parade in your honor. Now ask — are we actually ready for that? Success can wreck the unprepared as surely as failure: demand outstrips supply, servers crash under traffic, a small team drowns in customers, competitors swarm in.

The classic case is Robert Taylor of Minnetonka Corporation, who in the late 1970s invented **Softsoap**, liquid soap in a pump bottle. Taylor knew his tiny company held no meaningful patent — the idea was trivially copyable by giants like Procter & Gamble the moment it proved popular. His preparade-style question was: if this takes off, what will everyone need? The answer was the plastic pump, and only two manufacturers in America could make them. Taylor bet roughly $12 million — nearly the company's entire net worth — to order 100 million pumps, locking up the nation's supply for an estimated 18 to 24 months. When Softsoap boomed, the big players couldn't get pumps to copy it at scale, and the head start let Softsoap entrench itself as a brand worth hundreds of millions.

Taylor didn't predict success; he prepared for it. The preparade converts vague optimism into operational questions: How will we hire fast enough? Can suppliers scale with us? What will competitors do when they notice? Which bottleneck breaks first at 10x demand?

Run both exercises together and you've bookended the decision. The premortem hardens the plan against the bad tail; the preparade positions you to exploit the good tail. Neither requires knowing which future will arrive — which is precisely the point, because you don't.

## Safety Factors: Margin for the Problems You Can't Name

Premortems catch foreseeable failures, but some problems can't be anticipated specifically. For those, borrow the engineer's habit of the **safety factor** — deliberate excess capacity that absorbs unknown unknowns. Elevator cables are a vivid example: they're built to hold roughly eleven times the maximum rated load. Engineers don't know exactly which stress, flaw, or freak event the margin will someday absorb; they simply know that reality reliably outruns calculation, so they buy insurance in steel.

The same logic runs through construction codes (structures over-engineered against loads they'll likely never see) and software development, where seasoned project managers routinely add a substantial buffer to engineers' time estimates. The engineers aren't lying — they're estimating the version of the project where nothing goes wrong, a version that has never once shipped. The buffer isn't padding for laziness; it's a scheduled acknowledgment that integration snags, sick days, and surprise requirements always appear, even though no one can say which ones.

The transferable principle: whenever a plan's numbers feel precise, treat that precision as the error. Assume your budget estimate, timeline, and sales forecast are each optimistic, and build slack accordingly — extra weeks in the schedule, reserve cash in the budget, spare capacity in the team. Notice that a safety factor is different from a premortem fix. A premortem says "the vendor might miss the deadline, so let's line up a backup vendor." A safety factor says "something we haven't thought of will bite us, so let's leave a month unallocated." Robust plans use both: targeted defenses against named risks, plus generic margin against unnamed ones.

Safety factors feel wasteful right up until they're used — which, history suggests, is nearly always. Eleven-fold cable strength looks paranoid until you remember how rarely elevators kill anyone.

## Realistic Previews: Vaccinate Against Negative Surprise

The final tool handles a subtler future problem: not catastrophe, but the slow corrosion of predictable disappointment. Every job, purchase, and commitment has known downsides, and hiding them just schedules a nasty surprise. The antidote is the **realistic job preview (RJP)**, studied extensively by John Wanous: before a candidate accepts a job, the employer candidly describes its worst features — the angry callers a customer-service rep will face, the grinding travel, the tedium.

Across Wanous's meta-analytic research on many jobs and thousands of workers, realistic previews reliably reduced turnover and improved satisfaction once people were on the job. The surprising part is the mechanism. It's mostly not self-selection — relatively few candidates hear the ugly truth and walk away. Instead the preview works as **vaccination**: a small, controlled dose of the negative reality, administered in advance, lets people pre-process the pain, rehearse coping, and interpret the eventual unpleasantness as normal rather than as evidence they made a mistake. Honesty also breeds trust — people cut an employer slack when they feel they were told the truth up front.

The vaccination logic generalizes far beyond hiring. Warn a new customer about your product's learning curve and setup friction. Tell a family moving cities about the lonely first six months. Brief a first-time manager on how bruising performance conversations feel. In each case, the future negative event is close to certain; the only choice is whether people meet it inoculated or blindsided.

RJPs complete the bookending toolkit. The premortem and safety factors guard against the bad futures you might get; the preparade readies you for the great one; realistic previews prepare you — and the people affected by your decisions — for the ordinary disappointments guaranteed to come. None of it requires forecasting skill. As the Heaths argue, the point of preparing to be wrong is that you will be: humility about prediction, paired with concrete preparation, beats confident planning across almost any future that actually shows up.

## In Practice

- Before your next significant commitment, run a ten-minute premortem: announce "it's one year later and this failed completely," have everyone independently write the reasons, then revise the plan against the top threats.
- Pair it with a preparade: assume spectacular success and list what would break first — supply, staffing, systems, or competitor response — then secure the bottleneck now, as Taylor did with pumps.
- Bookend any estimate you're given: write down a realistic worst case and best case, and check that your plan survives the low end and can exploit the high end.
- Add explicit safety factors to your current project: pad the timeline and budget beyond what feels necessary, and keep the buffer unallocated for problems you can't yet name.
- Give a realistic preview before someone commits to you — a hire, a client, a collaborator: state the two or three worst features of the deal plainly and let the vaccination effect work.
- When you feel "completely certain" about a prediction this week, recall the 40% physician error rate and ask what you'd do differently if you knew you might be in the wrong 40%.
