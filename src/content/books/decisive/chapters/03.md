# Multitrack — and Find Someone Who's Solved Your Problem

## Key Ideas
- **Multitracking** — developing two or more real options simultaneously — produces better results, faster decisions, and less ego-bruising than working options one at a time.
- Fear of "too many options" is mostly misplaced: decision paralysis kicks in at dozens of choices, not at two or three, and options must be genuine, not sham alternatives staged to flatter a favorite.
- The strongest option sets blend a **promotion focus** (chasing gains) with a **prevention focus** (avoiding losses); firms that did both during recessions were the most likely to emerge as leaders.
- When you're stuck, someone has almost certainly solved your problem already — look for internal **bright spots**, study competitors, and borrow shamelessly.
- **Laddering up** through increasingly distant analogies, and keeping **playlists** of proven approaches for recurring decisions, turns other people's solutions into your options.

## Two Ads at Once Beat One at a Time

Steven Dow, then a Stanford researcher, gave designers a real task: create web banner ads for a magazine. One group worked serially — design an ad, get feedback, revise, repeat. The other worked in parallel, producing multiple ads at once and getting feedback on all of them before converging. Both groups spent the same total time.

The parallel group won on every measure. Their final ads earned higher click-through rates in a live online campaign, were rated better by independent experts, and were more creatively diverse. Comparing options taught the parallel designers the "landscape" of the problem — what makes an ad work — while serial designers just polished a single premise.

There was a psychological bonus. Serial designers, invested in one design, took criticism personally and improved less between rounds. Parallel designers heard feedback as information about the designs, not verdicts on themselves. Multitracking depersonalizes critique: when you have only one option, your ego is fused to it.

Speed doesn't suffer — it improves. Kathleen Eisenhardt studied top-management teams at Silicon Valley firms, where slow decisions die, and found that executives who weighed several alternatives simultaneously actually decided *faster* than those grinding through options one by one. Three reasons: comparison builds a quicker, deeper understanding of the situation; having alternatives keeps leaders from escalating commitment to a failing favorite; and a fallback already exists if the lead option collapses, so setbacks don't trigger a panicked restart.

The practical bar is low. You don't need ten options — you need at least one more real one. Ask "What else could we do?" or develop a second plan in parallel, even a cheap sketch of one. The habit directly attacks the narrow "whether or not" frame from the previous chapter: instead of debating one option's fate, you choose among genuine alternatives.

## Real Options Only — and Don't Fear Paralysis

Multitracking works only with authentic options. A **sham option** exists to make the preferred choice look good — the real-estate agent who shows you two dumps before "the one," or, as Henry Kissinger recalled of policy memos, alternatives framed as nuclear war, the current policy, or surrender. If everyone in the room can predict the winner, you haven't multitracked; you've decorated a foregone conclusion. One test: would you be genuinely disappointed if a given option were forbidden? If not, it's a sham.

The obvious objection is choice overload. Sheena Iyengar and Mark Lepper's famous jam study found that a grocery display of 24 jams drew crowds but only 3% bought, while a 6-jam display converted 30% — more options froze shoppers. **Decision paralysis** is real, but it's a disease of two dozen options, not two. The Heaths' prescription is modest: move from one option to two or three. That's enough to gain comparison, ego protection, and a fallback, while staying far below the paralysis threshold.

Beware, too, of multitracking as theater inside organizations — teams forced to "generate three options" who quietly gut two of them. The fix is to make each option's champion argue it seriously, or to keep developing the top two until evidence, not attachment, separates them. Real options compete; sham options perform.

## Toggle Between Promotion and Prevention

Options also have a mindset. Psychologists distinguish a **promotion focus** — playing to win, pursuing gains and positive outcomes — from a **prevention focus** — playing not to lose, guarding against harm. Under stress, most people and organizations snap into pure prevention, and their option set collapses into variations of "hunker down."

Ranjay Gulati and colleagues studied roughly 4,700 public companies across three global recessions. Most firms limped out; only about 9% truly flourished afterward. The flourishers weren't the pure cost-cutters (prevention) or the pure aggressive investors (promotion). They were the firms that combined both: cutting operational fat *selectively* while simultaneously investing in R&D, marketing, and assets that positioned them for the recovery. That blended group had a 37% chance of breaking away as post-recession leaders — far better odds than firms locked in either single mindset.

The lesson generalizes to personal decisions. Someone laid off tends to ask only prevention questions: How do I pay the bills? How do I find a similar job fast? A promotion lens adds different options: Is this the moment to switch fields, start the business, move cities? Neither lens is right alone — the goal is an option set containing at least one "protect what we have" play and one "pursue the opportunity" play.

This is multitracking in a second dimension. And when options seem to conflict, ask whether you can do **both** — "this AND that" rather than "this OR that." Gulati's winners didn't choose between defense and offense; they ran them in parallel.

## Someone Has Already Solved Your Problem

When you can't generate good options, stop inventing and start hunting: someone, somewhere, has faced your problem and beaten it. Search in three places.

**Inside: find the bright spots.** Bright spots are pockets of your own organization or life where the problem is already being solved. Kaiser Permanente's Alan Whippy dug into hospital mortality data and found that sepsis — infection-triggered systemic inflammation — was implicated in roughly one in four hospital deaths, yet got far less attention than heart attacks because it kills quietly and gradually. Rather than inventing a response from scratch, Kaiser spread practices already proven by pioneers (including protocols championed at its own Santa Clara facility and by researchers elsewhere): routinely screening at-risk patients, measuring lactate levels, and treating early warning signs with the urgency of a code alert. Rolled out across its 21 Northern California hospitals, the program cut sepsis mortality dramatically — well below the national average within a few years. If you've quit smoking for three days before, or one region's sales team is thriving, interrogate the bright spot: what happened there, and how do we do more of it?

**Outside: study your competitors.** In the 1950s, Sam Walton rode a bus 600 miles to visit Ben Franklin variety stores in Minnesota because he'd heard they used centralized checkout — one front-of-store line instead of registers at every counter. He saw it worked, copied it, and made it a Walmart standard. Walton was candid about the method: "most everything I've done I've copied from somebody else." Competitive scouting isn't cheating; it's option generation at someone else's R&D expense.

Best practices are simply this idea institutionalized — but they only help if you actively go looking before you decide.

## Ladder Up with Analogies — and Build Playlists

When nobody has solved your *exact* problem, someone has solved a version of it. Kevin Dunbar, who embedded in microbiology labs to watch scientists work, found that analogy was their workhorse: in lab meetings, researchers reasoned through experimental roadblocks by comparing them to similar past experiments and related organisms, reaching for more distant analogies as problems got more conceptual. Breakthroughs came less from lone eureka moments than from systematically asking, "What is this problem *like*?"

Lexicon Branding — the firm that named the BlackBerry, Pentium, Swiffer, and Dasani — turns this into method. Lexicon deliberately avoids staring at the product itself, because that yields literal, generic names. For the device that became the BlackBerry, the insight was that PDAs make people feel stressed and rushed, so the team hunted for things that slow people down and evoke pleasure — which led to berries, and to "blackberry," whose tiny keyboard keys even resemble the fruit's drupelets. Lexicon routinely assigns **excursion teams** to work an analogy from a distant domain, alongside teams on the direct brief.

The Heaths call the general move **laddering up**. The bottom rung is your exact situation; each rung up is a more abstract version of the problem, letting you search wider domains. Low rungs (a rival hospital's sepsis protocol) yield options that are highly usable but rarely novel. High rungs (what can a stressed-out phone user learn from picking berries?) yield options that are riskier to translate but far more original. Climb only as high as you need to find a workable model.

Finally, for **recurring decisions**, don't regenerate options every time — keep a **playlist**: a pre-assembled menu of proven approaches to consult before deciding. Unlike a checklist, which prescribes required behavior, a playlist *suggests* possibilities — e.g., a cost-cutting playlist listing a dozen levers beyond layoffs, or a marketing playlist of campaign types to consider for every launch. Playlists bottle past option-generation so future decisions start wide by default.

## In Practice
- Before committing to any significant plan, force at least one real alternative onto the table and develop both in parallel — even a rough sketch of option B changes how you judge option A.
- Test each option for authenticity: if you wouldn't feel a pang were it removed, it's a sham option — replace it.
- Audit your option set's mindset: add one promotion-focused option (what could we gain?) and one prevention-focused option (what could we protect?) to every important decision.
- When stuck, ask "Who has already solved this?" — search internal bright spots first, then competitors and best practices, before inventing anything.
- Ladder up: restate your problem one level more abstractly and look for solutions in distant domains that share that structure.
- For decisions you make repeatedly, write a playlist of options that have worked before, and review it at the start of each new instance.
