# Set Tripwires — and Trust the Process

## Key Ideas
- Most bad decisions aren't made — they're drifted into. **Tripwires** are pre-set signals ("if X happens, we stop and decide") that snap you out of autopilot before gradual change becomes catastrophe.
- Tripwires can take the form of deadlines, budget **partitions**, or metric thresholds; each converts an open-ended drift into a forced, conscious decision point.
- Tripwires also make bold action safer: knowing the downside is capped by a pre-committed exit condition, you can pursue risk wholeheartedly instead of hedging.
- Pattern-based tripwires — like hospital rapid-response triggers — authorize people low in the hierarchy to escalate the moment a recognizable danger pattern appears.
- Research on **procedural justice** shows people accept even unfavorable decisions when the process was demonstrably fair: they were heard, options were genuinely weighed, and the reasoning was explained.
- Decisiveness comes from process, not certainty. WRAP — Widen options, Reality-test assumptions, Attain distance, Prepare to be wrong — doesn't guarantee right answers; it guarantees you made the decision the right way, which is the best anyone can do.

## Autopilot, Not Bad Judgment, Killed Kodak
Kodak did not lose to digital photography in one bad meeting. Its own engineer, Steve Sasson, built the first digital camera prototype in 1975, and in 1981 researcher Vince Barabba delivered an internal study concluding digital would seriously threaten film — but not for about a decade. Kodak had a ten-year head start and treated it as ten years of permission to relax.

That is the signature failure mode of gradual change: no single day ever feels like the day to act. Film revenue was still enormous, quarterly results still looked fine, and each year the company recommitted to the status quo without ever explicitly deciding to. By the time reality was undeniable, the stock had collapsed from roughly $94 in 1997 to under a dollar in 2011, and Kodak filed for bankruptcy in 2012.

The Heaths' diagnosis: Kodak lacked tripwires. A tripwire is a trigger defined in advance — "if film sales fall 10% in any year, we convene and fundamentally reassess strategy" — that forces a real decision at a specified moment. It works precisely because it's set while you're still clear-eyed, before rationalization and sunk costs accumulate. Like the low-fuel light in a car, it doesn't make the decision for you; it guarantees you notice that a decision is needed.

Tripwires matter because the future arrives on a gradient. Frogs-in-boiling-water metaphors aside, humans genuinely struggle to react to slow trends — there's never a jolt. The fix is to manufacture the jolt yourself: pick the signal now, commit to waking up when it fires, and you've protected your future self from the comfortable blindness of autopilot.

## Brown M&Ms, Pay-to-Quit, and the Power of Deadlines
Van Halen's touring contract famously demanded a bowl of M&Ms backstage with every brown one removed. It wasn't rock-star petulance — it was quality control. The band hauled nine trucks of complex staging into arenas that had never seen anything like it, and the rider buried safety-critical technical specs. David Lee Roth used the candy as an instant test: brown M&Ms in the bowl meant the promoter hadn't read the contract carefully, so the band would line-check the entire production. The **brown-M&Ms clause** is a tripwire for hidden negligence — a cheap, visible signal that flags an expensive, invisible problem.

Zappos uses a tripwire against a different kind of autopilot: employees drifting into jobs they don't want. After initial training, the company makes new hires "The Offer" — a cash payment (it started at $100 and grew into the thousands) to quit on the spot. Staying stops being the default and becomes an active choice; the small minority who take the money were never going to thrive there, and everyone who declines has consciously recommitted. The tripwire forces a decision that inertia would otherwise smother.

Deadlines are the most familiar tripwire, and they demonstrably work. In a study by Amos Tversky and Eldar Shafir, students offered $5 to complete a survey returned it 66% of the time when given a five-day deadline — versus 25% with no deadline at all. Nothing about the task changed; the deadline simply created a moment when "later" was no longer available. Most deadlines in life are arbitrary or externally imposed, which means the important, deadline-free work — career changes, difficult conversations, strategy reviews — loses by default. The remedy is to impose artificial deadlines on what matters, turning "someday" into a date.

## Partitions, Tranches, and Pattern Triggers
Tripwires can be built out of resources, not just dates. Researchers Dilip Soman and Amar Cheema studied day laborers in rural India who wanted to save. Workers whose wages were physically divided into multiple envelopes — some earmarked for savings — saved dramatically more than those handed one lump sum; **partitioning** the money nearly doubled the savings rate. Opening a sealed envelope is a small, conscious violation, and that moment of awareness is the tripwire. (The same logic explains why individually wrapped cookies get eaten more slowly than an open bag.)

Organizations can partition too. Venture capitalists never hand a startup its whole ten-year budget; they fund in **tranches**, each round buying progress to the next milestone and each new round forcing a fresh, evidence-based decision to continue or stop. The Heaths urge managers to do the same internally: instead of approving a full project budget, release it in stages — "here's enough to validate demand; come back with results." Partitions cap losses from a failing course while preserving the option to double down on a winning one.

That's the underrated second gift of tripwires: they make risk psychologically affordable. Committing to "we'll try this until we've spent $50,000 or six months, then reassess" lets you act boldly inside the boundary instead of half-committing out of vague anxiety.

Not every danger announces itself with a number, so some tripwires must be **pattern-based**. Hospitals learned that patients often deteriorate for hours before a code blue while junior staff hesitate to challenge hierarchy. Rapid-response systems define trigger patterns — an acute change in heart rate or blood pressure, and crucially, "a staff member is worried about the patient" — that automatically authorize anyone to summon a **rapid-response team**. At Lucile Packard Children's Hospital, the approach cut codes outside the ICU by roughly 71% and reduced hospital-wide mortality by about 18%. The pattern trigger does two jobs: it teaches people what danger looks like, and it grants permission to act the instant they see it.

## Fair Process Makes Decisions Stick
A decision isn't finished when it's made — it has to survive the people affected by it. Decades of **procedural justice** research, much of it by Tom Tyler on courts and police, show that people's acceptance of an outcome depends heavily on whether the *process* felt fair, often as much as on the outcome itself. Litigants who lose in court, employees passed over, citizens ticketed by police — all comply more willingly when they had a voice, felt genuinely heard, and got an honest explanation of the reasoning.

Practicing fair process is behavioral, not cosmetic: let people present their views before the decision, demonstrate that you considered them (ideally by accurately restating the opposing case), explain why you decided as you did, and be candid about the downsides you're accepting. The Heaths describe a leader nicknamed the "velvet hammer" for delivering unwelcome decisions people nonetheless embraced — because he could argue the losing side's position better than they could, proof he had truly weighed it.

Two traps undermine this. First, fake consultation — soliciting input after the decision is really made — is worse than none, because people detect it and resent it. Second, treating disagreement as combat: leaders who respond to pushback with advocacy harden opposition, while those who respond with genuine curiosity ("what would have to be true for your option to be best?") often convert critics into supporters. Fair process isn't slower decision-making; it's insurance on execution, because a technically optimal decision that the team quietly sabotages loses to a good decision the team owns.

The same logic applies inward. When you know your process was sound, you can stop relitigating the choice in your head. Trusting the process is what quiets the second-guessing that makes people slow, timid, and miserable deciders.

## The Assembled WRAP Toolkit
The book closes with Matt D'Arrigo, founder of the San Diego arts charity ARTS (A Reason To Survive), facing a classic growth decision: keep everything in his beloved center or push programs out to other sites. Instead of agonizing on instinct, he ran the process — widened his framing beyond whether-or-not, reality-tested the expansion idea against evidence and other people's experience, stepped back to ask what his core priorities demanded, and prepared for the ways it could go wrong. The decision got easier not because the future got clearer, but because the process carried the load.

That is the whole argument in miniature. Each WRAP step neutralizes a specific villain: **Widen your options** defeats narrow framing (vanishing-options test, multitracking, finding people who've solved your problem). **Reality-test your assumptions** defeats confirmation bias (disconfirming questions, considering the opposite, zooming out to base rates, ooching). **Attain distance** defeats short-term emotion (10/10/10, advising your best friend, honoring core priorities). **Prepare to be wrong** defeats overconfidence (premortems and preparades, safety factors, realistic previews — and now tripwires, the guardrail against the overconfident assumption that today's course will keep being right).

None of it requires genius, and none of it delivers certainty. What it delivers is a repeatable procedure that beats both gut instinct and raw analysis — recall Lovallo and Sibony's finding that process mattered six times more than analysis across 1,048 decisions. As the Heaths put it, "Being decisive is itself a choice." You cannot control outcomes, but you can control how you decide — and people who use a process decide faster, act bolder, sleep better after mistakes, and course-correct sooner, because they trust the machinery that produced the call.

## In Practice
- Pick one slow-moving risk in your work or life and set an explicit tripwire this week: a metric, a date, or a spending threshold that, if crossed, forces a scheduled reassessment.
- Before starting your next risky project, partition the budget into tranches with a defined decision point at each — "we release the next stage only if X evidence appears."
- Design one brown-M&Ms check: a small, easily inspected signal that reveals whether a partner, vendor, or process is being handled with care.
- Give any important-but-deadline-free goal an artificial deadline, and tell someone about it so the tripwire actually fires.
- Define pattern triggers for your team — specific warning signs, including "someone is worried" — that pre-authorize anyone to escalate without permission.
- For your next unpopular decision, practice fair process deliberately: hear objections first, restate the opposing case until its advocates agree you've got it, then explain your reasoning and the trade-offs you accepted.
