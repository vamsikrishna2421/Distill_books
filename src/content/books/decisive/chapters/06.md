# Attain Distance: 10/10/10 and Core Priorities

## Key Ideas
- Short-term emotion — fear, embarrassment, excitement, dread — distorts choices; the fix is not to suppress feeling but to gain distance, as Andy Grove did by asking what a replacement CEO would do about Intel's dying memory business.
- Suzy Welch's **10/10/10** stretches a decision across three time horizons — 10 minutes, 10 months, 10 years — so a momentary emotion stops outvoting your long-term interests.
- Three quiet biases pull us toward whatever already exists: **mere exposure** (Zajonc: familiarity breeds liking), **loss aversion** (losses hurt roughly twice as much as equivalent gains), and their combination, **status quo bias**.
- The single fastest distancing tool is one question: "What would I tell my best friend to do in this situation?" — advice to others is clearer because it ignores our short-term emotions and spotlights the most important factors.
- When a decision stays agonizing even from a distance, it usually signals a conflict among **core priorities**; naming the priority explicitly — as Interplast did by declaring the patient, not the volunteer surgeon, its customer — resolves whole families of downstream disputes.
- Honoring priorities requires actively attacking lesser obligations: keep a Jim Collins-style **stop-doing list** and schedule protected calendar time, because research shows only about 10% of managers spend their time purposefully.

## Step Outside Yourself: The New-CEO Question at Intel

In 1985, Intel was bleeding money in memory chips — the business it was founded on in 1968 and the core of its identity — as Japanese competitors undercut it relentlessly. Its microprocessor line was thriving, but leadership had circled for months, unable to abandon "memories," which felt like abandoning Intel itself.

President Andy Grove broke the deadlock with a question to CEO Gordon Moore: if the board fired them and brought in a new CEO, what would that person do? Moore answered immediately — get out of memories. Grove's reply: then why don't we walk out the door, come back in, and do it ourselves. Intel exited memory chips, bet fully on microprocessors, and became one of history's great growth stories.

Notice what the question did. Grove and Moore had all the facts for months; what blocked them was **short-term emotion** — attachment to history, dread of admitting defeat, internal politics. A hypothetical outsider carried none of that baggage, so the right answer became obvious in seconds.

This is the third villain of decision making and the third step of WRAP: **Attain Distance Before Deciding**. Visceral emotion fades — but by the time it does, the decision is often already made. The classic advice to "sleep on it" points in the right direction but is too weak: some feelings (like a crush on a convertible in the showroom) fade overnight, while others (like anxiety and familiarity) persist for years and quietly steer us wrong. We need tools that manufacture distance on demand rather than waiting for it.

## 10/10/10: Audit Your Feelings on Three Time Horizons

Business writer **Suzy Welch**'s **10/10/10** tool asks you to consider a decision on three timelines: How will I feel about this choice **10 minutes** from now? **10 months** from now? **10 years** from now?

The Heaths illustrate it with a woman, Annie, agonizing over whether to be the first to say "I love you" to her cautious boyfriend of nine months, Karl, during an upcoming vacation. Run through 10/10/10: in 10 minutes she'd feel nervous but proud she'd taken the risk. In 10 months, she almost certainly wouldn't regret having tried. In 10 years, whichever way he responded would scarcely matter — the relationship would have progressed with someone. The analysis makes the choice nearly a no-brainer: speak up. Yet her in-the-moment fear had been treating the 10-minute horizon as the only one that existed.

That's the tool's real function. It doesn't pretend decisions should be emotionless — 10 years from now you'll still have feelings, and they count. It simply levels the playing field, forcing the vivid, present emotion to share the stage with your calmer, wiser future emotions. Short-term feelings get an outsized vote by default; 10/10/10 restores proportion.

Use it for both directions of error: it deflates hot impulses (the purchase, the angry email, the flattering job offer) and, just as often, it emboldens you against short-term pain — the awkward conversation, the risky ask, the change you keep postponing — by showing how quickly the discomfort fades relative to the long-term payoff.

## Why the Familiar Feels Right: Mere Exposure, Loss Aversion, Status Quo

Distance matters because two deep biases conspire to make whatever currently exists feel better than it is.

First, the **mere exposure effect**. Psychologist **Robert Zajonc** showed across dozens of studies that simply encountering something repeatedly — nonsense words, Chinese-style characters, photographs of faces — makes people like it more. Familiarity itself, with no other merit, breeds preference. This is why incumbent logos, routines, and org charts feel "right": we've merely seen them a lot.

Second, **loss aversion**. Prospect-theory research shows losses loom roughly two to four times larger than equivalent gains. Offered a coin flip where tails costs $100, most people demand about $200 on heads before they'll play — an irrationally steep premium. And ownership kicks in almost instantly: participants handed a mug moments earlier demand far more to sell it than non-owners will pay for the identical mug.

Stack the two together and you get **status quo bias**: the familiar feels comfortable (mere exposure) and any change foregrounds what we'd lose rather than what we'd gain (loss aversion). This is the gravitational force behind organizational inertia — why companies like Intel cling to dying businesses and why individuals stay in stale jobs and relationships. Crucially, both biases operate as short-term emotions: the pain of loss and the comfort of familiarity are felt *now*, while the benefits of change arrive later and abstractly.

Distance is the antidote because these feelings are largely invisible from the outside. The new-CEO question worked precisely by deleting Grove and Moore's sense of ownership and familiarity from the equation.

## The Best-Friend Question: Borrow an Outside Perspective in One Sentence

Perhaps the most practical distancing tool in the book is a single question: **"What would I tell my best friend to do in this situation?"**

People who are hopelessly tangled in their own decision can usually answer this instantly — and the answer is usually right. When we advise friends, two things change. We naturally weigh the *single most important factor* rather than drowning in dozens of considerations, and we're immune to the friend's short-term emotions; their fear of embarrassment or loss doesn't register in our analysis. Advice-giving strips a decision down to its essentials.

The same logic works in high-stakes domains. Physicians who feel uncertain recommending a treatment often clarify their own judgment by asking what they'd advise if the patient were their brother or mother — a reframe that reliably surfaces their true read of the evidence.

So when you're stuck, deliberately switch roles: describe your situation as if it belonged to someone you care about, and hear what you'd say. Better yet, notice that other people can do this for you effortlessly — which is why friends often see our right answer years before we do. The perspective you'd give away for free is usually the best one you own.

## Agonizing Decisions Are Priority Conflicts: Interplast's Customer Question

Sometimes distance isn't enough, because the difficulty is genuine: two things you care about are in conflict. The Heaths' answer is to identify and enshrine your **core priorities** — the long-term emotional values, goals, and aspirations you most want to serve — and let them arbitrate.

**Interplast**, a nonprofit sending volunteer surgeons to perform free reconstructive surgery (cleft lips and palates, burn scars) in developing countries, was torn by recurring board fights. Could volunteer surgeons bring spouses and kids on trips? Could residents come along to operate, when a more experienced surgeon would be safer? Should the organization keep flying in American doctors at all, or shift to training host-country surgeons? Each dispute pitted the interests of the volunteers — the people donating their labor and funding the mission — against the interests of patients.

The leadership finally cut through by answering one question: *Who is our customer — the volunteer or the patient?* They chose the patient. That single declaration resolved the entire cluster of arguments at once: residents' training mattered less than patient safety; empowering local surgeons served patients best even if it made American volunteers less central. Some offended volunteers left. The organization got dramatically easier to run, because a thousand future fights had been settled in advance.

That's the general lesson: when a decision keeps you agonizing for days, it's usually because it activates a clash between core priorities. Naming the priority converts an emotional tug-of-war into a solvable question — and every priority you make explicit "unweights" countless later decisions.

## Priorities Are Worthless Unless You Attack What Displaces Them

Declaring priorities is cheap; the scarce resource is time, and lesser obligations will devour it by default. Researchers **Heike Bruch and Sumantra Ghoshal**, studying managers' time use, concluded that about 90% of managers squandered their time on ineffective busyness — only around 10% spent it in a committed, purposeful, reflective way. Core priorities lose not to villains but to inboxes.

The Heaths offer two countermeasures. First, **Jim Collins's "stop-doing list."** Collins built it from a thought experiment posed by a mentor: imagine you inherit $20 million free and clear — but you have only ten years left to live. What would you stop doing right now? Whatever answers that question belongs on a standing list, maintained as seriously as your to-do list, because making room for a core priority mathematically requires evicting something else.

Second, *defend calendar time*. Collins himself tracks his hours against explicit targets (roughly half his time on creative work, the rest split between teaching and everything else) — treating time allocation as a measurable budget rather than a hope. The same move works anywhere: schedule recurring, protected blocks for the priority — the product work, the family dinner, the strategic thinking — and treat incursions as what they are: small decisions to betray a big commitment.

The two chapters combine into one discipline. Distance tools (the new-CEO question, 10/10/10, the best-friend question) neutralize short-term emotion so you can see what you truly want; core priorities plus a stop-doing list ensure that what you truly want actually governs your choices — and your calendar.

## In Practice
- For your current stuck decision, ask: "If my successor took over tomorrow, what would they do?" — then consider walking out the door and coming back in to do it yourself.
- Run 10/10/10 on any emotionally charged choice this week: write down how you'll feel in 10 minutes, 10 months, and 10 years, and let the three answers vote.
- When you catch yourself defending the status quo, name the bias out loud — is this genuinely better, or just familiar (mere exposure) and change-averse (loss aversion)?
- Before deciding anything personal, ask "What would I tell my best friend to do?" and take your own advice seriously.
- If a decision has kept you agonizing for days, write down the two core priorities in conflict, decide which one wins in general — then let that ruling settle this case and future ones.
- Start a stop-doing list: identify one recurring obligation that serves no core priority, drop it, and give its time a protected weekly calendar block for something that does.
