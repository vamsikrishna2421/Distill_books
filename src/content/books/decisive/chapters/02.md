# Widen Your Options: Escape 'Whether or Not'

## Key Ideas
- Framing a decision as "whether or not to do X" is the single most common decision trap: it silently collapses a rich option space into a referendum on one alternative.
- Baruch Fischhoff's study of teenagers found roughly 65% of their decisions involved no real alternatives at all — and adults and executives make the same mistake in dressed-up form.
- Paul Nutt's research on organizational decisions shows "whether or not" choices fail 52% of the time over the long term, while decisions comparing two or more genuine alternatives fail only 32% of the time.
- The **vanishing options test** — imagine a genie has erased all your current options — reliably forces you to discover alternatives that were hiding in plain sight.
- **Opportunity-cost thinking** widens your frame automatically: Shane Frederick showed that merely reminding shoppers they could "keep the money for other purchases" cut purchase rates from 75% to 55%.
- You often don't have to choose at all: asking "can I do this AND that?" frequently dissolves a false either/or.

## "Whether or Not" Is a Warning Sign

Listen to how people describe their hardest decisions: "Should I break up with my boyfriend or not?" "Should we fire this employee or not?" "Should I buy the new car or not?" Each question sounds like a decision, but it isn't one yet. It's a **narrow frame** — a single option put to an up-or-down vote, with every other possibility left offstage.

The Heaths call this the "whether or not" trap, and it is the first villain of decision making. Chapter 1 introduced Kahneman's spotlight metaphor: our attention illuminates whatever is directly in front of us and treats it as all there is. A whether-or-not question keeps the spotlight frozen on one alternative. The fix is not a brighter spotlight — more analysis of the same option — but a moving one that sweeps the rest of the stage.

How common is the trap? Decision researcher **Baruch Fischhoff** and colleagues interviewed adolescents about their recent decisions and coded how each was framed. Roughly **65%** of the teens' decisions involved no real alternatives — they were either whether-or-not questions ("Should I smoke or not?") or mere statements of resolve ("I'm going to stop blaming others") with no options considered at all. Genuine comparisons between two or more alternatives were a small minority.

It's tempting to smile at teenagers agonizing over a single option, but the evidence says grown-ups — including well-paid executives — behave the same way. As covered in Chapter 1, Paul Nutt found that only 29% of major organizational decisions considered more than one alternative. Dan Lovallo has quipped that most businesses make decisions like hormone-addled teens.

The deeper problem is that a whether-or-not frame doesn't just limit your choices; it distorts your analysis. Once the question is "this option: yes or no?", your energy flows into building a case for or against it (confirmation bias, Chapter 4's territory) rather than into asking the prior question: *what else could I do?* The first move of the WRAP process — **Widen Your Options** — exists to reopen that question before you invest in evaluating anything.

## The Evidence: Narrow Frames Fail More Than Half the Time

Does adding alternatives actually improve outcomes, or does it just slow you down? **Paul Nutt**, who spent decades assembling a database of real organizational decisions — business launches, acquisitions, hiring, new products — tracked how those decisions fared over time, judged by whether the choice was still in place and regarded as successful years later.

The split was stark. Decisions framed as **whether or not** — a single alternative given thumbs up or thumbs down — **failed 52% of the time** in the long run. Decisions in which the team weighed **two or more genuine alternatives failed only 32% of the time**. One additional real option cut the long-term failure rate by more than a third.

Why does one extra alternative help so much? Three mechanisms. First, comparing options forces you to understand the *landscape* of what's possible — you learn what you're trading away, which sharpens your judgment of every option including the original. Second, having alternatives gives you a built-in fallback: if Plan A stumbles, you're not starting from zero, because Plan B was already scouted. Third, multiple options loosen your ego's grip. When there's only one proposal on the table, its sponsor defends it like a personal cause; when there are several, people can abandon a weakening option without losing face.

Note what the finding does *not* say. It doesn't say you need ten options — the paralysis of too many choices is a real but overrated worry (Chapter 3 covers the jam-study caveat and how to **multitrack** two or three options efficiently). The bar here is embarrassingly low: get from one option to at least two real ones. Most decisions, personal and corporate, fail to clear even that.

A practical diagnostic follows directly: whenever you hear yourself or a colleague say "whether or not," treat it like a smoke alarm. The decision isn't ready to be made. The right response is not to deliberate harder but to generate — to ask what other ways exist to achieve the underlying goal before anyone votes on the option that happened to show up first.

## The Vanishing Options Test

Knowing you should generate alternatives is easy; actually generating them is not, because the current option monopolizes your attention. The Heaths' tool for breaking the monopoly is the **vanishing options test**: imagine a genie appears and declares that the options you're currently weighing have been removed from the universe. You cannot choose any of them. *What else could you do?*

The test sounds like a parlor trick, but it works because it converts "evaluate this option" into "solve this problem," and people are far better problem-solvers than they realize — they just never get asked. The Heaths describe advising a manager stuck on "Should I fire Anna, our receptionist, or not?" Anna was passable but flawed: good with customers, sloppy with details, prickly with colleagues. Asked to imagine that both firing and keeping-things-as-is had vanished, the manager quickly produced options no one had voiced: give Anna direct feedback and a chance to improve, redesign the role so it leaned on her strengths, shift the detail work to someone else, or hire a part-timer to cover her gaps. Any of those might beat both original "options" — and none surfaced until the originals were forcibly removed.

The same move rescues personal decisions. The college student asking "Do I break up with my boyfriend or not?" is choosing between two blurry bundles. Vanish both and better questions appear: How could this relationship be improved? What specifically is missing, and is it fixable? What would a trial separation reveal? The whether-or-not frame hid an entire menu of intermediate moves.

Two implementation notes. First, the test works best when the constraint is taken seriously — really pretend the options are gone, rather than treating it as a formality before returning to the original debate. Second, apply it *before* you're desperate. Scarcity of options is usually an illusion created by attention, not reality; the genie's only magic is forcing your spotlight to move. If your new alternatives turn out worse than the original, you've lost ten minutes and gained confidence. Usually, they don't.

## Opportunity Cost and the Power of AND

Economists have a second frame-widening tool: **opportunity cost** — what you give up by making a choice, because the same money, time, or attention could have gone elsewhere. Dwight Eisenhower used it vividly in a 1953 speech: one bomber, he noted, costs the equivalent of modern schools in more than thirty cities, or two fully equipped hospitals. Whether or not to build the bomber is the narrow frame; bomber-versus-schools is the real decision.

The remarkable finding is how little effort opportunity-cost thinking requires. **Shane Frederick** — inspired by his own stereo shopping, when a bystander reframed his choice between a $1,000 Pioneer and a $700 Sony as "the Pioneer, or the Sony plus $300 of albums?" — ran a simple experiment. Participants were asked whether they'd buy a $14.99 video of a favorite actor. One group chose between "buy" and "don't buy." The other group's second option added a trivially obvious reminder: "don't buy — **keep the money for other purchases**." That phrase, which tells shoppers nothing they don't already know, cut purchases from **75% to 55%**. Merely mentioning the default alternative made a fifth of buyers reconsider. Our frames are so narrow that even a nudge toward the obvious widens them.

The habit to build: when weighing any expenditure — money, headcount, calendar hours — ask "what's the *next-best use* of these resources?" and make that use an explicit competing option. A $20,000 project isn't competing against nothing; it's competing against everything else $20,000 could do.

Finally, interrogate the "or" itself. Many either/or dilemmas are false. **Steve Cole**, research VP at the nonprofit HopeLab, needed a portable device to measure kids' activity and, rather than picking one vendor, split the first phase of work among several — small bets that let him compare real performance instead of proposals. His rule of thumb: "Is there a way I can do this AND that?" Surprisingly often there is, especially when you shrink the initial commitment. That instinct — running options in parallel — is where the next chapter picks up.

## In Practice

- Treat the phrase "whether or not" as an alarm. Before deciding, force the question "What else could we do?" and don't proceed until you have at least two genuine alternatives.
- Run the vanishing options test on a current dilemma: assume every option on your list is forbidden, set a timer for ten minutes, and write down what you'd do instead.
- Attach an opportunity cost to every significant purchase or project this week: name the specific next-best use of the same money or time, and compare against *that*, not against nothing.
- When a subordinate or colleague brings you a yes/no proposal, send it back once with the request: "Bring me this plus one real alternative."
- Convert one either/or dilemma into an AND: ask whether a smaller version of both options could run simultaneously before you commit fully to either.
- For personal decisions, ladder up to the underlying goal ("a better relationship," "career growth") and brainstorm three routes to the goal that don't pass through the option currently hogging your spotlight.
