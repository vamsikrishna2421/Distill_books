# Zoom Out, Zoom In — Then Ooch

## Key Ideas
- Your internal predictions about how a decision will turn out are almost always too optimistic; the **outside view** — the track record of everyone who has faced a similar situation — is a far more reliable guide.
- Experts are poor at predicting your specific outcome but excellent at supplying **base rates**, so ask them "what usually happens in cases like mine?" rather than "what will happen to me?"
- Averages hide texture. Complement the zoomed-out statistics with a **close-up** — direct, firsthand observation of the reality on the ground — before committing.
- When you can test an option instead of predicting its outcome, do it: **ooching** means running a small, cheap experiment before making a big, irreversible bet.
- Work samples and test-drives beat interviews and speculation; decades of research show interviews are among the weakest predictors of job performance.
- Ooching has one hard limit: for decisions that only pay off with full commitment, running endless small trials is just procrastination in disguise.

## The Outside View Beats Your Inside Story

When we assess an option, we instinctively build a story from the information in front of us — our plans, our talents, our special circumstances. The Heaths call this the **inside view**. Its rival, the **outside view**, ignores your particulars and asks a colder question: how do situations like this generally turn out?

Daniel Kahneman learned the difference the hard way. He led a team designing a decision-making curriculum for Israeli high schools, and after a productive first year he polled members on how long the full project would take. Estimates clustered around two years. Then he asked Seymour, the team's curriculum expert, how comparable teams had fared. Seymour's answer was chilling: roughly 40% of such teams never finished at all, and none finished in under seven years — and this team was, if anything, below average in resources. The group heard the numbers, shrugged, and kept working. The curriculum took eight years to complete, and by then nobody wanted it.

The lesson is not that Seymour was clairvoyant. It's that base rates — the aggregated experience of everyone who has tried what you're trying — encode information your inside view cannot see. Every restaurant founder knows the majority of new restaurants fail; almost every one believes theirs is the exception. The inside view feels richer and more relevant precisely because it's built from vivid, specific detail, but that vividness is what makes it seductive and unreliable.

The practical move is humility by arithmetic. Before deciding, ask: of people who faced this same choice, what fraction succeeded, how long did it take them, and what did it cost? Treat your own estimate as needing a heavy correction toward that average. You may still proceed — sometimes the odds are worth it — but you'll proceed with your eyes open rather than inside a flattering story.

## Ask Experts for Base Rates, Not Predictions

Where do you find base rates? Often from an expert — but you must ask the right question. Experts are notoriously bad at predicting specific outcomes ("Will I win my lawsuit?"), because that question invites the same inside-view storytelling that fools everyone else. They are remarkably good at reporting the distribution of outcomes they've seen ("What percentage of cases like mine settle before trial? What's the typical range of settlements?"). Interrogate an expert about the past and the population, not about your future.

Brian Zikmund-Fisher put this into practice with his life on the line. A doctoral student in behavioral decision theory, he was diagnosed in his late twenties with a rare blood disorder that would likely kill him within five or six years. A bone-marrow transplant offered a chance at cure — but roughly a one-in-four chance of dying from the procedure itself. Instead of agonizing over predictions, he zoomed out: he dug into survival statistics for patients like him at different treatment centers, learning that hospitals performing many transplants had meaningfully better outcomes.

Then he did something equally important: he zoomed in. He talked at length with nurses — not just doctors — because nurses see the unvarnished day-to-day reality of treatment and recovery, and he interviewed patients who had lived through the procedure. The close-ups gave him texture the statistics couldn't: what recovery actually felt like, what complications looked like, how families coped. He chose the transplant at a high-volume center, survived, and years later was celebrating "re-birthdays" with the daughter born after his recovery.

His method is the chapter's template: base rates set realistic expectations; close-ups reveal the ground truth behind them. Neither alone is sufficient. The outside view can miss what makes your situation genuinely different; the close-up alone can seduce you back into anecdote. Together they triangulate reality.

## Zoom In: Close-Ups and Genchi Genbutsu

Zooming in means going to see reality firsthand instead of trusting summaries, dashboards, and secondhand reports. Toyota institutionalized this as **genchi genbutsu** — roughly, "go and see for yourself." Before redesigning the Sienna minivan, Toyota engineer Yuji Yokoya drove one more than 50,000 miles across all fifty U.S. states, Canada, and Mexico. He discovered things no spreadsheet would surface: crosswinds on prairie highways demanded better handling, and North American families practically live in their vans, so kids' comfort and cargo flexibility mattered enormously. The redesigned 2004 Sienna's sales jumped about 60%.

Franklin Roosevelt ran the same play on a nation. Distrustful of the filtered reports that reach any leader, FDR actively solicited letters from ordinary citizens — the White House received thousands daily — and had staff prepare systematic "mail briefs" summarizing a representative sample, occasionally spot-checking batches himself. He engineered a permanent close-up on public sentiment that no aide's summary could distort.

The close-up matters because averages and reports strip out the texture where problems and opportunities hide. A college brochure gives you the zoomed-out view of a school; sitting in on an actual class gives you the close-up. Online reviews are, in effect, crowdsourced base rates — a restaurant's star rating is the outside view of hundreds of strangers' experiences, and it will predict your evening better than the menu in the window.

A caution comes with the technique: a close-up is still a sample of one, so it can't override robust base rates — it enriches them. The disciplined sequence is zoom out first to learn what usually happens, then zoom in to see whether the usual story fits the specific reality in front of you. When both lenses agree, you can move with confidence.

## Ooch: Test Before You Leap

Sometimes you can skip prediction entirely. **Ooching** means dipping a toe — running a small, cheap, fast experiment that lets reality answer the question you were about to answer with speculation. Entrepreneurs know the idea as prototyping or little bets; the principle is the same: why forecast when you can test?

Intuit's Fasal service shows the payoff. An Intuit team in India suspected poor farmers were being underpaid because they couldn't compare prices across markets. Rather than build a product on that hypothesis, they ooched: they identified their riskiest "leap of faith" assumptions and tested them with a bare-bones trial, manually texting price information to about a hundred farmers while faking the technology behind the scenes. Farmers acted on the data and earned more, the assumptions held, and Fasal grew into a service reaching hundreds of thousands of Indian farmers. Founder Scott Cook champions this culture of "leadership by experiment," arguing that decisions otherwise get made by "politics, persuasion, and PowerPoint."

Ooching also defuses high-stakes vendor and design gambles. When Steve Cole of the health nonprofit HopeLab needed a partner to build a portable activity-measuring device, he refused to award one big contract based on proposals. He hired several small firms to complete just phase one of the work, planning to continue with whoever performed best — converting a prediction problem ("who will do the best job?") into an observation problem ("who did the best job?").

The same logic transforms career choices. Before spending three years and six figures on law school, spend a week shadowing a lawyer or take a paralegal job; before committing to nursing or teaching, volunteer in a hospital or classroom. A test-drive generates real data about daily reality — which is usually nothing like the fantasy version of the job — at a tiny fraction of the cost of a wrong commitment. Medicine builds this in through residencies; most fields leave it to you.

## Work Samples Beat Interviews — but Don't Ooch Commitment

Hiring is where ooching most clearly outperforms prediction. Decades of research show that job interviews are among the weakest predictors of actual performance — worse than work samples, job-knowledge tests, and peer ratings of past work. Yet interviewers walk away supremely confident in their judgments, a bias psychologist Richard Nisbett dubbed the **interview illusion**.

The starkest evidence comes from the University of Texas medical school. In 1979 the school admitted 150 students ranked highly by its interview process; then the legislature abruptly expanded the class by 50 seats, forcing it to accept applicants from near the bottom of its interview rankings — students virtually every school had passed over. Researchers led by Robyn DeVaul tracked both groups and found no difference in attrition, academic performance, or clinical performance through residency. The interviews had measured essentially nothing. The implication for any hirer: assign a small paid work sample — a real task from the actual job — and weight it far above interview charm.

But ooching has a boundary the Heaths insist on: it is the wrong tool for decisions that require commitment to succeed. You cannot ooch your way into having a child, and half-hearted trial runs at inherently all-in ventures — a startup that needs your full energy, a move, a marriage — generate misleading data, because the tentative version isn't the real thing. Worse, ooching can become a socially acceptable form of stalling. If you already know what the right choice is — the analysis is done, the values are clear, only the leap remains — another "small experiment" isn't prudence; it's procrastination wearing a lab coat.

Use ooching when you genuinely lack information that a cheap test can supply. Use commitment when the information is in and only courage is missing.

## In Practice
- Facing a big decision this week, write down the base rate before your own estimate: find out what fraction of people who tried this succeeded, and how long it actually took them.
- When consulting an expert (lawyer, doctor, contractor, advisor), ask about the distribution of past cases like yours — not for a prediction about your specific outcome.
- Schedule one genchi genbutsu visit: go see the school, sit in the meeting, ride along with the sales rep, or work the support queue yourself instead of reading the report about it.
- Before your next irreversible commitment (hire, product, career change), design the smallest real test that would let evidence replace prediction — a paid work sample, a pilot with a handful of customers, a week of shadowing.
- Replace one interview round in your hiring process with a short, paid, job-realistic work sample, and weight it above everyone's gut impressions.
- Audit your current "experiments": if you already know the right answer and are running trials to avoid the leap, stop ooching and commit.
